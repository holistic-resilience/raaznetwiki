# IP address

A device on the internet needs its own address to receive [data ![](https://ssd.eff.org/assets/efforg/info-7ae1fb29e1e734b20023f3e3470316c82b84a7e4b9f1d1e2f49e399f1f8de2d3.png)](https://ssd.eff.org/ps/glossary/data), just like a home or business needs a street address to receive physical mail. This address is its IP (Internet [Protocol](https://ssd.eff.org/glossary/protocol)) address. When you connect to a website or other server online, you usually reveal your own [IP address](https://ssd.eff.org/glossary/ip-address). This doesn't necessarily reveal either your identity (it's hard to map an [IP address ![](https://ssd.eff.org/assets/efforg/info-7ae1fb29e1e734b20023f3e3470316c82b84a7e4b9f1d1e2f49e399f1f8de2d3.png)](https://ssd.eff.org/ps/glossary/ip-address) to a real address or a particular computer), but it can be combined with other pieces of information to identify you. An IP address can give away some information about you, however, such as your rough location or the name of your Internet Service Provider (ISP). Services like Tor let you hide your IP address, which helps give you anonymity online.

# Exercise: Talking About Security in Groups and Teams

## Purpose & Output

This exercise helps groups reflect on how and when they discuss security with peers, colleagues, or team members. While it works best when facilitated by at least two people, it can also serve as a useful individual reflection on your interactions with colleagues.

**Goal:** Start a constructive process for discussing security within your team or group.

## Materials Needed

- Writing materials (cards, sticky notes, markers)
- Large wall space, flip-chart, or pin-board for documentation

## Format

Individual work combined with group discussion

## Exercise Steps

### Step 1: Pair Discussion

Divide the group into pairs. Each pair should consider and write down answers to these questions about group dynamics:

1. What topics take up the majority of time in group conversations?
2. What topics do we never seem to find time for?
3. What aspects of our group interaction do we find energizing?
4. What aspects of our group interaction do we find exhausting?
5. What happens in the group when people disagree?
6. Do you have enough space to talk about security issues with peers or close colleagues? How might this space be created or expanded?

### Step 2: Collate Responses

Gather all responses on a board or in a notebook for the whole group to see.

### Step 3: Team Discussion

As a full team, discuss:

1. Where and how do we want to set our priorities concerning security?
2. What common problems arise when talking about security as a group?
3. What prevents us from talking about security? How can we address this?
4. How can we create and maintain adequate space for security discussions? What time and resources will this require?
5. How might we increase the effectiveness of our group interaction on security?
6. What problems arise around committing to changing our security practices? Do we resist change individually or collectively, and why?

### Step 4: Individual Reflection

Each person reflects individually on:

1. Should you have similar security awareness for your family and loved ones?
2. What are the differences in how family and loved ones are affected?
3. How do you communicate the threats you face to family, community, friends, and others outside your work circle?

### Step 5: Group Sharing and Guidelines

Share points that people feel comfortable discussing with the whole group. Then agree on:

- What information can be communicated to those outside the group
- Guidelines for confidentiality, intimacy, and security
- Commitment from the whole group to follow these guidelines

## Discussion Points

Consider these questions as you work through the exercise:

- What happens if you don't make progress on talking about security?
- What happens if someone doesn't follow the guidelines about external communication?

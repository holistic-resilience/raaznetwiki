# Protect the Privacy of Your Online Communications

Each communication method—digital or otherwise—comes with advantages and disadvantages in terms of convenience, popularity, cost, and security. It's up to each of us to weigh the benefits and risks of the methods we use to communicate. When our risks are higher, it is wise to choose our communication tools more deliberately.

This guide illustrates the steps needed to create and implement a communication plan as well as specific best practices aimed at protecting your privacy and sensitive information when communicating with others online.

---

## Map Your Communications and Make a Plan to Protect Them

### Assess the Sensitivity Level of Your Communications

Start by understanding your current communication landscape:

- **Map your communications**: Who is communicating with whom? What channels are you using? What kind of information are you sharing? Are you using different channels for different types of communications?
- **Evaluate potential consequences**: Ask yourself what would happen if information you need to share was compromised, lost, or exposed.
- **Rethink based on sensitivity**: Reorganize your communications according to their sensitivity level.
- **Plan protection measures**: Decide how you will protect the most sensitive communications.

> **Why this matters:** Sensitive information can disrupt work continuity, endanger individuals through identification, enable impersonation or reputational harm, or violate laws in specific jurisdictions. Careful protection mitigates legal, operational, and personal risks.

### Reflect on Who Needs Access

Decide whether the information you are sharing should be restricted to specific recipients or can be shared more widely.

### Analyze Your Needs and Capacities

Ask yourself these questions to determine the appropriate communication channel:

- Should participants' identities be kept secret from administrators and others with server access?
- Do you need real-time communication or can you wait for replies?
- What formats do you need to send—text only, or other content types?
- Do you prefer writing, calls, or video conferences for your current needs?
- Do you need access on both computers and phones? What operating systems are involved?
- What tools do your contacts already use? Can they learn new tools?
- What's your budget? Can you self-host your communication platform?

### Basic Security Measures

When selecting communication platforms:

1. **Enable two-factor authentication** when available
2. **Ensure HTTPS/TLS is enabled** on whatever tool you use
3. **Verify end-to-end encryption** claims—check if the encryption technology is free and open-source

> **Understanding encryption types:** Most online services use client-to-server encryption (HTTPS/TLS), which protects messages in transit but leaves them unencrypted on servers. End-to-end encryption ensures only you and your recipient can read messages. For sensitive information, strongly prefer end-to-end encrypted services. If unavailable (common with large group video calls), use services hosted by entities you trust or self-host if you have the technical expertise.

### Define a Communication Plan

Based on your assessment, establish and rehearse a communication plan:

- **Define a Traffic Light Protocol (TLP)**: Decide what sensitive information can be shared with whom, in what forms, and over which channels. See [CIRCL's Traffic Light Protocol](https://www.circl.lu/pub/traffic-light-protocol/) for guidance.
- **Coordinate with contacts**: Propose, discuss, and agree upon specific communication methods—including what channels you will and won't use.
- **Consider informal steps**: Quick changes you can implement immediately.
- **Consider formal steps**: Documented policies and procedures.
- **Rehearse your plan**: Practice before a crisis occurs.

> **Why rehearse?** In a crisis, we don't think as clearly and may act hastily. A pre-defined communication plan helps prevent emergencies and contributes to safety in critical situations.

## Protect and Anonymize Your Connections

- [Learn how to browse more securely](https://securityinabox.org/en/internet-connection/safer-browsing) to protect your online activities from surveillance.
- [Learn how to anonymize your connections](https://securityinabox.org/en/internet-connection/anonymity) to achieve goals without leaving traces, especially in high-risk situations.

> **Note:** Using Tor Browser or other anonymity tools allows you to visit websites without disclosing who you are or where you're from. However, if you log in with your usual account while doing so, you will still share your account information with that platform.

## Use Email More Safely

→ [Learn how to secure your email communications](https://securityinabox.org/en/communication/secure-email/)

> **Why focus on email?** Although many people prefer encrypted chat apps or video calls for everyday communications, email remains essential for creating accounts and organizing work. By default, email is not the most secure method—it's unencrypted, carries extensive metadata, persists on servers, and exposes you to phishing and malware. However, it's a resilient technology that ensures communication continuity even when servers malfunction, making it worth learning to use safely.

## Use Messaging Apps More Safely

→ [Learn how to use chat apps more securely](https://securityinabox.org/en/communication/secure-chat/)

> **Why this matters:** Chat services may access and collect information on your location, activity, content, and contacts. Choosing the right app is critical to protecting your safety.

## Remove Identifying Information from Files

→ [Learn how to remove identifying information from photos, videos, PDFs, and other files](https://securityinabox.org/en/files/destroy-identifying-information/)

> **Why this matters:** Simply blurring faces or covering sensitive details may not adequately protect people or places in your images—some editing methods allow recovery of hidden content. Additionally, all files contain invisible **metadata** that can reveal when and where they were created, by whom, and with what device. You can view some metadata by right-clicking a file and selecting "Properties" or "Get Info." Learn to visualize and remove sensitive metadata before sharing files.

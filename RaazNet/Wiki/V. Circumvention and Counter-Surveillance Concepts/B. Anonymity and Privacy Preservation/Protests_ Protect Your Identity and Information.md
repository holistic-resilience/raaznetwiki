# Protests: Protect Your Identity and Information

Many human rights defenders and activists depend heavily on computers and mobile phones for organizing and coordinating peaceful protests. This makes confiscation, theft, and surveillance of devices and communications a priority for adversaries attempting to limit free speech and assembly.

While transparency and open engagement can be useful security tools in many contexts, protecting the identity and sensitive information of those involved in a protest may be essential.

## Protecting Your Online Identity

When planning or coordinating a protest over the internet or using mobile phones, consider ways of protecting your identity and that of your network. Authorities and others may put social movements under surveillance to predict, prevent, or disrupt protests. Governments and private companies use tactics like observation, infiltration, and automated search engines to identify usernames using specific words or images.

**Key precautions for online organizing:**

- **Avoid relying on social media** for protest organization, as it is often heavily surveilled
- **Don't use your real name** on social networks when organizing or participating in actions
- **Avoid associating yourself** with groups likely to be under surveillance on social media
- **Be cautious with new connections** — avoid accepting friend requests from protesters you don't already know
- **Use privacy tools** like a VPN or [Tor Browser](https://torproject.org/) to hide your IP address and browsing activities

## Protecting Your Device

To address the threat of confiscation, loss, or theft, modern smartphones offer several data protection options.

### Android Devices

Devices running Android 4.0 or later typically allow encryption of stored data. You can find this option in your phone's security settings.

**Important considerations:**
- Encrypting your phone prevents data access when the device is powered off, unless someone can break your passphrase
- Encrypt your device at least a day before the protest to allow time for backup and the encryption process (which may take about an hour)
- Note that Micro-SD cards may not be encrypted by this option — only built-in storage is protected

### iPhone and iPad

To enable protection on iOS devices:

1. Enter privacy settings to enable a passphrase for the lock screen
2. Verify that "Data protection is enabled" appears once the passphrase is set
3. Consider enabling the option to erase data after 10 incorrect passphrase attempts — this can also serve as a quick way to delete sensitive data in emergencies

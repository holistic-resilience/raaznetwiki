# Account Deletion: Reclaiming Your Digital Privacy

Over time, it's easy to accumulate online accounts that you no longer use. Deleting these dormant accounts is an important step in reclaiming your privacy, as unused accounts are vulnerable to data breaches—incidents where a service's security is compromised and protected information is accessed by unauthorized actors.

Data breaches are unfortunately [all too common](https://haveibeenpwned.com/PwnedWebsites), making good digital hygiene essential for minimizing their impact on your life. This guide walks you through the process of finding and deleting old accounts, which services often make difficult through [deceptive design](https://deceptive.design/).

## Finding Old Accounts

### Check Your Password Manager

If you use a password manager, this step is straightforward. Most password managers include built-in functionality for detecting if your credentials were exposed in a data breach, such as Bitwarden's [Data Breach Report](https://bitwarden.com/blog/have-you-been-pwned).

Even if you haven't explicitly used a dedicated password manager, you may have used one built into your browser or device:

- [Firefox Password Manager](https://support.mozilla.org/kb/password-manager-remember-delete-edit-logins)
- [Google Password Manager](https://passwords.google.com/intro)
- [Edge Password Manager](https://support.microsoft.com/microsoft-edge/save-or-forget-passwords-in-microsoft-edge-b4beecb0-f2a8-1ca0-f26f-9ec247a3f336)
- Windows [Credential Manager](https://support.microsoft.com/windows/accessing-credential-manager-1b5c916a-6a16-889f-8581-fc16e8165ac0)
- macOS [Passwords](https://support.apple.com/HT211145)
- iOS [Passwords](https://support.apple.com/HT211146)
- Linux: Gnome Keyring (via [Seahorse](https://wiki.gnome.org/Apps/Seahorse)) or [KDE Wallet Manager](https://userbase.kde.org/KDE_Wallet_Manager)

### Search Your Email

If you think you have accounts not captured in your password manager, search your email for keywords like "verify," "welcome," or "confirm." Most services send verification or welcome emails when you create an account, making this an effective way to discover forgotten accounts.

## Deleting Old Accounts

### Step 1: Log In

To delete an account, you first need to access it. If the account is in your password manager, this is easy. Otherwise:

1. **Try to guess your password** based on patterns you may have used
2. **Use the "forgot password" option** typically found on login pages
3. **Contact customer support** if other methods fail

**Note:** If the site says no account exists for your email, or you never receive a reset link, you likely don't have an account with that email address. Some services also automatically delete inactive accounts over time.

### Step 2: Exercise GDPR Rights (EEA Residents)

If you're a resident of the European Economic Area, you have additional data erasure rights under [Article 17 of the GDPR](https://gdpr-info.eu/art-17-gdpr). Important considerations:

- Review the service's privacy policy for erasure procedures
- Some "Delete Account" options only disable accounts—full deletion may require additional steps
- You may need to fill out surveys, email a data protection officer, or prove EEA residency
- **Do not overwrite account information** if using GDPR rights, as your identity may need verification
- GDPR applies to any service serving European users, regardless of where the service is located
- If a service doesn't respect your erasure rights, contact your national [Data Protection Authority](https://ec.europa.eu/info/law/law-topic/data-protection/reform/rights-citizens/redress/what-should-i-do-if-i-think-my-personal-data-protection-rights-havent-been-respected_en)

### Step 3: Overwrite Account Information

Before abandoning an account, consider replacing your information with fake data. Many sites retain previous information even after deletion, so overwriting may help protect your data. However, there's no guarantee that backups won't contain your original information.

For the account email, create an alias using an [email aliasing service](https://www.privacyguides.org/en/email-aliasing/), then delete the alias after you're done. Avoid temporary email providers, as these can often be reactivated.

### Step 4: Delete the Account

Check [JustDeleteMe](https://justdeleteme.xyz/) for service-specific deletion instructions. The process varies widely:

- Some sites offer a simple "Delete Account" option
- Others require contacting support
- Some make deletion impossible

**For services that don't allow deletion:**

1. Falsify your account information
2. Enable [multi-factor authentication](https://www.privacyguides.org/en/basics/multi-factor-authentication/)
3. Change the password to a randomly-generated maximum-length password
4. Store the credentials and occasionally log in to reset the password

**Important:** Even after successful deletion, some information may be retained. Companies are sometimes legally required to keep certain data, particularly for financial transactions.

## Preventing Future Account Accumulation

Before signing up for a new account, ask yourself:

- "Do I really need this?"
- "Can I accomplish my goal without creating an account?"

Deleting an account is often much harder than creating one. Even after deletion, cached versions may exist on third-party services like the [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/). Your future self will thank you for exercising restraint.

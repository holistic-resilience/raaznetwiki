# A Journalist's Guide to Digital Security Risk Assessment

If you've joined one of our digital security team's many training sessions, you know that we kick off nearly all of them by covering risk assessment.

**What is a risk assessment (or "threat model")?** It's a way journalists and filmmakers can determine potential digital security challenges and develop strategies to mitigate them. It's a systematic process for identifying and evaluating the digital assets you own, create, or engage with.

This tool is extensible—you can work through this process again and again, perhaps as a first step for covering a new story, or even during each phase of an investigation. Risk will change based on the work you are trying to do and where you are trying to do it.

---

## My Assets: What Am I Protecting?

- **Devices** — cellphones, computers, and tablets
- **Communications** — email, texts, social media posts, and DMs
- **Documents** — tips from sources, field notes, and drafts
- **Footage** — interviews and b-roll
- **Browsing data** — search history

From morning until night, if you are using a device, you are generating an asset of some kind. One way to start your risk assessment is to take note of everything you do in a day with your phone, computer, and anything else that might store your information.

Once you've tracked your activities, see if you can find patterns in the dataset you are building. This can narrow the field to assets you'd like to protect, prioritizing those most important to you (e.g., passwords to accounts containing your personal information or contact information of sensitive sources). Identifying high-priority assets will help you address risks efficiently and without too much stress.

---

## My Adversary: Who Am I Protecting My Assets From?

### Governments
Foreign and domestic governments are in the business of collecting and using our data in a variety of ways. There are many recorded cases in which they gain access to privileged information through networked devices, even using targeted exploits specifically aimed at a person or group. Governments also use dragnet surveillance policies that impact nearly everyone using the internet.

### Corporations
Corporations are increasingly buying and selling our data for many reasons, from targeted advertising to shopping our personal information for financial gain. Our personal information can also become an asset when companies are bought and sold.

### Hackers
Hackers exploit vulnerabilities in systems to gain a foothold, perhaps for ransomware or data exfiltration purposes. Hackers often work in groups and, in certain cases, are sponsored by nation-states.

### Scammers
Scammers are ubiquitous, engaging in social engineering with psychological manipulation so you will divulge confidential information or otherwise behave in a way beneficial to them. Venues for these types of attacks include phishing emails, phone calls, and text messages—among other new tactics.

### Trolls
Trolls engage in a range of provocative behaviors online, including sharing your personal information (sometimes your address and phone number) to unleash threats that transcend the digital world for the real one.

> While journalists are a high-risk group, these adversaries affect most everyone whose information is online. Rest assured, there are lots of steps you can take to protect yourself. Knowledge is power.

## The Adversary's Resources: What Might They Be Capable Of?

When we speak in training sessions to journalists, filmmakers, and advocates, we break adversary capabilities into two categories:

### Legal Requests
Government agencies around the world can *legally* request user information from service providers (e.g., Meta, Google, or your internet service provider). This data is purportedly collected for national security purposes or as a means of gathering evidence in a criminal case. In the United States, journalists have legal protections for information collected during newsgathering.

### Illegal Activities
Some adversaries will engage in illegal activities, like hacking networks and systems, to gain unlawful access to sensitive information. They may extort organizations and individuals. They may attempt to trick you into disclosing private information.

**Important:** These actors do not distinguish between professional and personal accounts, and in many cases are not above relying on intimidation tactics like online harassment and physical threats.

## Likelihood: How Big of a Risk Is This to Me Now?

The most important question in the risk assessment may be how likely you are to be targeted. We regularly hear about scary new cybersecurity threats. At the same time, we use technologies every day that we aren't meant to fully understand. It can be hard to figure out the likelihood of any given threat against us as individuals.

Even within the field of journalism, your identity is a factor worth considering. For example, female journalists are far more likely to experience online harassment than their male peers.

## My Resources: What Can I Do to Protect Myself?

### Protect Yourself from Hacking and Account Takeovers
Dial in on your account security:
- Write solid passwords
- Use a password manager
- Choose and use strong two-factor authentication methods
- Stay aware of phishing tactics and techniques

### Safeguard Your Communications
Opt into encrypted channels like Signal and WhatsApp. Learn about encrypting your email messages too.

### Limit Access to Your Browsing Habits
- Thoughtfully choose a browser
- Understand the use cases for a virtual private network (VPN)

### Keep Prying Eyes Out of Your Source Communications
- Set up a tipline
- Keep a keen eye out for malware

### Seek Additional Training
Since our expertise lies in digital security, and the online and physical world are interconnected in ways we haven't seen before, we also recommend that journalists seek out hostile environment and first aid training from organizations including the International Women's Media Foundation (IWMF).

## Summary: The Five Questions of Risk Assessment

1. **My assets:** What am I protecting?
2. **My adversary:** Who am I protecting my assets from?
3. **The adversary's resources:** What might they be capable of?
4. **Likelihood:** How big of a risk is this to me now?
5. **My resources:** What can I do to protect myself?

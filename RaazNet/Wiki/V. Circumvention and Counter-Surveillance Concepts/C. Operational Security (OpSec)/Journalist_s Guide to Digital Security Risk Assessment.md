# A Journalist's Guide to Digital Security Risk Assessment

A **risk assessment** (or "threat model") is a systematic process for journalists and filmmakers to identify potential digital security challenges and develop strategies to mitigate them. It involves evaluating the digital assets you own, create, or engage with.

This tool is **extensible**—you can work through this process repeatedly as a first step for covering a new story or during each phase of an investigation. Risk changes based on the work you're doing and where you're doing it.

---

## What Am I Protecting? (My Assets)

Digital assets include:

- **Devices**: Cellphones, computers, tablets
- **Communications**: Email, texts, social media posts, DMs
- **Documents**: Tips from sources, field notes, drafts
- **Footage**: Interviews, b-roll
- **Browsing data**: Search history

From morning until night, using any device generates assets. Start your risk assessment by tracking everything you do with your devices throughout a day.

Look for patterns in your activities to identify which assets need protection. **Prioritize high-value assets** such as:
- Passwords to accounts containing personal information
- Contact information of sensitive sources

---

## Who Am I Protecting My Assets From? (Adversaries)

### Governments
Both foreign and domestic governments collect data through:
- Targeted exploits aimed at specific individuals or groups
- Dragnet surveillance policies affecting all internet users

### Corporations
Companies buy and sell personal data for:
- Targeted advertising
- Financial gain through data brokers
- Asset acquisition during mergers and bankruptcies

### Hackers
Exploit system vulnerabilities for:
- Ransomware attacks
- Data exfiltration
- Nation-state sponsored operations

### Scammers
Use social engineering through:
- Phishing emails
- Phone calls and text messages
- Psychological manipulation tactics

### Trolls
Engage in provocative behaviors including:
- Doxxing (sharing personal information publicly)
- Coordinated harassment campaigns
- Threats that extend beyond digital spaces

---

## What Are Adversaries Capable Of?

### Legal Methods
Government agencies can legally request user information from service providers (Meta, Google, ISPs) for:
- National security purposes
- Criminal investigations

*Note: In the United States, journalists have legal protections for information collected during newsgathering under the Privacy Protection Act of 1980.*

### Illegal Methods
- Hacking networks and systems
- Extortion
- Tricking users into disclosing private information

**Important**: Adversaries do not distinguish between professional and personal accounts, and may use intimidation tactics including online harassment and physical threats.

---

## How Big Is the Risk to Me Now? (Likelihood)

Assessing likelihood requires considering:

- **Your current work**: What stories are you covering?
- **Your identity**: Female journalists experience significantly higher rates of online harassment
- **Your location**: Geographic and jurisdictional factors
- **Your visibility**: Public profile and platform presence

---

## What Can I Do to Protect Myself? (Resources)

### Account Security
- Use strong, unique passwords with a password manager
- Enable strong two-factor authentication
- Stay aware of phishing tactics

### Secure Communications
- Use encrypted messaging apps (Signal, WhatsApp)
- Consider email encryption options
- Configure security settings appropriately

### Browsing Protection
- Choose privacy-focused browsers
- Understand when to use VPNs

### Source Protection
- Set up secure tiplines
- Be cautious with unknown USB drives and files

### Additional Training
Consider hostile environment and first aid training from organizations like the International Women's Media Foundation for situations where digital and physical security intersect.

---

## Next Steps

Risk assessment is an ongoing process. Revisit these questions regularly:

1. What assets am I currently generating?
2. Who might want access to them?
3. What are their capabilities?
4. How likely is targeting?
5. What protective measures can I implement?

For personalized risk assessment or organizational training, contact the Freedom of the Press Foundation's digital security team.

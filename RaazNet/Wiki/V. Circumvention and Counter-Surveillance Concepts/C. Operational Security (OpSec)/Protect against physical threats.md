# Protect Against Physical Threats

We do a lot of digital work to protect sensitive information, but that is only one aspect of information security. The work you do to protect your valuable devices and documents can be undone in an instant if your devices are lost, stolen, tampered with, confiscated, or damaged. Planning for physical security is as important as protecting your devices digitally.

Careful risk assessment, maintaining a safe work environment, and writing up a security policy can help you avoid physical disasters. Even if you are not working with a formal organization, it is a good idea to write out guidelines and response plans for yourself, your household, and the people you work with.

Both criminals and politically motivated attackers may have reasons to target your data. They might be seeking financial information, sensitive data related to your work, or personal details they can use to intimidate, blackmail, or impersonate you. You could also be targeted due to information received from others, such as contact lists, interviews, testimonies, or identifying information on victims or witnesses.

> **Important**: While working on your physical protection, you will exchange and store sensitive information. Make sure to protect both your [sensitive information](https://securityinabox.org/en/files/secure-file-storage) and the [privacy of your online communications](https://securityinabox.org/en/communication/private-communication/).

## Low-Tech Security Measures

While it is important to budget for physical security, money is not the only way to achieve protection. Your actions, procedures, teamwork, preparation, planning, practice, time, and learning are all free of cost.

**Key principles:**

- Consider creative, low-tech protection solutions—a dog can be as effective as a surveillance camera
- Plan for multiple security measures; like threads in a rope, more layers create stronger protection
- Use "defense in depth"—each measure may be defeated, but the combination provides stronger security

## Creating a Physical Security Policy

### Working with Colleagues and Family

If you live with other people or share an office with another organization, talk to them about security. Determine what behaviors you can expect from one another and from visitors.

**Policy development steps:**

- Get to know your neighbors—they may provide support or early warning
- Set aside dedicated time to work on a policy document
- Schedule regular dates to revisit and update the policy
- Plan regular security training and capacity-building exercises
- Establish security briefing procedures and expectations for sharing incident information
- Store and back up policy documents in accessible locations
- Plan how to present your policy to new organization members

**Resources:**
- [Front Line Defenders' Workbook on Security](https://www.frontlinedefenders.org/en/resource-publication/workbook-security-practical-steps-human-rights-defenders-risk)
- [Access Now Digital Security Helpline's security policy templates](https://gitlab.com/AccessNowHelpline/helpline_documentation_resources/-/tree/master/templates/Organizational_Security_Policies-Templates)

### Emergency Communication Plan

Your plan should address:

- Network of allies and supporters who can provide assistance
- Emergency contacts and medical conditions of staff
- Contacts for fire, flood, or natural disaster response
- Procedures for responding to burglary or office raid
- Steps to take if a device is lost or stolen
- Who to notify if sensitive information is disclosed or misplaced
- How to recover information from off-site backup systems
- How to perform important emergency repairs
- Contact information for utility providers (power, water, internet)

### What Goes in Your Plan?

Consider documenting:

- How information might be lost or compromised, and consequences
- What devices you use and their physical locations
- Policy on personal device use for work
- Communication channels and usage protocols
- Sensitive information storage methods (including encryption status, backup locations, and key/password access)
- Procedures for destroying sensitive data
- Travel security protocols
- Emergency response procedures for different scenarios

## Office Access Policy

Your policy should include rules about key distribution, monitoring systems, alarm systems, and procedures for deliveries, maintenance, and cleaning staff.

### Physical Security Layers

Think of your physical security as having multiple layers, and plan protection for each:

1. **Perimeter** (building exterior, grounds)
2. **Building access** (doors, windows, entry points)
3. **Internal zones** (restricted areas, sensitive workspaces)
4. **Device level** (individual computers, storage)

### Space Arrangement

- Decide which parts of your space should be restricted to visitors
- Arrange rooms for greater privacy and security
- Purchase devices from known, trusted vendors (Apple, Google, Samsung, Sony) to avoid pre-loaded malware and ensure security updates
- Keep device ports (USB, ethernet) out of reach of public areas to prevent unauthorized access

---

## Working Outside Your Home or Office

- Treat public wifi and internet cafes as insecure
- Carry your laptop in something that does not look like a laptop bag
- Keep devices near you at all times—do not leave them unattended while charging
- Consider traveling with a security cable for securing devices to fixed objects
- Be aware that thieves exploit meal times and restroom visits
- Remember that hotel safe deposit boxes are accessible to staff with master keys

---

## Surveillance Cameras and Motion Sensors

### Considerations

- Low-tech solutions (bells, alarms, dogs) can be as effective as cameras for fast alerts
- CCTV cameras may require someone to monitor them or review footage
- Consider whether cameras could put workers at risk if adversaries gained access to footage

### Recommendations

- Avoid "Internet of Things" devices like Amazon Ring—they are vulnerable to spying and may share footage with law enforcement without permission
- Consider using [Haven](https://securityinabox.org/en/assess-plan/tools/#haven), designed for human rights defenders to monitor spaces with control over their own data
- Transmit and store video encrypted
- Plan for physical access protection, retention periods, and secure deletion of footage

## Protecting Your Local Network

### Wired vs. Wireless

- Ethernet cables may be safer than wireless, which can be breached without physical access
- Avoid running ethernet cables outside your building or protected areas where they could be tampered with

### Wireless Network Security

- Use a [strong passphrase](https://securityinabox.org/en/passwords/passwords)
- [Set your network to use WPA2 or WPA3 security](https://securityinabox.org/en/phones-and-computers/malware/#secure-your-router)
- Change your wifi network name to something that does not identify you or your organization
- Create a separate, password-protected guest wifi account

### Network Infrastructure

- Consider installing a firewall router between your network and your ISP's router to prevent ISP access to your network traffic
- Avoid connecting unnecessary devices (TVs, cameras, IoT devices, game consoles) to your network
- Regularly check connected devices and unplug those not in use
- Lock networking equipment (servers, routers, switches, modems) in secure rooms or cabinets
- Ensure servers encrypt their data

## Preventing Accidents and Outages

Computers and networking equipment are delicate. Electric fluctuations, extreme temperatures, dust, and moisture can cause damage.

### Power Protection

- Use electric sockets with ground lines to prolong equipment life and reduce failure risk
- Use surge protectors (check for maximum voltage and joule rating)
- Consider power filters or line conditioners for unstable electricity
- Place power equipment where it cannot be accidentally unplugged
- Install Uninterruptible Power Supplies (UPSs) for servers and desktop computers
- Have battery-powered or solar-charged lighting available

### Environment

- Test power supply before plugging in important equipment in new buildings
- Have a trusted electrician review cabling
- Use high-quality cables, surge protectors, and power strips
- Ensure adequate ventilation—do not house equipment near heating/cooling vents

## Protecting Against Theft, Tampering, and Confiscation

### Physical Locks

- Install high-quality locks on doors and windows
- Consider laptop safes or locking cabinets for sensitive documents and equipment
- Use device locks where possible

### Device Placement

- Avoid placing important devices in easily accessible locations (hallways, reception areas, near windows/doors)
- Protect main electric switches with locks

### When You're Away

Consider whether devices are safer with you or left behind, weighing:
- Likelihood of space being raided vs. being detained with devices
- Your activities (travel, border crossings, protests)

**Tamper detection methods:**
- Use security tape on USB ports and disk drives
- Scatter small objects over devices and photograph them before leaving
- Use monitoring apps like [Haven](https://securityinabox.org/en/assess-plan/tools/#haven)
- Consider locked boxes for device storage

### Device Registration

If your local law enforcement is trustworthy, registering device model and serial numbers may help recover stolen devices.

---

## Protecting Visual Information

### Clean Desk Policy

Ensure sensitive information is not left visible, including:
- Passwords
- Paper calendars and planners
- Journals and address books
- Sticky notes

### Screen Privacy

- Position screens so they cannot be seen from outside (account for windows, doors, waiting areas)
- Use privacy screens for laptops, monitors, tablets, and smartphones
- Avoid using devices in public where shoulder-surfing is possible

### Location Privacy

If hiding your location, be aware that visual and audio clues in calls or videos can reveal where you are.

### Camera Covers

Cover device cameras when not in use to prevent unauthorized surveillance. [Learn more about camera covers](https://securityinabox.org/en/phones-and-computers/windows/#use-a-camera-cover).

---

## Disposing of Sensitive Information

- Set a regular schedule to securely erase files
- Learn how to [securely delete files, wipe blank space, and dispose of old devices](https://securityinabox.org/en/files/destroy-sensitive-information)
- Incorporate secure deletion procedures for all devices (Android, iOS, Windows, macOS, Linux) into your security plan
- When disposing of hard drives, physically destroy them (drill, hammer, nails)—do not burn, use acid, or microwave

# Use Two-Factor Authentication

Two-factor authentication (2FA), also called multi-factor authentication (MFA), adds a second layer of protection to your login system. To access an account, you need:

1. **Something you know** — your password or passphrase
2. **Something you own** — a hardware device, authenticator app, or phone to receive codes

By using 2FA, even if someone guesses your password, they cannot log in without the second factor. This second factor is harder to obtain because it's either generated by a physical device you own or sent to you at the moment you need it.

## Setting Up Two-Factor Authentication

To check which services offer 2FA and find setup instructions, visit the [2FA Directory website](https://2fa.directory/).

**Priority accounts for 2FA setup:**
- Email accounts
- Banking and financial services
- Social media accounts
- Cloud storage services
- Any account containing sensitive information

## Authentication Methods

### Authenticator Apps
Software applications that generate time-based one-time codes. Examples include:
- Aegis (Android)
- Raivo OTP (iOS)
- Bitwarden Authenticator

### Hardware Security Devices
Physical devices (also called security tokens, dongles, or FIDO U2F keys) that you plug into your computer or phone, or connect wirelessly via NFC.

## Choosing the Right 2FA Method

**Safety ranking (most to least secure):**
1. Hardware device or authenticator app
2. Email-based codes
3. SMS text messages

**Key considerations:**
- You can use one authenticator app or hardware device for multiple services
- Authenticator apps and hardware devices work offline
- SMS and email methods require network connectivity
- SMS may not work when traveling internationally or in areas without coverage
- **Never disable 2FA once enabled** — some services offer this for convenience, but it compromises your security

> **Why this matters:** Multiple layers of protection mean that if your password is compromised, the second factor still protects your account. While SMS is convenient, it's the least secure 2FA option due to vulnerabilities like SIM swapping.

## Protecting Your Backup Codes

When you activate 2FA, most services provide backup codes. These are critical for account recovery if you lose access to your 2FA device.

### Best Practices for Backup Codes

1. **Store codes in a password manager** as an attachment to the relevant account entry
2. **Keep codes separate** from other login information — consider creating a separate password manager database on a different device
3. **Generate backup codes manually** if not provided automatically
4. **Save the QR code** — take a screenshot when setting up your authenticator app and store it securely
5. **Set up multiple 2FA methods** when possible (e.g., both an authenticator app and a hardware device)

> **Why this matters:** Backup codes never expire and provide your only way back into an account if you lose your 2FA device. However, anyone with access to these codes can use them to log in, so they must be stored securely and separately from your passwords.

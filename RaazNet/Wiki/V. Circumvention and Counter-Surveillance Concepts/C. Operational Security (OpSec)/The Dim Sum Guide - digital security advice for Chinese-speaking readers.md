# The Dim Sum Guide: Digital Security for Chinese-Speaking Readers

**By Haven | Published 2025.02.12**

> **Access the guide:** [https://yummydimsum.github.io/](https://yummydimsum.github.io/)

## Overview

Some countries experience more complex censorship and cyber-attacks against human rights defenders than others. People from China are one of the groups in need of contextualised digital protection. The Dim Sum guide, developed with support from Front Line Defenders, builds on Security in a Box content while providing specific instructions for Chinese-speaking readers. **Ideally, both guides should be read together.**

## Key Topics Covered

### iOS Device Restrictions in China

The guide details restrictions on iPhone and iPad usage in China:

- **No eSIM support**
- **FaceTime limited to video only** (no audio calls)
- **Heavy content censorship** on books, music, movies, and other media

#### AirDrop Warning

During the 2022 "Sitongqiao Protests" and "White Paper Movement," many Chinese people used AirDrop to share protest-related content. However, in early 2024, Beijing authorities reportedly traced mobile phone numbers and email accounts of AirDrop senders.

> **Recommendation:** Avoid using AirDrop entirely due to identity tracing risks.

### Safer Chinese Input Methods

Chinese input methods are essential software for Chinese language users, but inappropriate apps risk exposing sensitive information. Research has proven that some Chinese company input methods contain security vulnerabilities or backdoors.

The Dim Sum guide provides recommendations for more secure Chinese input methods across different operating systems.

### Secure VPNs for China

China maintains one of the world's strictest internet filtering regimes. While "going over the Great Firewall" via VPN is routine for many, not all VPNs are stable or secure. Reports indicate Chinese police have developed VPN applications specifically designed to phish user data.

The guide recommends secure censorship circumvention solutions and is **constantly monitored and updated** with current information.

### Responding to Police Phone Searches

Following the 2022 "White Paper Movement," police in many Chinese cities began randomly stopping pedestrians to check devices for VPNs, Telegram, and similar tools.

The Dim Sum guide explains how to respond to police phone checks from both:
- **Digital security perspectives**
- **Legal perspectives**

## Conclusion

The Dim Sum guide aims to help Chinese readers—especially human rights defenders—protect their activities, data, and devices. However, the digital landscape constantly shifts, and no resource guarantees 100% security.

The guide will continue to be monitored and updated with the latest recommendations.

# Preparing for Online Harassment

Journalists are increasingly attacked online for their work, both by persistent individuals and collective mobs. Harassment takes many forms, with journalists, their families, and colleagues receiving barrages of hate-filled messages. Sometimes these threats escalate to the physical world when harassers attempt to identify a reporter's address and location.

**It's far easier to prepare defenses in advance than while experiencing the stress of harassment.** Preparing for these situations puts you in control and can be deeply empowering.

The following resources help journalists prepare for harassment by cultivating digital security and psychological safety.

## Comprehensive Guides

### [PEN America's Online Harassment Field Manual](https://onlineharassmentfieldmanual.pen.org/)

An exhaustive set of resources covering:
- **Preventive measures** when anticipating harassment
- **Response strategies** for active harassment (including documentation)
- **Psychological safety** considerations
- **Law enforcement** engagement guidance
- **Employer resources** and support strategies

## Protecting Your Personal Information

### [Yael Grauer's Big Ass Data Broker Opt-Out List](https://github.com/yaelwrites/Big-Ass-Data-Broker-Opt-Out-List)

Harassers often locate targets through data broker sites (Acxiom, Radaris, etc.) that aggregate personal information from:
- State and public court records
- Publicly accessible social media
- Personal data sold by other companies

This comprehensive list provides opt-out procedures for dozens of data broker websites, allowing you to request removal of your personal information.

### [NYT Open's How to Dox Yourself on the Internet](https://open.nytimes.com/how-to-dox-yourself-on-the-internet-d2892b4c5954)

From The New York Times' Information Security team—structured guidance for:
- Determining your digital footprint
- Finding your personal information on search engines and data brokers
- Locking down social media accounts with optimal privacy and security settings

## Key Takeaways

1. **Proactive preparation** is more effective than reactive response
2. **Remove personal data** from data broker sites before harassment occurs
3. **Audit your digital footprint** to understand your exposure
4. **Document everything** if harassment begins
5. **Seek support** from established organizations and communities

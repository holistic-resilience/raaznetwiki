# Protests: Protect Your Identity and Information

Many human rights defenders and activists depend heavily on computers and mobile phones for organizing and coordinating peaceful protests. This makes confiscation, theft, and surveillance of our devices and communications a priority for adversaries attempting to limit free speech and assembly.

While transparency and open engagement can be useful security tools in many contexts, it may be important to protect the identity and sensitive information of those involved in a protest.

## Understanding the Surveillance Threat

When planning or coordinating a protest over the internet or using mobile phones, consider ways of protecting your identity and that of your network. Authorities and others may put social movements under surveillance to predict, prevent, or disrupt protests.

**Common surveillance tactics include:**
- Direct observation and infiltration
- Automated search engines scanning social networks for specific words or images
- Monitoring of known activist groups and their connections

## Protecting Your Online Identity

Social media is often heavily surveilled, making it potentially dangerous to rely on for organizing protests.

**Recommended practices:**
- Avoid using your real name on social networks when organizing or participating in actions
- Avoid associating yourself with groups likely to be under surveillance on social media
- Be cautious about accepting friend requests and connections with protesters you don't already know
- Use a VPN or [Tor Browser](https://torproject.org/) to hide your IP address and browsing activities from site administrators and your internet service provider

## Securing Your Mobile Device

To protect against confiscation, loss, or theft of your device, take these precautions before attending a protest.

### Android Devices

Devices running Android 4.0 or later typically allow encryption of stored data:

1. Navigate to **Security Settings** on your phone
2. Select the option to encrypt the phone
3. **Important:** Encrypt your device at least a day before the protest to allow time for:
   - Backing up your data first
   - Completing the encryption process (approximately one hour)

> **Note:** The Micro-SD card may not be encrypted by this option—only built-in storage is protected.

### iPhone and iPad

1. Enter **Privacy Settings**
2. Enable a passphrase to lock the screen
3. Verify that "Data protection is enabled" appears once the passphrase is set
4. **Optional:** Enable the setting to erase data after 10 incorrect passphrase attempts (this can also serve as an emergency data deletion method)

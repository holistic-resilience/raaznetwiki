# Protect the Privacy of Your Online Communications

Each communication method—digital or otherwise—comes with advantages and disadvantages in terms of convenience, popularity, cost, and security. It's up to each of us to weigh the benefits and risks of the methods we use to communicate. When our risks are higher, it is wise to choose our communication tools more deliberately.

This guide illustrates the steps needed to create and implement a communication plan, along with specific best practices aimed at protecting your privacy and sensitive information when communicating with others online.

---

## Map Your Communications and Make a Plan

### Assess the Sensitivity Level of Your Communications

Start by understanding your current communication landscape:

- **Map your communications**: Who is communicating with whom? What channels are you using? What kind of information are you sharing, and in what forms?
- **Evaluate the risks**: Ask yourself what would happen if information you need to share was compromised, lost, or exposed
- **Rethink your approach**: Reorganize your communications based on their sensitivity level
- **Prioritize protection**: Decide how you will protect the most sensitive communications

> **Why this matters**: Sensitive information that is compromised can:
> - Disrupt work continuity through loss of critical files
> - Endanger individuals through identification or contextual details
> - Enable impersonation or reputational harm
> - Violate laws in specific jurisdictions

### Reflect on Who Needs Access

Decide whether the information you are sharing should be restricted to specific recipients or can be shared more widely.

### Analyze Your Needs and Capacities

Ask yourself these questions when choosing a communication channel:

- Should participants' identities be kept secret from administrators and others with server access?
- Do you need real-time communication, or can you wait for replies?
- What formats do you need to send—text only, or other content types?
- Do you prefer writing, calls, or video conferences?
- What devices and operating systems are you and your contacts using?
- What tools do your contacts already use? Can they learn new ones?
- What's your budget? Can you self-host your communication platforms?

### Keep Basic Security Measures in Mind

When choosing communication platforms:

- **Enable 2-factor authentication** whenever available
- **Ensure HTTPS/TLS is enabled** on whatever tool you use
- **Verify end-to-end encryption** claims—check if the encryption technology is free and open-source

> **Understanding encryption types**: Most online services use client-to-server encryption (HTTPS/TLS), which protects messages in transit but leaves them readable on the provider's servers. End-to-end encryption ensures only you and your recipient can read the messages—even the service provider cannot access them.

If you cannot find a platform that offers end-to-end encryption for all your needs (such as large video conferences), consider using a service hosted by a trusted entity, or self-hosting if you have the technical expertise.

### Define a Communication Plan

Based on your assessment, establish and rehearse a communication plan:

1. **Define a Traffic Light Protocol (TLP)** to categorize what sensitive information can be shared with whom, in what forms, and over which channels. See [CIRCL's Traffic Light Protocol](https://www.circl.lu/pub/traffic-light-protocol/) for guidance.

2. **Talk to your contacts**: Propose, discuss, and agree upon specific plans for how you will (and will not) communicate

3. **Consider informal steps**: Code words, backup channels, check-in schedules

4. **Consider formal steps**: Written policies, secure communication hierarchies

5. **Rehearse your plan**: Practice before a crisis occurs

> **Why rehearse?** In a crisis, we don't think as clearly and may need to act fast. A rehearsed plan helps prevent emergencies and contributes to safety in critical situations.

## Create Multiple Identities

Consider creating alternative online identities to separate your communications based on levels of trust.

Using different identities for each of your activities can help mitigate threats like political or economic retribution for what you do online.

**Learn more**: [Managing and protecting multiple online identities](https://securityinabox.org/en/communication/multiple-identities/)

## Protect and Anonymize Your Connections

Two key strategies for protecting your online activities:

1. **Browse more securely** to protect your activities from surveillance on websites you access through your browser
   - [Safer browsing guide](https://securityinabox.org/en/internet-connection/safer-browsing)

2. **Anonymize your connections** to achieve goals without leaving traces, especially in high-risk situations
   - [Anonymity guide](https://securityinabox.org/en/internet-connection/anonymity)

> **Note**: Using the Tor Browser or other anonymity tools allows you to visit websites without disclosing your identity or location. However, if you log into a service with your usual account, you will still share that account information with the platform.

## Use Email More Safely

Although many people prefer encrypted chat apps or video calls for everyday communications, email remains essential for creating accounts, organizing work, and maintaining formal communications.

**Key considerations**:
- Email is not encrypted by default
- It carries significant metadata
- Messages persist on provider servers
- It exposes you to phishing and malware risks

Despite these limitations, email is a resilient technology that ensures communication continuity even when servers experience downtime.

**Learn more**: [Secure email communications guide](https://securityinabox.org/en/communication/secure-email/)

Chat services may access and collect information about your location, activity, content, and contacts. Choosing the right app is critical to protecting your safety.

## Remove Identifying Information from Files

When sharing photos, videos, PDFs, and other files, be aware that:

- Face blur features or covering sensitive details may not fully protect people or places in your images
- Some editing methods preserve hidden information that can be recovered
- All files contain **metadata**—invisible information about when, where, by whom, and with what device they were created

You can view some metadata by right-clicking a file and selecting "Properties" or "Get Info."

**Learn more**: [Removing identifying information from files](https://securityinabox.org/en/files/destroy-identifying-information/)

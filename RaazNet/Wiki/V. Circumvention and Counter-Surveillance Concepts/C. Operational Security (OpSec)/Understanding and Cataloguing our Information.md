# Understanding and Cataloguing Our Information

To get a complete picture of our security situation, we must treat information as a valuable asset. This section guides you through mapping your information and exploring threats to its integrity.

## Types of Information We Handle

- **Work products**: Reports, databases, images, voice and video recordings
- **Operational information**: Text messages, files, progress reports, and other office communications
- **Personal information**: Data that identifies us as members of an organization, along with personal or professional affiliations
- **Metadata**: Data generated by our use of digital devices that can track movements or monitor relationships

This information can be stored and communicated in many ways. If accessed by adversaries, it can provide an alarmingly comprehensive picture of our actions and relationships—making it a valuable asset that requires careful protection.

## The Digital Security Challenge

As human rights defenders have gained increased access to digital technologies, their opponents have shown greater interest in accessing devices and intercepting communications. Several factors compound this challenge:

- Many popular digital tools offer limited protection against well-resourced adversaries
- Some opponents may have collaborative relationships with software providers or online service companies
- Service providers should be considered when building your actor map

**Learning how digital technologies work and increasing digital literacy is an important and empowering step in activism.**

## Categories of Information

The first step in creating an information security strategy is understanding:
- What information we have
- Where it resides
- How it moves from place to place

Cataloguing information helps us view it as a tangible asset rather than a vague mass of data, enabling greater control and allowing us to pinpoint vulnerabilities.

### Information States

| State | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **At Rest** | Stationary, stored information | Files in a filing cabinet, data on a hard drive |
| **In Motion** | Information that travels | Mobile phone messages, email exchanges |

> **Note**: Digital information often changes states. Cloud storage data is typically at rest, but enters motion when uploaded or accessed as it travels across the internet.

## Mapping Your Information Ecosystem

Create a map of your personal or organizational information. This can be a text document or spreadsheet and should be updated regularly.

> **Exercise**: [Information Ecosystem Mapping](https://holistic-security.tacticaltech.org/exercises/explore/information-ecosystem.html)

### Key Questions for Your Information Map

**1. What information is it?**
Group similar types together. For example:
- All financial documents may belong in one category
- Not all emails belong together—categorize by sensitivity
- Include software you use, as some applications can be considered sensitive

Pay attention to metadata in documents and communications (such as pictures and emails). Consider whether this metadata could be sensitive:
- Some metadata can be removed or distorted
- Email metadata is typically visible, but you can reduce sensitivity (e.g., using vague subject lines)

**2. Where does it reside?**
Document physical and digital storage locations.

**3. Who can access it?**
Identify all individuals and systems with access.

**4. How sensitive is it?**

| Classification | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Secret** | Only specific persons should have access |
| **Confidential** | Not for public consumption, but staff may access |
| **Public** | No risk from public exposure |

## Building Your Security Strategy

By categorizing and annotating your information map with the above questions, you can:

1. Get an overview of your current sensitive information status
2. Identify who may have access to what
3. Spot gaps in your current information security
4. Begin building a strategy to close these gaps

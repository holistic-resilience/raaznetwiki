# Building New Approaches to Security

Defining a comprehensive security strategy differs significantly from taking an ad-hoc or improvised approach. While many instinctive reactions to threats keep us safe, some may not—and can even be harmful.

This chapter builds on tactics and capacities identified through threat assessment, connecting them to an overall strategy for maintaining and expanding the socio-political space for human rights work.

## Three Core Security Strategies

Security strategies for human rights defenders typically fall into three complementary categories:

### Acceptance Strategies

Engage with other actors—allies, neutral parties, and opponents—to foster tolerance, acceptance, and support for human rights activities. Implementation methods include:

- Advocacy and campaigning
- Diplomatic engagement
- Public education initiatives

### Protection Strategies

Focus on learning and implementing new methods that defend your space for work:

- Developing new security practices and protocols
- Leveraging ally networks for protection
- Bridging gaps in existing security measures

### Deterrence Strategies

Raise the cost of attacks against you or your work for adversaries:

- Increase monetary, reputational, or political costs for attackers
- Requires well-developed actor mapping to identify helpful allies
- Depends on advocacy, campaigning, and diplomatic activities

Most effective security plans incorporate activities from two or all three categories, depending on context and identified threats.

## Building Security Capacities

Effective capacity building requires attention to five interconnected elements:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Well-being** | Physical and mental conditions for active learning, plus adequate time and safe space |
| **Attitudes** | Subjective assessment of learning value; can be affected by stress, fear, and trauma |
| **Knowledge** | Understanding of political, economic, social, technological, legal, and environmental context |
| **Skills** | Abilities to engage with your environment—physical fitness, advocacy, technical tools, self-care |
| **Resources** | Financial, material, and human resources available for capacity building |

## Working with External Trainers and Consultants

External support can help organizations develop security plans and skills. When engaging consultants:

**What to Look For:**
- Trainers who empower you regarding your own security situation
- Focus on tools and tactics relevant to your specific activities
- Collaborative approach involving group members in analysis

**What to Avoid:**
- "Quick-fix" solutions implemented without your involvement
- Analysis conducted without group participation
- Consultants who make decisions for you
- Claims of immediate security improvements

### Selecting Trainers

Before engaging a trainer, consider:

- **Recommendations**: Seek advice from trusted colleagues, fellow defenders, and international organizations
- **Expectations**: Define clear learning goals while respecting trainer expertise on achievable outcomes
- **Qualifications**: Evaluate relevant experience and knowledge
- **Logistics**: Language, timing, location, and follow-up opportunities
- **Group buy-in**: Ensure all participants are engaged; disinterest negatively impacts training effectiveness

## Resource Considerations

Improving security capacities often has financial implications. Budget considerations may include:

- Hardware replacement (compromised devices)
- Training costs
- Security tools and software
- Consultant fees

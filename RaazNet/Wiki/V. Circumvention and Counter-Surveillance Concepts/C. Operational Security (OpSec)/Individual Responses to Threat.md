# Individual Responses to Threat

Understanding how we naturally perceive and respond to threats is fundamental to developing an effective security practice. While our survival instincts work constantly to keep us safe, several factors can distort our threat perception—particularly in digital environments.

## Intuition and Survival Responses

Your natural survival mechanisms are already working to protect you, even before you develop an organized approach to security. However, there are important situations where our instincts may not be reliable guides.

## The Digital Space Challenge

The digital realm—our computers, phones, devices, and communication systems—is deeply woven into our daily lives. Yet our ability to detect digital threats remains far less developed than our capacity to recognize physical dangers.

**Key challenges in digital threat perception:**

- **Invisibility**: Surveillance and harassment online is often secretive and difficult to perceive
- **Prevention difficulties**: Digital threats can be hard to prevent or pursue justice for
- **Perception gaps**: We may fail to recognize real threats, or conversely perceive threats that don't exist

## How Trauma, Stress, and Fatigue Affect Perception

Our mental and emotional state significantly impacts our ability to identify and respond to threats accurately.

### Trauma's Impact

Experiencing disturbing or traumatic events can distort our danger responses in two ways:

- **Hypervigilance**: Overreacting and seeing danger where none exists
- **Numbness**: Mental exhaustion causing us to stop paying attention and miss real dangers

### Stress and Fatigue Effects

Both stress and fatigue affect our ability to realistically gauge threat levels. Managing these requires:

- Maintaining work/life balance
- Being realistic about workload and commitments
- Avoiding becoming overwhelmed

### Understanding the Stress Curve

A certain amount of stress is normal—even healthy—and prevents boredom or depression. We can cope with elevated stress for short periods, but prolonged high stress leads to:

1. Feeling overwhelmed
2. Panic responses
3. Despondency
4. Potential depression

## Building a Culture of Self-Care

Developing stress management and self-care practices—both individually and within groups, organizations, or movements—is fundamental to holistic security. This approach:

- Prevents threats caused by long-term stress and fatigue exposure
- Enhances critical thinking about security in general
- Supports sustainable activism and advocacy work

# Defend Against Phone Number Theft (SIM Swapping)

Your phone number is more valuable to attackers than you might realize. Because it's commonly used to log into financial services and receive two-factor authentication codes, criminals actively target phone numbers for fraud and cryptocurrency theft.

## Understanding SIM Swapping

**SIM swapping** is an attack where criminals impersonate you to your phone carrier. They convince a customer service representative that they've gotten a new phone and need their number transferred to a new device. Once successful, the attacker can:

- Intercept two-factor authentication codes sent via text message
- Break into online accounts that rely on SMS verification
- Access financial services and cryptocurrency wallets

## What You Can Do

### Enable Carrier Protection Against Phone Number Theft

Most major carriers now offer features to prevent unauthorized SIM swaps:

| Carrier | Protection Feature | How to Enable |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| **AT&T** | [Wireless Account Lock](https://www.att.com/support/article/wireless/000102016/) | Via AT&T website or app |
| **T-Mobile** | [SIM Swap Protection](https://www.t-mobile.com/support/plans-features/help-with-t-mobile-account-fraud) | Via T-Mobile website or app |
| **Verizon** | [Number Lock](https://www.verizon.com/about/account-security/sim-swapping) | Via Verizon website or app |

If your carrier doesn't offer a dedicated feature, contact customer service to manually add a PIN to your account.

> [!warning] PIN Protection Limitations
> Account PINs aren't bulletproof—customer service representatives can sometimes override them. Use this as one layer of defense, not your only protection.

### Use Stronger Two-Factor Authentication Methods

Since SMS-based codes can be intercepted through SIM swapping, consider upgrading to more secure 2FA methods:

- **Authentication apps** (like Authy or Google Authenticator)
- **Hardware security keys** (like YubiKey)
- **Passkeys** (when available)

For detailed setup instructions, see the [FPF guide to two-factor authentication](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/2fa-beginners/).

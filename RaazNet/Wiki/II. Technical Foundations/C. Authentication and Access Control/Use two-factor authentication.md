# Use Two-Factor Authentication

Two-factor authentication (2FA), also called multi-factor authentication (MFA), adds a second layer of protection to your login process. To access an account, you need:

1. **Something you know** — your password or passphrase
2. **Something you own** — a hardware device, authenticator app, or phone to receive codes

By using 2FA, even if someone guesses your password, they cannot log in without the second factor. This second factor is harder to obtain because it's either generated by a physical device you own or sent to you at the moment you need it, and it's only valid once.

## Learn How to Set Up Two-Factor Authentication

To check which services offer 2FA and find instructions for enabling it, visit the [2FA Directory website](https://2fa.directory/).

**Prioritize enabling 2FA for:**
- Email accounts
- Banking and financial services
- Social media accounts
- Cloud storage services
- Password managers

## Use an Authenticator App or Hardware Device

The two most secure 2FA methods are:

- **Authenticator apps** — Applications that generate time-based one-time codes (e.g., Aegis, andOTP, Google Authenticator)
- **Hardware security keys** — Physical devices (also called security tokens, dongles, or FIDO U2F keys) that plug into your computer or phone, or connect wirelessly via NFC

## Choose the 2FA Method That Works Best for You

**Key considerations:**

- You can use one authenticator app or hardware device for multiple services
- Authenticator apps and hardware devices work offline; SMS and email 2FA require network connectivity
- **Security ranking (safest to least safe):**
  1. Hardware security key
  2. Authenticator app
  3. Email
  4. SMS

- SMS is the least secure option and may not work when traveling internationally or in areas without mobile coverage
- **Do not disable 2FA once enabled** — some services offer to turn it off temporarily for convenience, but this compromises your security

> **Why this matters:** When it comes to logins, multiple layers of protection are safer. If someone breaches your password, the second factor protects your account.

## Keep 2FA Backup Codes Safe and Separate

When you activate 2FA, ensure you have a backup method in case you lose your 2FA device.

**Backup options:**
- **Backup codes** — Most services provide a set of one-time codes when you enable 2FA
- **Alternative 2FA method** — Add a hardware key if you're using an authenticator app, or vice versa
- **QR code backup** — Screenshot the QR code during authenticator setup and store it securely

**Best practices for storing backup codes:**
- Save codes in your password manager as an attachment to the relevant account entry
- For additional security, create a separate password manager database on a different device
- Keep backup codes separate from other account credentials

> **Why this matters:** Backup codes are your way back into your account if you lose your 2FA device. They never expire, but anyone with access to them can use them to log in—so store them securely.

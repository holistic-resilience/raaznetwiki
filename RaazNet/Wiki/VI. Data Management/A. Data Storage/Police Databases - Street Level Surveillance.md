# Police Databases

Law enforcement agencies maintain extensive data repositories on individuals who have come into contact with the criminal justice system. These range from databases of criminal records (RAP sheets) to lists of alleged gang members. This data is frequently shared regionally, across states, and nationally through data switchboards operated by both government and non-government services. Many law enforcement agencies also purchase data from commercial data brokers catering to the public safety sector.

## Federal Law Enforcement Networks

The FBI's Criminal Justice Information Services (CJIS) operates at least [five distinct criminal justice information systems](https://www.justice.gov/tribal/national-crime-information-systems):

- **National Crime Information Center (NCIC)** - The most commonly accessed system by law enforcement officers
- **Next Generation Identification (NGI)** - Biometric identification system
- **National Data Exchange (N-DEx)** - Criminal justice information sharing
- **National Instant Criminal Background Check System (NICS)** - Background check system
- **Law Enforcement Enterprise Portal (LEEP)** - Connects to additional systems including RISSnet, National Gang Intelligence Center, and eGuardian

### National Crime Information Center (NCIC)

NCIC has been the United States' central database of criminal justice information [since 1967](https://www.fbi.gov/news/stories/ncic-turns-50), when it was founded during the J. Edgar Hoover administration. It contains:

- Criminal record history information
- Fugitive records
- Stolen property reports
- Missing persons data
- Other criminal justice data

CJIS has established security standards for all law enforcement agencies—including local and state agencies—that access or integrate data from these systems, covering everything from password complexity to vetting requirements for maintenance workers.

## Nlets: The National Data Switchboard

The [National Law Enforcement Telecommunications System](https://nlets.org/) (Nlets) serves as a switchboard for data across the United States. Notably, it is not a government agency but rather a non-profit organization with a board of directors composed of law enforcement officers.

**Key features:**
- State agencies, including motor vehicle departments, connect their databases to Nlets
- Enables data sharing across state lines
- Provides access to Canadian entities and Interpol
- Connects law enforcement to dozens of data systems

Nlets maintains a [public, interactive map](https://nlets.org/resources/maps/message-keys/key) showing which agencies can send or receive data from each source.

## State and Local Police Databases

### Statewide Networks

Most state governments maintain statewide networks, such as:

- **California**: California Law Enforcement Telecommunications System (CLETS)
- **Oklahoma**: Oklahoma Law Enforcement Telecommunications System (OLETS)
- **Florida**: Florida Crime Information Center (FCIC)

These systems typically provide access to state-level databases, connect data sources between local agencies, and share data with out-of-state agencies through Nlets.

### Regional and Local Systems

Local and regional law enforcement agencies also maintain their own systems. Examples include:

- **San Diego area**: SANDAG's Automated Regional Justice Information System (ARJIS)
- **Los Angeles**: LAPD's Network Communications System (NCS)

## Gang Databases

Gang databases identify people whom authorities have connected to gangs, often based on [unsubstantiated evidence](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/02/03/opinion/los-angeles-gang-database.html) such as:

- Associations with certain individuals
- Clothing choices
- Tattoos

In some cases, individuals have been entered into gang databases based on [falsified information](https://abc7.com/lapd-falsified-records-scandal-moore/6257995/).

### Critical Issues with Gang Databases

- Typically [unaudited](https://www.reuters.com/legal/government/dcs-gang-database-highlights-unconstitutional-systems-nationwide-2022-01-19/)
- No process for individuals to challenge their inclusion
- No clear mechanism to [petition for removal](https://voiceofsandiego.org/2019/12/09/few-people-have-successfully-removed-themselves-from-state-gang-database/)—even without evidence linking them to gang activity

### CalGang (California)

In California, activists have pushed local police departments to [dissociate from CalGang](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2020-06-24/california-police-urged-to-stop-using-gang-database-deemed-biased), the statewide gang database. In 2020:

- The database contained information on over 90,000 people
- LAPD [discontinued](https://www.mercurynews.com/2020/06/22/lapd-ending-use-of-calgang-database-after-2-decades/) its use of the database

In 2023, a San Diego city subcommittee [began studying](https://voiceofsandiego.org/2023/07/26/sdpds-use-of-gang-database-is-under-the-microscope/) SDPD's use of CalGang, prompting debate about civil rights concerns, arbitrary use, and staffing requirements.

### Chicago Gang Database

In 2023, Chicago planned to re-launch its gang database after its [previous iteration](https://www.chicagotribune.com/news/criminal-justice/ct-chicago-police-gang-database-update-20230106-cidgtynd4je4fc6x3rzioqgn3i-story.html) was criticized by the city's inspector general and became the subject of a federal lawsuit for being racially biased and unconstitutional.

The 134,000-person list had little oversight or accountability, and opponents claimed people on it "were at risk of severe sentencing, high bond, deportation or losing jobs because of it."

## Commercial Databases

Law enforcement agencies often purchase subscriptions to privately-managed intelligence databases that combine law enforcement data with commercially acquired information, including:

- Consumer records
- [Utility data](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2021/02/26/ice-private-utility-data/)
- Some systems claim to use AI to develop leads or connections between suspects

### Common Commercial Products

| Product | Provider |
|---------|----------|
| CLEAR | Thomson Reuters |
| Accurint | Lexis-Nexis |
| TLOxp | Transunion |
| CoplinkX | Various |

Police can also purchase access to:
- [Real-time location databases](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2022/08/fog-revealed-guided-tour-how-cops-can-browse-your-location-data)
- Face recognition databases
- Automated license plate reader databases

## Threats and Harms

### Database Misuse by Law Enforcement

Law enforcement personnel are regularly caught accessing criminal justice information inappropriately and illegally, according to research by the [Associated Press](https://apnews.com/general-news-699236946e3140659fff8a2362e16f43) and [EFF](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2018/06/clets-misuse-2017).

**Common misuse patterns include:**

- **Targeting women**: Officers using data for personal purposes, such as [finding dates in Bradenton, Florida](https://www.nytimes.com/2019/03/11/us/florida-cop-dating-women.html)
- **Domestic abuse**: Using databases as part of abuse patterns, such as [an incident in Akron, Ohio](https://www.cleveland.com/akron/2015/09/akron_police_officer_pleads_gu.html)
- **Assisting criminals**: Officers [providing information to gang members](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-2010-nov-14-la-me-lapd-gang-ties-20101115-story.html)
- **Adding false information**: [Falsifying records](https://www.justice.gov/usao-sdca/pr/former-supervisory-us-border-patrol-agent-sentenced-following-convictions-civil-rights) for retribution

### Interstate Data Sharing Risks

Interstate sharing of police data can threaten vulnerable populations:

- **Abortion access**: Prosecutors in states with abortion bans may attempt to access data from states where abortion is protected to identify individuals seeking or assisting with abortion services
- **Immigration enforcement**: Federal agencies like ICE may attempt to access databases to apprehend undocumented immigrants in "sanctuary" states

### Data Breach Risks

The more data a government agency collects, the greater the risk of a data breach. Recent examples include:

- [Leak of personal information](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/us-news/california-doj-data-breach-exposes-personal-information-concealed-carr-rcna35849) belonging to concealed carry permit holders in California
- "[Blueleaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BlueLeaks)" - massive dump of records from hacked agencies, including many fusion centers

### Gang Database Harms

Inclusion in a gang database can have serious repercussions:

- Increased stops, interrogations, and use of force by law enforcement
- Enhanced punishment in the criminal legal system
- Negative effects on employment prospects

**Racial disparities are severe**: In 2019, nearly 99% of the 18,000-person [New York City gang database were people of color](https://theappeal.org/spotlight-the-dangers-of-gang-databases-and-gang-policing/), with 88% being Black and Latine individuals.

Additionally, [ProPublica reported](https://www.propublica.org/article/cook-county-sheriffs-office-database-new-ban-law) that hundreds of people in Cook County's 25,000-entry gang database were actually deceased, demonstrating poor maintenance of these systems.

ICE has also used gang databases in deportation efforts, including instances when individuals have been [erroneously entered](https://theappeal.org/spotlight-the-dangers-of-gang-databases-and-gang-policing/) into these unregulated systems.

## EFF Advocacy and Reform Efforts

Since 2015, EFF has been [documenting abuse](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2015/11/misuse-rampant-oversight-lacking-californias-law-enforcement-network) of CLETS by California agencies.

### Key Accomplishments

- **Improved misuse reporting**: Successfully campaigned for CADOJ to improve how it collects misuse data
- **California Values Act**: Supported legislation banning federal agencies from accessing certain criminal justice data for immigration enforcement
- **Immigration enforcement classified as misuse**: Lobbied CADOJ to pass rules classifying [immigration enforcement as formal misuse](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2019/05/california-now-classifies-immigration-enforcement-misuse-statewide-law-enforcement), leading to ICE's Enforcement and Removal Operations [being removed from CLETS](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2019/12/california-doj-cuts-ice-deportation-officers-state-law-enforcement-database) in 2019
- **AB 1242**: [Supported legislation](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/governor-newsom-signs-assemblymember-bauer-kahan-and-attorney-general-bonta%E2%80%99s) signed by Gov. Gavin Newsom to prevent law enforcement from releasing data to out-of-state agencies investigating abortions
- **CalGang reform**: Joined a coalition calling for an [immediate moratorium](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2020/06/california-coalition-calls-moratorium-state-gang-database) on CalGang and [supported reform legislation](https://www.eff.org/es/deeplinks/2017/10/victory-california-just-reformed-its-gang-databases-and-made-them-more-accountable)
- **Predictive policing**: Advocated for a [ban on predictive policing algorithms](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2020/09/technology-cant-predict-crime-it-can-only-weaponize-proximity-policing)

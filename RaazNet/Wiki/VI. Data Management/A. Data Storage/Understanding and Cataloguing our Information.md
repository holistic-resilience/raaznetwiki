# Understanding and Cataloguing Your Information

To develop a complete picture of your security situation, you must treat information as a valuable asset. This guide helps you map your information, understand threats to its integrity, and develop strategies for protection.

## Types of Information We Handle

Our work generates and uses several categories of information:

- **Work Products**: Reports, databases, images, audio and video recordings
- **Operational Information**: Text messages, files, progress reports, office communications
- **Personal Information**: Data identifying us as organization members, professional affiliations
- **Metadata**: Data generated by device usage that can reveal movements and relationships

This information, if accessed by adversaries, can provide a comprehensive picture of our actions and relationships—making it extremely valuable and requiring careful protection.

## The Digital Security Challenge

As human rights defenders and activists have gained access to digital technologies, opponents have increased efforts to access devices and intercept communications. Two factors compound this challenge:

1. Many popular digital tools offer limited protection against well-resourced adversaries
2. Some adversaries may have collaborative relationships with software providers or online services

> **Tip**: Consider adding your technology providers to your actor map when assessing potential risks.

Building digital literacy is an empowering step in strengthening your security posture.

## Information States: At Rest vs. In Motion

Understanding how information exists helps identify vulnerabilities:

| State | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **At Rest** | Information that is primarily stationary/stored | Files in a cabinet, data on a hard drive |
| **In Motion** | Information that travels between locations | Text messages, emails, file transfers |

**Important**: Digital information often changes states. Cloud storage data is typically "at rest" but enters "motion" during uploads and downloads as it travels across the internet.

## Mapping Your Information Ecosystem

Creating an information map—whether as a document or spreadsheet—helps you view data as tangible assets rather than abstract masses. Update this map regularly.

### Key Questions for Each Information Type

**1. What information is it?**
- Group similar types together (e.g., all financial documents)
- Include software you use (some may be sensitive)
- Consider metadata in documents and communications

**2. Where does it reside?**
- Physical locations (offices, filing cabinets)
- Digital locations (devices, cloud services, email servers)

**3. Who can access it?**
- Authorized personnel
- Third-party services
- Potential adversaries

**4. How sensitive is it?**

| Classification | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Secret** | Only specific individuals should access |
| **Confidential** | Not public, but staff may access |
| **Public** | No risk from public exposure |

## Metadata Considerations

Pay attention to metadata in documents and communications (photos, emails, etc.). In some cases:
- Metadata can be removed or altered
- For emails, most metadata remains visible—use vague subject lines to reduce sensitivity

## Next Steps

After completing your information analysis, you should have:
- A clear overview of your sensitive information
- Understanding of who may have access
- Identification of gaps in your current security

Use these insights to build a strategy that addresses vulnerabilities in both information at rest and in motion.

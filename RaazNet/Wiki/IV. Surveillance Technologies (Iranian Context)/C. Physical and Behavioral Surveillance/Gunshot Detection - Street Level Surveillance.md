# Gunshot Detection

Acoustic gunshot detection is a system designed to detect, record, and locate the sound of gunfire and then alert law enforcement. The equipment usually takes the form of sensitive microphones and sensors, some of which must always be listening for the sound of gunshots. They are often accompanied by cameras and are usually mounted on street lights or other elevated structures, though some are mobile and others operate indoors.

## Overview and Purpose

Police and the companies that manufacture acoustic gunshot detection systems claim the technology informs police of the location of shots fired more quickly and accurately than relying on witnesses who may or may not call the police.

However, reports have [questioned the accuracy](https://www.macarthurjustice.org/shotspotter-generated-over-40000-dead-end-police-deployments-in-chicago-in-21-months-according-to-new-study/) of acoustic gunshot detection, including:
- Not registering some actual gunshots
- Erroneously registering loud noises like fireworks as gunshots

This can change police behavior and put people at risk by sending officers expecting gunfire to locations where there is no gunfire but innocent people are present.

**Critical Privacy Concern:** Gunshot detection systems can record human voices—and police have used these recordings as evidence in court. A device initially deployed to detect gunshots has been expanded to spy on conversations with sensitive microphones.

For reasons including accuracy concerns, fiscal cost, and community backlash, cities like Dayton, Ohio have [canceled their contracts](https://www.daytondailynews.com/local/dayton-not-renewing-contract-with-shotspotter-program/PYYGLLNVRJGUHEAF4OSJJ7K4NA/) for acoustic gunshot detection.

## How Acoustic Gunshot Detection Works

Acoustic gunshot detection relies on a series of sensors, often placed on lamp posts or buildings. The process works as follows:

1. **Detection**: Sensors detect loud noises
2. **Analysis**: The system attempts to determine whether the noise matches the specific acoustic signature of a gunshot
3. **Verification**: According to [ShotSpotter](https://www.shotspotter.com/technology/), human acoustic experts verify the automated system's match to confirm the sound is really gunfire (not a car backfire, firecrackers, or similar sounds)
4. **Alert**: If confirmed, the system sends the time and location to police
5. **Location Determination**: Calculated by measuring the time it takes for sound to reach sensors in different locations

## Threats and Concerns

### Accuracy Problems

There are grave concerns about acoustic gunshot detection accuracy, particularly false positives:

- A study by the [Chicago Inspector General found](https://igchicago.org/2021/08/24/oig-finds-that-shotspotter-alerts-rarely-lead-to-evidence-of-a-gun-related-crime-and-that-presence-of-the-technology-changes-police-behavior/) that **less than 10% of ShotSpotter alerts** resulted in evidence of a gun-related criminal offense
- In [one 2017 case](https://www.sfexaminer.com/news/courtroom-testimony-reveals-accuracy-of-sf-gunshot-sensors-a-marketing-ploy/), a ShotSpotter forensic analyst testified that sensors incorrectly placed a shooting's location a block away from the actual site
- When asked about the company's accuracy guarantee, the analyst stated: *"Our guarantee was put together by our sales and marketing department, not our engineers."*

> "At the end of the day this is a machine that basically can tell you that there was a loud sound and then a human has to tell you whether it was gunfire or not."
> — Dana Delger, Innocence Project Attorney ([Democrat & Chronicle](https://www.democratandchronicle.com/story/news/2017/11/17/shot-spotter-technology-relshot-spotter-technology-coming-under-increased-scrutiny-judicial-communit/844335001/))

### Wrongful Searches and Seizures

False positives can contribute to constitutional violations. Chicago's Office of the Inspector General [found a pattern](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2021/08/chicago-inspector-general-police-use-shotspotter-justify-illegal-stop-and-frisks) of CPD officers detaining and frisking civilians—a dangerous and humiliating intrusion on bodily autonomy and freedom of movement—based at least in part on "aggregate results of the ShotSpotter system."

### Discriminatory Deployment

Acoustic gunshot detection systems are often placed in what police consider "high-crime areas." As with many police surveillance systems, this can result in excessive scrutiny of neighborhoods where people of color live.

### Voice Recording and Privacy

This technology captures human voices at least some of the time. People in public places—for example, having a quiet conversation on a deserted street—often have a reasonable expectation of privacy and should be free from overhead microphones unexpectedly recording their conversations.

**Legal Cases on Voice Recording:**

| Case | Jurisdiction | Outcome |
|------|--------------|---------|
| *People v. Johnson* | California | Court allowed voice recording as evidence |
| *[Commonwealth v. Denison](https://www.mass.gov/files/documents/2018/01/08/CRIMINAL%20ESI%20DEC%202017.pdf)* | Massachusetts | Court ruled recording of "oral communication" is prohibited "interception" under the Massachusetts Wiretap Act |

## EFF's Position

[EFF has long raised concerns](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2021/07/its-time-police-stop-using-shotspotter) about acoustic gunshot detection systems, including their recording the conversations of unsuspecting people in public places.

In 2021, in light of reports discussing the [harmful effects of ShotSpotter alerts on policing](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2021/08/chicago-inspector-general-police-use-shotspotter-justify-illegal-stop-and-frisks) and questions about their accuracy, [EFF recommended](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2021/07/its-time-police-stop-using-shotspotter) that **cities not use the technology**.

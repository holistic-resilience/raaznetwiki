# Surveillance Camera Networks

## Overview

Surveillance camera networks have become ubiquitous in public and private spaces. This guide covers the types of cameras you may encounter, how law enforcement accesses and shares footage, the rise of real-time crime centers, and emerging privacy concerns.

## Recognizing Camera Types

Understanding the different types of surveillance cameras helps you identify what you're encountering in daily life. Note that some cameras may incorporate multiple features.

### Bullet Cameras

Bullet cameras are compact, tube-shaped devices with the camera, lens, and housing packaged in a cylindrical body. They face one direction and sometimes include infrared LEDs for low-light use. These cameras are deployed both indoors and outdoors.

### Dome Cameras

Dome cameras are typically ceiling-mounted for indoor surveillance. Their dome shape makes it difficult to determine which direction the camera faces. Optional features include:
- Infrared light for night vision
- Tamper-resistant housing
- Varifocal lenses for adjustable focal distance

### PTZ (Pan Tilt Zoom) Cameras

Unlike fixed cameras that face one direction, PTZ cameras are remotely controlled, allowing operators to:
- **Pan**: Move left or right
- **Tilt**: Move up and down
- **Zoom**: Focus closer or farther away

This enables tracking of individuals as they move through an area. PTZ cameras can also run automated patterns or turn to preset positions.

### Mobile Surveillance Towers and Poles

Law enforcement agencies deploy mobile surveillance units for situational awareness at public events and high-traffic areas. These include:
- **Manned towers**: Can be raised 25 feet in the air
- **Unmanned trailers**: Extend poles with PTZ cameras covering all directions

### Thermal Cameras

Thermal cameras capture movement based on body temperature rather than visible or infrared light. While not always distinguishable from conventional cameras, the brand name "FLIR" often indicates thermal imaging capability.

### Automated License Plate Readers (ALPR)

ALPR systems use computer-controlled cameras to capture:
- License plate numbers
- Vehicle photographs
- Sometimes images of drivers and passengers

Deployment types include:
- **Fixed cameras**: Mounted on traffic lights, telephone poles, or other infrastructure
- **Mobile cameras**: Attached to police patrol cars
- **Mobile trailers**: Repositionable to targeted locations

*For more information, see EFF's [Automated License Plate Readers guide](https://www.eff.org/pages/automated-license-plate-readers-alpr).*

## Data Sharing Practices

Surveillance cameras often broadcast footage over the internet for remote monitoring. Data sharing occurs through several mechanisms:

### Government-Operated Systems

Cities may pay for and operate their own surveillance systems, feeding footage directly to law enforcement.

### Private Camera Registration Programs

Many agencies run registration programs (often called "SafeCam") where residents and business owners provide information about their cameras and locations. When crimes are reported, police search their database and contact owners for footage. Examples include:
- Philadelphia Police Department (PA)
- Fultondale Police Department (AL)
- Phoenix Police Department's "Virtual Block Watch"

### Business Improvement District Networks

Private organizations sometimes build extensive camera networks that law enforcement can access. In San Francisco, the Union Square Alliance launched an outdoor camera program in 2012 with six cameras that has grown to over 450 cameras. Police regularly seek footage from this network and have conducted real-time monitoring during significant events, including 8 days of surveillance during the 2020 George Floyd protests.

## Real-Time Crime Centers (RTCCs)

Real-time crime centers—also called "domain awareness centers"—are monitoring headquarters where cities aggregate video surveillance feeds. The EFF's [Atlas of Surveillance project](https://atlasofsurveillance.org/real-time-crime-centers) has identified more than 80 RTCCs across the United States, concentrated in the South and Northeast.

### Notable Examples

**New Orleans**: Live feeds from over 300 street-facing cameras flow into a $5 million monitoring center created in 2017, monitored 24/7. The city continues expanding the network and has integrated private cameras and automated license plate readers.

**Birmingham, Alabama**: Nearly 100 surveillance cameras—24 PTZ cameras, 17 dome cameras, and 54 ALPRs—installed on 64 power poles feed into the Jefferson County Metro Area Crime Center. ALPR data uploads to the FBI's Criminal Justice Information Services database.

### Funding Sources

RTCC costs range from a few hundred thousand dollars to $11 million (New York City). Funding comes from:
- City budgets
- Voter-approved bonds
- State and federal grants
- Private institutions
- Individual donors

Some cities have scaled back due to costs. Fresno Police Department let go of part-time RTCC staff in 2019 and ceased real-time operations in 2020.

## Emerging Privacy Concerns

### Security Vulnerabilities

Internet-connected camera networks present security risks:
- **Ransomware attacks**: In 2017, hackers attacked two-thirds of D.C.'s police surveillance cameras days before the presidential inauguration, causing over 100 cameras to go dark.
- **Exposed ALPR systems**: In 2015, EFF discovered more than 100 ALPR cameras exposed online with open web pages, allowing anyone to view live video streams and plate captures.

### Face Recognition Integration

Combining surveillance cameras with face recognition technology raises serious privacy and civil rights concerns, particularly because face recognition is prone to error.

*See EFF's [Face Recognition guide](https://www.eff.org/pages/face-recognition) for more information.*

### Video Analytics

Beyond face recognition, sophisticated analytical software can track individuals based on:
- Clothing
- Glasses
- Facial hair

Products like BriefCam offer these capabilities and are deployed at real-time crime centers.

### Private Camera Integration

A significant emerging trend is law enforcement's ability to connect to private security cameras on homes and businesses. Companies like Fusus sell:
- Cloud-based platforms for creating real-time crime centers
- Hardware that converts private cameras into police-accessible surveillance feeds
- Integrated interfaces for multiple surveillance streams (predictive policing, gunshot detection, license plate readers, drones)

Cities including Atlanta, Memphis, and Orlando have adopted Fusus-fueled systems, with police actively encouraging public participation in these surveillance networks.

## Key Takeaways

The proliferation of surveillance cameras has rapidly reduced anonymity in public spaces. The combination of expanding camera networks, data sharing between public and private entities, real-time monitoring centers, and automated tracking technologies represents a significant and growing threat to privacy.

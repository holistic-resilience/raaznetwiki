# Protect Against Physical Threats

We do a lot of digital work to protect sensitive information, but that is only one aspect of information security. The work you do to protect your valuable devices and documents can be undone in an instant if your devices are lost, stolen, tampered with, confiscated, or damaged. Planning for physical security is as important as protecting your devices digitally.

Careful risk assessment, maintaining a safe work environment, and writing up a security policy can help you avoid physical disasters. Even if you are not working with a formal organization, it is a good idea to write out guidelines and response plans for yourself, your household, and the people you work with.

Both criminals and politically motivated attackers may have reasons to target your data. They might be seeking financial information, sensitive data related to your work, or personal details they can use to intimidate, blackmail, or impersonate you. You could also be targeted due to information received from others, such as contact lists, interviews, testimonies, or identifying information on victims or witnesses.

> **Important:** When planning your protection, always consider the well-being of all the people involved. See [Front Line Defenders' resources for well-being and stress management](https://www.frontlinedefenders.org/en/resources-wellbeing-stress-management).

While working on your physical protection, you will exchange and store sensitive information. Make sure to protect both [your sensitive information](https://securityinabox.org/en/files/secure-file-storage) and [the privacy of your online communications](https://securityinabox.org/en/communication/private-communication/).

## Low-Tech Security Measures

While it is important to budget for physical security, money is not the only way to achieve protection. Your actions, procedures, teamwork, preparation, planning, practice, time, and learning are all free of cost.

**Key principles:**

- Consider creative, low-tech protection solutions—a dog can be as effective as a surveillance camera
- Plan for multiple security measures; like threads in a rope, the more layers you have, the stronger your protection
- Each measure may be defeated or fail, but the combination makes you more protected

This approach is called "defense in depth"—using many different security measures that work together to create stronger overall protection.

## Creating a Physical Security Policy

### Working with Colleagues and Family

If you live with other people or share an office with another organization, talk to them about security. Determine what behaviors you can expect from one another and from visitors.

**Policy development steps:**

1. Get to know your neighbors—they may become allies or help monitor unusual activity
2. Set aside dedicated time to work on a policy document
3. Schedule regular reviews and updates to the policy
4. Plan regular security capacity-building exercises
5. Establish security briefing procedures and expectations about sharing information on incidents
6. Store and back up your policy documents for quick access
7. Plan how you will present the policy to new members

**Resources:**
- [Front Line Defenders' Workbook on Security](https://www.frontlinedefenders.org/en/resource-publication/workbook-security-practical-steps-human-rights-defenders-risk)
- [Access Now Digital Security Helpline's security policy templates](https://gitlab.com/AccessNowHelpline/helpline_documentation_resources/-/tree/master/templates/Organizational_Security_Policies-Templates)

### Emergency Communication Plan

Your plan should include:

- Network of allies and supporters who can assist in emergencies
- Emergency contacts and medical conditions of staff
- Contacts for fire, flood, or natural disasters
- Response procedures for burglary or office raids
- Steps to take if a device is lost or stolen
- Who to notify if sensitive information is disclosed or misplaced
- How to recover information from off-site backups
- How to perform important emergency repairs
- Contact information for utility providers (power, water, internet)

### What Goes in Your Plan?

Consider documenting:

- How your information might be lost or compromised, and consequences
- What devices you use and their physical locations
- Policy on personal devices used for work (BYOD)
- Communication channels and their usage
- How sensitive information is stored, including:
  - Whether data is encrypted
  - Backup locations
  - Who has access to decryption keys or passwords
- How you destroy sensitive data when no longer needed
- Travel security procedures
- Response plans for different emergency scenarios

> **Tip:** Any piece of information might be vulnerable in multiple ways. For example, files on a USB stick could be vulnerable to malware, but the stick could also be lost or stolen. Include each possibility in your plan.

## Office Access and Physical Layers

### Creating an Access Policy

Your policy should include rules about:

- Key distribution
- Monitoring systems (cameras, alarms)
- Procedures for deliveries, maintenance, and cleaning staff

### Physical Security Layers

Think of your physical security in layers and plan protection for each:

1. **Perimeter** (building exterior, grounds)
2. **Building access** (doors, windows, entry points)
3. **Internal areas** (restricted vs. public spaces)
4. **Device security** (individual equipment protection)

**Recommendations:**

- Decide which parts of your space should be restricted to visitors
- Arrange rooms for greater privacy and security
- Purchase devices from known, trusted vendors (Apple, Google, Samsung, Sony) to avoid pre-loaded malware and ensure security updates
- Keep printer, monitor, and projector ports out of reach or outside public areas to prevent unauthorized device connections

### Working Outside Your Home or Office

- Treat public wifi and internet cafes as insecure
- Carry your laptop in something that doesn't look like a laptop bag
- Keep devices near you at all times—don't leave them unattended while charging
- Consider traveling with a security cable for securing devices to fixed objects
- Remember that hotel safes are accessible to staff with master keys

## Surveillance and Monitoring

### Cameras and Motion Sensors

**Considerations:**

- Low-tech solutions (bells, alarms, dogs) can be as effective as cameras for fast alerts
- CCTV cameras require someone to monitor them or review footage
- Assess whether cameras could put people at risk if adversaries gained access to the footage
- Avoid "Internet of Things" devices like Amazon Ring—they are vulnerable to spying and may share footage with law enforcement without consent

**Recommended alternative:** [Haven](https://securityinabox.org/en/assess-plan/tools/#haven)—created specifically to help human rights defenders monitor their spaces while maintaining control over their data.

If using surveillance cameras:
- Transmit video encrypted to a location other than the one being monitored
- Keep footage encrypted in storage
- Plan for secure deletion and determine retention periods

## Protecting Your Local Network

### Wired vs. Wireless Networks

- Ethernet cables may be safer since wireless networks can be breached without physical access
- Avoid running ethernet cables outside your building or protected areas where they could be tampered with

### Wireless Network Security

- Use a [strong passphrase](https://securityinabox.org/en/passwords/passwords)
- [Set your network to use WPA2 or WPA3 security](https://securityinabox.org/en/phones-and-computers/malware/#secure-your-router)
- Change the default network name to something that doesn't identify you, your organization, or location
- Create a separate guest wifi network with its own strong password

### Network Infrastructure

- **Install a firewall router** between your network and the ISP's router to prevent ISP access to your network traffic and connected devices
- **Limit connected devices**—TVs, cameras, printers, game consoles, and IoT devices all carry security risks
- **Lock up networking equipment** (servers, routers, switches, modems) in a secure room or cabinet
- **Encrypt servers**—this protects files if equipment is seized or needs repair

## Preventing Accidents and Outages

Computers, networking equipment, and data storage devices are delicate. Electric fluctuations, extreme temperatures, dust, and moisture can all cause damage.

### Electrical Protection

| Protection Level | Equipment | Purpose |
|-----------------|-----------|---------|
| Basic | Grounded outlets | Prolongs equipment life, reduces failure risk |
| Standard | Surge protectors | Protects against voltage spikes |
| Enhanced | Power filters/line conditioners | For unstable electricity |
| Premium | UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply) | Stabilizes power, provides backup during outages |

**Tips:**
- Verify power strips contain actual surge protectors (check for joule rating)
- Position power equipment where it won't be accidentally unplugged
- Test power supply in new buildings before connecting important equipment
- Invest in quality cables—poor ones can damage devices and cause safety hazards

### Environmental Considerations

- Ensure adequate ventilation for computers, especially in cabinets
- Keep equipment away from radiators, heating vents, and air conditioners
- Consider battery-powered or solar lighting for power outages

## Protecting Against Theft, Tampering, and Confiscation

### Physical Locks

- Install high-quality locks on doors and windows
- Consider laptop safes or locking cabinets for sensitive documents and equipment
- Use device locks (security cables, Kensington locks)

> Locks on doors, gates, and windows act as outer layers of security—they help delay and detect intrusions, not only stop them.

### Device Placement

- Avoid placing important devices in easily accessible locations (hallways, reception areas, near windows or doors)
- Protect main electric switches with locks
- Keep servers and wifi routers in secured areas

### Leaving Devices Behind vs. Taking Them

Consider your specific situation:
- Is it more likely someone will raid your space while you're out?
- Or is it more likely you'll be detained and searched with devices on you?
- Are you crossing borders or attending protests?

**Tamper detection techniques:**
- Use security tape over USB ports and disk drives
- Scatter small objects over your device and photograph them before leaving
- Leave a thread or hair on the device (if no breeze) to detect movement
- Use a monitoring app like [Haven](https://securityinabox.org/en/assess-plan/tools/#haven)

### Device Registration

If your local law enforcement is trustworthy, registering device model and serial numbers may help recover stolen equipment.

## Visual Security

### Clean Desk Policy

Ensure you and colleagues do not leave sensitive information visible:
- Passwords
- Paper calendars and planners
- Journals and address books
- Sticky notes with sensitive information

### Screen Privacy

- Position screens so they cannot be seen from outside (account for windows, doors, waiting areas)
- Use [privacy screens](https://securityplanner.consumerreports.org/tool/use-a-privacy-filter-screen) when working in public
- Cover cameras on devices when not in use

### Location Privacy

If hiding your location, be aware that clues in video calls or photos can reveal where you are:
- Background objects and scenery
- Ambient sounds
- Visible addresses or landmarks

## Disposing of Sensitive Information

### Regular Secure Deletion

- Set a regular schedule to securely erase files
- Ensure sensitive information doesn't remain on devices, hard drives, USB sticks, SD cards, or phones
- Learn proper techniques in the [guide on destroying sensitive information](https://securityinabox.org/en/files/destroy-sensitive-information)

### Physical Destruction of Hard Drives

When disposing of hard drives:
- Use a power drill to destroy platters
- Drive nails through the drive
- Use strong hammer blows

**Do not:**
- Burn drives
- Pour acid on drives
- Microwave drives

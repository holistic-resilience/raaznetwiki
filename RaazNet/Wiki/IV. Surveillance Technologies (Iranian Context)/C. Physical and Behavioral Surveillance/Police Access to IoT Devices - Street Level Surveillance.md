# Police Access to IoT Devices

## Overview

The "internet of things" (IoT) refers to everyday devices—from refrigerators to robot vacuum cleaners—that connect to the internet and can be controlled via apps from anywhere in the world. While convenient, these devices create significant privacy concerns because they often store personal data on remote company servers rather than locally on the device itself.

This guide explains how law enforcement accesses IoT device data, the privacy threats this poses, and what you can do to protect yourself.

## The Privacy Problem

Many IoT devices collect and store data that can be accessed by multiple parties, including law enforcement. The most concerning are networked devices that capture video and audio from in and around our homes, sometimes in real-time.

Other IoT data of interest to police includes:
- **Thermostat data** — Can indicate whether you were home at a specific time
- **Fitness tracker data** — Records physical activity and location
- **Smart appliance logs** — Show usage patterns and presence in the home

Some IoT companies have made the problem worse by building police involvement directly into their infrastructure, creating specialized request forms that allow law enforcement to obtain customer data without a warrant or customer permission in "emergency" circumstances.

### The Scale of the Problem

- **As of 2019**: 69% of U.S. homes had at least one IoT device
- **As of January 2021**: 18% of U.S. homes have a surveillance doorbell

Networked doorbell cameras represent one of the largest surveillance systems in the country, with data potentially available to police from a majority of American homes.

## How IoT Devices Work

Consumer IoT devices connect to the internet in various ways:

- **Interconnected systems**: Devices like Echo or Alexa can control other devices (lights, television, etc.)
- **App-controlled devices**: Managed through smartphone applications
- **Voice-activated devices**: Microphones remain partially active, listening for command words

### Security Cameras and Doorbells

Devices like Amazon Ring doorbells with video and audio capabilities:

1. Are triggered by motion detection
2. Activate both camera and microphone to record the incident
3. Upload recorded data to remote company servers
4. Allow real-time viewing, listening, or speaking through the device via app

## How Police Access IoT Data

Law enforcement can obtain data from your IoT devices through several methods:

### 1. Direct Requests
Police may approach homeowners directly if they see a doorbell or security camera pointed at a crime scene. They can:
- Knock on your door and request footage in person
- Send emails (individually or en masse) requesting footage

**Important**: Without a warrant, you have the right to say "no" to these requests.

### 2. Warrants
With a warrant, police can:
- Present it to the device owner (if data is stored locally)
- Present it to the company (if data is stored on company servers)

Note: Some companies do not inform device owners when they turn over data to police.

### 3. Emergency Requests
Some companies have specialized forms allowing police to request data directly without a warrant or user consent in claimed "emergency" circumstances. Amazon Ring cameras, for example, have provided data to police through this method.

## Corporate Consolidation Concerns

IoT companies are frequently bought and sold, making it difficult to track who controls your data. A notable example is Amazon's attempted acquisition of iRobot (makers of Roomba vacuums), which raised concerns about:
- Amazon inheriting detailed maps of people's homes
- Integration with Amazon's existing surveillance products (Ring, Astro robot, indoor drones)

## Threats to Privacy

Police access to IoT data represents a significant expansion of government surveillance:

- **Massive evidence collection**: Without proper safeguards, unencrypted data from inside your home could be stored on company servers, effectively creating one of the largest police evidence lockers in history
- **Chilling effect on speech**: The ubiquity of these devices could make people uncomfortable having sensitive conversations in their own homes, yards, or neighborhoods
- **Warrant requirement erosion**: Emergency request procedures bypass traditional legal protections

## Protecting Your Privacy

### What EFF Has Achieved

EFF advocacy has resulted in several improvements:

1. **End-to-end encryption**: Ring introduced encryption for footage in early 2021, giving users more control over police access
2. **Mass email restrictions**: Ring stopped allowing police to send mass emails requesting footage from many customers at once (June 2021)
3. **Third-party tracker exposure**: EFF discovered and publicized that the Ring app contained undisclosed third-party trackers, pressuring the company to stop

### What You Can Do

- **Know your rights**: You can refuse warrantless requests for footage
- **Check encryption settings**: Enable end-to-end encryption when available
- **Review privacy policies**: Understand how your device manufacturer handles law enforcement requests
- **Consider local storage**: Some devices offer local storage options that keep data off company servers

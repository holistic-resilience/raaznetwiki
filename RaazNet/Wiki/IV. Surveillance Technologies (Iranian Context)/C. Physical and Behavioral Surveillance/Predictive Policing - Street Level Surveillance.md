# Predictive Policing

For well over a century, police have attempted to find ways to predict where, when, and by whom crime is going to be committed. In the 21st century, this ambition often takes the form of predictive algorithms that use historical data in an attempt to anticipate future events. Police often plug in events as disparate as traffic information, crime statistics and incident reports, or even the grades of school children. The algorithmic outputs often guide police procedure, patrols, and even who to put under increased surveillance.

But police cannot actually predict crime, with or without a computer. Nobody can see the future. What police can do, however, is make assumptions about who and where crime is most likely to occur and put those places and people under intense scrutiny. Such heightened surveillance can lead to increased traffic and sidewalk stops, documentation of a person's activities over time, and even use of force.

## The Self-Fulfilling Prophecy Problem

Predictive policing is often a self-fulfilling prophecy. If police focus their efforts in one neighborhood and arrest dozens of people there during the span of a week, the data may reflect that area as a hotbed of criminal activity, leading the algorithm to deploy more officers there the following week. The system also considers only reported crime, so neighborhoods where the police are called more often might see predictive policing technology concentrate resources there.

This system can aggravate existing patterns of policing—especially in neighborhoods with concentrations of people of color, unhoused individuals, and immigrants—by using the cloak of scientific legitimacy and the supposed unbiased nature of data.

## How It Works

Predictive policing programs generally work by feeding crime data, and sometimes massive amounts of other data, into an algorithm which tries to look for unseen patterns that can reveal where and when crimes are most likely to take place. That information can include:

- Locations of all crimes recorded by police
- Time at which crimes were committed
- Weather conditions
- Traffic patterns

The predictive model can shape deployment of patrols, often displayed as "heat maps" showing city blocks that supposedly will have higher-than-normal crime levels.

### Person-Based Prediction

In some cases, programs like these can focus on individuals. In Pasco County, Florida, police assigned a point system to individuals based on factors like a family's income bracket or grades in order to assign a likelihood that a child would commit crimes—and then harass them and their family.

## How Law Enforcement Uses It

Law enforcement often claims that predictive policing is a "force multiplier," something that allows departments to allocate officers and resources more strategically. Police feed data into software which identifies patterns that dictate where they should deploy officers to prevent criminal activity. This typically results in "cops on the dots" deployment of officers to identified blocks.

## Vendors and Market

**PredPol (now Geolitica)** was an early predictive policing vendor, beginning a pilot program in the Santa Cruz Police Department in 2011. By 2020, some police departments began canceling their contracts amid public concerns over civil liberties:

- Los Angeles ended its subscription
- Santa Cruz banned predictive policing algorithms entirely
- New Orleans banned the technology (though later rolled back these protections)

**Palantir** was reported in 2018 to be testing a predictive policing system in New Orleans without the knowledge of elected officials.

**Banjo** briefly held a multi-million dollar contract with Utah but was found by a government audit to be incapable of providing the services it offered and was given access to state databases containing sensitive information.

**ShotSpotter**, known for acoustic gunshot detection, acquired HunchLab in 2018 to predict where gunshots might happen in the future.

Some departments work with vendors to build their own tools, like the NYPD/Microsoft Domain Awareness Systems.

## Threats to Civil Liberties

Digital predictive policing poses significant civil liberties threats:

### Constitutional Concerns
No one should have their constitutional rights abated because they live in or walk through areas where an algorithm has predicted there will be crime.

### Data Quality Issues
There are major concerns about the type of data being fed into these programs. Sensitive personal data about individuals is increasingly being used, and police-generated data is inherently skewed.

### Racial Bias Amplification
If police put majority Black neighborhoods under intense scrutiny and surveillance, they will be more likely to make stops and arrests for minor quality-of-life infractions. Derivative maps then disproportionately weigh those neighborhoods already living under intense police presence. This creates a self-fulfilling prophecy that uses the supposed objectivity of math to legitimize racially biased police procedures.

### Dubious Claims
Police agencies and vendors claim they can train machine learning algorithms to predict whether a person is likely to commit crimes, including by analyzing physical attributes of a person's face—a particularly dubious and dangerous claim.

## EFF's Position

Because of the risk predictive policing poses to civil liberties and vulnerable populations, EFF advocates for bans on predictive policing. EFF supports lawmakers who want to take a more active role in probing predictive policing vendors and police departments, and will continue to support attempts at the town, state, and national level to rein in this dangerous technology.

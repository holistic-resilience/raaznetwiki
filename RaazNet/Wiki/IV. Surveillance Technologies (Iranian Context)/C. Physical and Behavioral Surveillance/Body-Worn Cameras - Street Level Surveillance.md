# Body-Worn Cameras

## How Body-Worn Cameras Work

Body-worn cameras are small devices that can be clipped onto a police officer's uniform or worn as a headset. When activated, they record video and audio of law enforcement encounters with the public. The footage is typically saved with time and date stamps and GPS coordinates.

### Key Features and Capabilities

- **Real-time streaming**: Some body cameras offer live video streaming capabilities
- **Automatic activation triggers**: Agencies can configure cameras to automatically turn on when:
  - A cruiser's sirens are activated
  - The vehicle exceeds a certain speed
  - Nearby dashboard or body cameras are switched on
  - A gun is drawn from a wireless holster sensor
- **Bluetooth connectivity**: Some models support trigger options for automatic recording
- **Facial recognition**: At least one company has been developing body-worn cameras with live facial recognition capabilities

Footage is uploaded to external databases maintained by police agencies or third-party vendors.

## Data Collection

When police activate body-worn cameras, they collect:

- **Video footage** of people in the officer's vicinity
- **Audio recordings** of conversations and ambient sound
- **Date and time stamps**
- **GPS coordinates**
- **Facial data** that can be analyzed by face recognition technology

## Law Enforcement Usage

### Deployment Scale

Many states mandate statewide use of body-worn cameras by law enforcement officers interacting with the public, though several have phased implementation. EFF's Atlas of Surveillance has catalogued **more than 4,517 police departments** using body-worn cameras nationwide.

### Common Recording Scenarios

Police can collect video footage during:
- Stop and frisk encounters
- Property searches
- Car stops or chases
- Witness interviews
- Issuance of summons
- Arrests

### Policy Variations

The Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights issued a detailed scorecard comparing body-worn camera practices across the country. Key findings from their 2017 data:

- **52 of 68 police departments** in major cities have body-worn camera programs with policies in place
- **49 departments** make their policies available on department websites
- **4 departments** (Cincinnati, Las Vegas, Parker, Colorado, and Washington, D.C.) provide recorded individuals access to all footage
- **12 departments** prohibit officers from reviewing footage before filing initial written incident reports for critical incidents like officer shootings
- **7 departments** (Baltimore, Baltimore County, Boston, Cincinnati, Montgomery County, Parker, and Seattle) limit the use of biometric technologies with camera footage

## Vendors and Technology Providers

### Major Manufacturers

Many police departments use body-worn cameras made by **Axon** (formerly Taser), which provides free cameras and sells data storage services. Other vendors include:

Aventura, Black Mamba, BrickHouse Security, Brimtek, COBAN, Data911, DEI, Digital Ally, FlyWIRE, Global Justice, GoPro, HauteSpot, HD Protech, Kustom Signals, L-3 Mobile-Vision, Law Systems, Marantz Professional, Martel, Motorola, Panasonic, Patrol Eyes, Paul Conway, Pinnacle, PRG, Primal USA, PRO-VISION, Reveal Media, Safety Innovations, Safety Vision, Titan, Utility Inc, VIEVU, VP360, WatchGuard, Wolfcom, Zepcam, and Zetronix.

### Data Storage Services

In addition to selling body cameras, some vendors also provide data storage for footage:
- **VIEVU** owns the cloud-based service VERIPATROL
- **Axon** owns the cloud-based service Evidence.com
- **Utility Inc.** and **Motorola** partner with Amazon Web Services for storage

### Contractual Concerns

Some body-worn camera providers, such as Axon, require police departments to sign contracts permitting footage to be stored in proprietary systems. Defense attorneys may have to agree with restrictive terms of service to access documents produced in judicially mandated discovery.

## Privacy and Civil Liberties Threats

Body-worn cameras pose significant threats to privacy, protest rights, and racial justice. They have the potential to massively expand government surveillance capabilities.

### Primary Concerns

**Mass Surveillance Expansion**
- Thousands of hours of footage can be algorithmically analyzed
- Video can be converted into searchable metadata databases
- Footage may be kept indefinitely
- Can be combined with other surveillance technologies like stationary pole cameras

**Disproportionate Impact**
Given ongoing inequities in the criminal justice system, body-worn cameras disproportionately impact:
- People of color
- Immigrants
- Other vulnerable groups

This may discourage people from seeking assistance from officers, further undermining community trust in law enforcement.

**Chilling Effect on Free Speech**
Police use of body-worn cameras at protests could discourage people from exercising their First Amendment rights.

### Privacy Violations in Private Spaces

When police enter a citizen's home with a body-worn camera, they may capture:
- Innocent private domestic activity
- Children
- People in various states of undress
- Victims of domestic violence or sexual assault

Some departments have policies protecting vulnerable individuals from being recorded without informed consent, while others offer only vague guidance or no protections at all.

### Data Security and Retention Issues

- Footage can be saved for years, creating risks of data breaches and employee misuse
- Retention policies should balance security concerns with the need for victims and the public to access videos involving use of force, misconduct, or complaints
- Police often have immediate access to video while recorded civilians and their attorneys cannot

### Selective Release and Manipulation

Police often have discretion to release portions of video selectively as a public relations strategy. Examples include:

- **Ferguson case**: After Michael Brown was fatally shot, police released surveillance video of an earlier, unrelated incident to shape public perception
- **Chicago case**: The Chicago Police Department delayed release of Laquan McDonald shooting footage for 13 months

### Officer Discretion Problems

When police can decide when to record and when to turn off cameras, this circumvents the oversight cameras are meant to provide:

- Officers often neglect to activate cameras even when required
- Compliance with recording rules can be low
- Failures to record during use-of-force incidents don't always result in disciplinary action
- Albuquerque's Civilian Police Oversight Agency found officers violated body-worn camera recording rules more than any other policy

### Pre-Report Viewing Concerns

Many departments allow officers to review footage before making initial statements to investigators. This enables:
- Tailoring stories to match video evidence
- Potential for officers to lie in ways consistent with the video
- "Fishing expeditions" to search for additional crimes not previously detected

## EFF's Recommended Safeguards

EFF has consistently opposed body-worn cameras absent strict rules to ensure they protect privacy and advance police accountability:

### Mandated Activation
Officers must turn cameras on at the start of all investigative encounters with civilians and leave them on until the encounter ends.

### No Political Spying
Officers must not use cameras to gather information about people exercising their First Amendment rights.

### Retention of Footage
Footage must be stored for a few months so injured civilians have time to request it, then promptly destroyed to prevent long-term surveillance.

### No Face Surveillance
Facial recognition technology is too dangerous for police to use, especially in connection with body-worn cameras.

### Officer Review Restrictions
If footage depicts use of force or an episode subject to civilian complaint, the officer must make an initial statement before reviewing the footage.

### Public Access to Footage
- If footage depicts a person, they must have access
- If it depicts force, everyone must have access
- For other types of police misconduct, a court must weigh privacy costs against accountability benefits

### Rigorous Enforcement
- Footage collected in violation of rules must be excluded from court
- Missing footage should create an evidentiary presumption that it would have helped the civilian

### Community Control
Police must not deploy body-worn cameras unless the city council votes to approve them after community input. Many communities will say "no."

## EFF's Advocacy Work

EFF has engaged in extensive advocacy on body-worn camera policies:

- **2015**: Opposed LAPD's policies for failing to adopt necessary safeguards about camera activation, public access, officer misuse, and face recognition
- **2017**: Sent letter to Santa Clara County Board with suggestions to improve their proposed Sheriff's body-worn camera policy (jointly with ACLU of California and CAIR Bay Area)
- **2020**: Recommended safeguards to the federal Justice in Policing Act's body-worn camera section
- **California A.B. 1215**: Supported law placing a three-year moratorium on face surveillance and biometric surveillance on body-worn camera footage
- **San Diego**: Successfully enforced the moratorium against the city's face recognition program

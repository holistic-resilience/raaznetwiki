# Automated License Plate Readers (ALPRs)

Automated license plate readers (ALPRs) are high-speed, computer-controlled camera systems typically mounted on street poles, streetlights, highway overpasses, mobile trailers, or police squad cars. ALPRs automatically capture all license plate numbers that come into view, along with the location, date, and time. The data, which includes photographs of the vehicle and sometimes its driver and passengers, is then uploaded to a central server.

Vendors claim this information can be used by police to:
- Determine where a plate has been in the past
- Identify if a vehicle was at the scene of a crime
- Discover travel patterns
- Find vehicles that may be associated with each other

Law enforcement agencies can share their information with thousands of other agencies.

> **Privacy Concern**: Taken in aggregate, ALPR data can paint an intimate portrait of a driver's life and chill First Amendment protected activity. ALPR technology can target drivers who visit sensitive places such as health centers, immigration clinics, gun shops, union halls, protests, or centers of religious worship.

Since the government requires all drivers to display license plates in public view, drivers have no control over whether their vehicle is tracked. This makes it particularly disturbing that ALPRs are used to track millions of ordinary people, even though the overwhelming majority are not connected to any crime.

## How ALPR Systems Work

Automated license plate readers can be broadly divided into three categories.

### Stationary (Fixed) ALPR Cameras

Stationary ALPRs are installed in fixed locations such as:
- Traffic lights
- Telephone poles
- Facility entrances
- Freeway exit ramps

These cameras generally capture only vehicles in motion that pass within view.

**Capabilities of networked fixed cameras:**
- Reveal direction and speed of travel
- Enable real-time vehicle tracking with sufficient camera density
- Show every time a plate has passed a given location (when data is stored)
- Allow inference about where a driver lives or works

Smaller municipalities often install ALPR cameras at town entrances and exits, creating a "virtual gated community" where no one can visit without being documented. Police sometimes disguise ALPRs as mundane objects like traffic cones or cacti.

ALPR cameras are often used in conjunction with:
- Automated red-light enforcement
- Speed enforcement systems
- Toll assessment on roads and bridges

### Mobile ALPR Cameras

Mobile ALPRs are attached to police patrol cars, allowing officers to capture license plate data while driving throughout their shifts. These cameras are typically turned on at shift start and remain active until shift end.

**Key characteristics:**
- Capture images of passing vehicles and parked cars
- Can scan hundreds of plates in minutes in parking lots
- Enable "gridding" — systematically driving every block to capture resident data
- Private vendors like Vigilant Solutions also collect and sell plate data

### ALPR Trailers

ALPR trailers are portable units that police can tow to specific areas and leave for extended periods. They collect data similarly to fixed ALPRs without requiring permanent installation.

**Notable uses:**
- The Drug Enforcement Administration has used these disguised as speed enforcement trailers near the U.S.-Mexico border
- Police have parked them near gun shows and political rallies

## ALPR Databases

Most ALPR data is stored in databases for extended periods—often up to five years. These databases may be maintained by:
- Police departments directly
- Private companies like Vigilant Solutions or Flock Safety

Law enforcement agencies without their own ALPR systems can access data through:
- Regional sharing systems
- Networks operated by private companies
- Commercial databases (available on subscription)

Several companies operate independent databases by contracting with drivers to put cameras on private vehicles. This data is sold to insurers and law enforcement alike.

### Hotlists

Law enforcement agencies pre-load lists of license plates the ALPR system actively seeks, including:
- Stolen vehicles
- Vehicles associated with outstanding warrants
- Custom officer-created lists

When an ALPR scans a plate on the list, the system alerts the officer or agency. Some hotlists include low-level misdemeanors and traffic offenses, and some agencies use them to generate revenue by stopping citation scofflaws.

## What Data ALPRs Collect

**Primary data collected:**
- License plate numbers
- Location data
- Exact date and time
- Vehicle make and model (some systems)

**Collection capacity:**
- Thousands of plates per minute
- One vendor claims over 6.5 billion scans
- Growth rate of 120 million data points monthly

**Derived intelligence from combined data:**
- Direction and speed of travel (through triangulation)
- Historical travel patterns
- Predicted future locations (with algorithms)
- All visitors to particular locations

**Photographic data may include:**
- The vehicle itself
- Drivers and passengers
- Immediate surroundings
- People entering and exiting vehicles

Some products create "vehicle fingerprints" including color, make, model, physical damage, and bumper stickers.

## How Law Enforcement Uses ALPR Technology

ALPR data is gathered indiscriminately on millions of ordinary people. By plotting vehicle times and locations, police can paint a specific portrait of drivers' lives and potentially predict future behavior—despite most people never being accused of a crime.

> **Key Finding**: A 2021 EFF report found that according to data from 63 California law enforcement agencies, only **0.05%** of ALPR data was relevant to public safety at the time of capture.

### Real-Time Investigations

Officers use ALPR to automatically identify or track vehicles in real time by adding plates to "hot lists" when:
- A vehicle is stolen
- A vehicle is associated with an outstanding warrant
- A vehicle was seen at a crime scene
- The owner is a crime suspect
- A vehicle is believed gang-associated

### Historical Investigations

Since ALPRs collect data on everyone, officers can search historical data using:
- A plate or partial plate number
- A physical address

**Example use**: An officer may enter a convenience store's location to identify vehicles nearby during a robbery, then look up where those plates have been captured elsewhere.

**Important note**: Training materials and policies in some jurisdictions state that a hot-list alert alone may not warrant a stop. Officers should visually confirm plate matches. Failure to confirm, combined with machine error, has caused wrongful stops.

### Data Retention

Retention periods vary by agency:
- Some retain data for mere days
- Others retain for several years
- Some entities—including private companies—retain data indefinitely

## Major ALPR Vendors

**Primary U.S. vendors:**
- **Vigilant Solutions** (Motorola Solutions subsidiary) — offers access to privately collected data through Digital Recognition Network
- **Flock Safety** — partners with homeowners associations to provide data to law enforcement

**Other vendors:**
- Rekor
- Elsag
- Axon
- Perceptics
- Jenoptik

Both Vigilant and Flock offer law enforcement the ability to share data nationally.

## Threats Posed by ALPR

ALPR is a powerful surveillance technology that can invade individual privacy and violate the rights of entire communities.

### Documented Abuses

**Targeting communities:**
- New York police electronically recorded plates of everyone parked near a mosque
- Birmingham police targeted a Muslim community while misleading the public
- Oakland Police Department data showed disproportionate ALPR deployment in low-income communities and communities of color

**Individual officer abuse:**
- 1998: A Washington, D.C. officer pleaded guilty to extortion after looking up plates near a gay bar and blackmailing vehicle owners
- 2022: A Kechi, Kansas officer was arrested for accessing Flock Safety ALPR to stalk his estranged wife

### Misidentification Incidents

**San Francisco (2009):** Denise Green, an African-American city worker, was handcuffed at gunpoint, forced to her knees, and searched due to a license plate reader error misidentifying her car as stolen. The U.S. Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals ruled that technology alone cannot be the basis for such stops—but this ruling doesn't apply everywhere.

**Aurora, Colorado:** Black youths were traumatized by police after an ALPR incorrectly identified their vehicle as stolen.

### Systemic Risks

- Aggregate data stored long-term becomes more invasive and revealing
- Data is susceptible to misuse and breaches
- Even Customs & Border Protection's vendor (Perceptics) was hacked, with data published online

**Vulnerable populations:**
- Immigrant communities
- People seeking or providing reproductive health services

### Recommended Protections

- Sensible retention limits
- Specific policies about data access within agencies
- Audit and control processes
- Ideally, no data retention when a passing vehicle doesn't match a hot list

## EFF's Work on ALPR

EFF has investigated and combated ALPR privacy threats through public records requests, litigation, and legislative advocacy since 2012.

### Litigation

**ACLU of Southern California and EFF v. LAPD and LASD**
EFF and ACLU sued after agencies refused to release ALPR data, claiming records were exempt as "investigative records." In 2017, the California Supreme Court ruled in favor of EFF and ACLU.

**Lagleva v. Marin County Sheriff (2021)**
EFF and ACLU sued on behalf of local activists after the sheriff shared ALPR data with out-of-state agencies, including ICE, violating state laws. The settlement required the sheriff to cease sharing data outside California.

**Other cases:**
- Filed briefs challenging excessive ALPR data storage in Virginia
- Filed briefs in Massachusetts and Nevada cases

### Accountability and Transparency

**California S.B. 34 (2015)** requires ALPR users to:
- Protect data
- Maintain access logs
- Hold public meetings before starting ALPR programs
- Implement usage and privacy policies
- Prohibits selling, sharing, or transferring data except to other public agencies

**EFF initiatives:**
- Coordinated volunteers to collect ALPR policies across California
- Exposed agencies failing to comply with the law
- Filed public records requests through Data Driven and Data Driven 2 projects
- Successfully advocated for a 2019 California Legislature audit

The California State Auditor issued a report substantiating EFF's concerns that agencies weren't following the law, failed to enact adequate policies, and shared data too broadly.

# Police Databases

Law enforcement agencies maintain extensive data repositories on individuals who have come into contact with the criminal justice system. These range from RAP sheet databases to alleged gang member lists. This data is frequently shared regionally, across states, and nationally through government and non-government data switchboards. Many agencies also purchase data from commercial brokers catering to the public safety sector.

## Federal Law Enforcement Networks

The FBI's Criminal Justice Information Services (CJIS) operates at least five distinct criminal justice information systems:

- **National Crime Information Center (NCIC)** - The most commonly accessed system by law enforcement officers
- **Next Generation Identification (NGI)** - Biometric identification system
- **National Data Exchange (N-DEx)** - Criminal justice information sharing
- **National Instant Criminal Background Check System (NICS)** - Firearm purchase background checks
- **Law Enforcement Enterprise Portal (LEEP)** - Connects to RISSnet, National Gang Intelligence Center, and eGuardian

### NCIC Overview

NCIC has been the United States' central database of criminal justice information since 1967, when it was founded during the J. Edgar Hoover administration. It contains:

- Criminal record history information
- Fugitive data
- Stolen property records
- Missing persons information

CJIS establishes security standards for all law enforcement agencies accessing these systems, including requirements for password complexity and personnel vetting.

## Nlets (National Law Enforcement Telecommunications System)

Nlets serves as a data switchboard across the United States. It is not a government agency but rather a non-profit organization with a board of directors composed of law enforcement officers.

**Key characteristics:**
- State agencies (including motor vehicle departments) connect their databases to Nlets
- Enables data sharing across state lines
- Provides access to Canadian entities and Interpol
- Maintains a [public, interactive map](https://nlets.org/resources/maps/message-keys/key) showing which agencies can send or receive data from each source

## State and Local Police Databases

### State Networks

Most state governments maintain statewide networks, such as:

- **California**: California Law Enforcement Telecommunications System (CLETS)
- **Oklahoma**: Oklahoma Law Enforcement Telecommunications System (OLETS)
- **Florida**: Florida Crime Information Center (FCIC)

These systems provide access to state-level databases, connect local law enforcement agencies, and share data with out-of-state agencies through Nlets.

### Local and Regional Systems

Local agencies maintain their own systems, including:

- **San Diego area**: Automated Regional Justice Information System (ARJIS) operated by SANDAG
- **Los Angeles**: Network Communications System (NCS) operated by LAPD

## Threats and Harms

### Database Misuse by Personnel

Law enforcement personnel regularly access criminal justice information inappropriately and illegally:

- **Targeting women**: Officers have used official data to pursue romantic interests or facilitate domestic abuse
- **Assisting criminals**: Cases of officers providing database access to criminal associates
- **Falsifying records**: Adding false information to exact retribution

### Interstate Data Sharing Risks

- Prosecutors in states with abortion bans may attempt to access data from states where abortion is protected
- Federal agencies like ICE may access databases to apprehend undocumented immigrants in sanctuary states

### Data Breach Vulnerabilities

The more data collected, the greater the breach risk. Examples include:

- **2022**: Leak of personal information belonging to California concealed carry permit holders
- **2020**: "Blueleaks" - massive dump of records from hacked agencies including fusion centers

### EFF Advocacy

Since 2015, EFF has documented CLETS abuse by California agencies and successfully:

- Campaigned for improved misuse data collection by California DOJ
- Supported the California Values Act banning federal access to certain criminal justice data for immigration enforcement
- Lobbied for rules classifying immigration enforcement as formal misuse of data
- Contributed to ICE's Enforcement and Removal Operations being removed from CLETS in 2019
- Supported AB 1242 to prevent law enforcement from releasing data to out-of-state agencies investigating abortions

## Gang Databases

Gang databases identify people authorities have connected to gangs, often based on unsubstantiated evidence such as:

- Personal associations
- Clothing choices
- Tattoos

### Key Problems

- Individuals entered based on falsified information
- Typically unaudited
- No mechanism to challenge inclusion or petition for removal
- No evidence requirement for listing

### CalGang (California)

In 2020, CalGang contained information on over 90,000 people. Recent developments:

- **2020**: LAPD discontinued use of the database
- **2023**: San Diego city subcommittee began studying SDPD's use of CalGang amid civil rights concerns

### Chicago Gang Database

Chicago's previous gang database iteration:

- Contained 134,000 people
- Criticized by city inspector general
- Subject of federal lawsuit for racial bias
- Individuals faced severe sentencing, high bond, deportation, and job loss

### Threats and Harms from Gang Databases

**Consequences of inclusion:**
- Stops, interrogations, and use of force by law enforcement
- Enhanced punishment in the criminal legal system
- Negative effects on employment prospects

**Racial disparities:**
- In 2019, nearly 99% of New York City's 18,000-person gang database were people of color
- 88% were Black and Latine

**Additional issues:**
- Poor maintenance (Cook County's 25,000-entry database included hundreds of deceased individuals)
- ICE uses gang databases for deportation, including erroneously entered individuals

**EFF's position**: EFF supports abolition of CalGang and joined a coalition calling for an immediate moratorium on its use in 2020.

## Commercial Databases

Law enforcement agencies purchase subscriptions to privately-managed intelligence databases that combine law enforcement data with commercially acquired information, including consumer records and utility data.

### Common Products

- **Thomson Reuters CLEAR**
- **Lexis-Nexis Accurint**
- **Transunion TLOxp**
- **CoplinkX**

### Additional Commercial Access

Police can purchase access to:
- Real-time location databases
- Face recognition databases
- Automated license plate reader databases

## Related Concerns

Like gang databases, **predictive policing algorithms** use accumulated or questionable information to label individuals as potential threats, making them vulnerable to police harassment and surveillance. EFF advocates for a ban on predictive policing algorithms.

# Social Media Monitoring

## How It Works

Social media contains some of the most intimate details of our lives, including health information, likes and dislikes, political views and religious beliefs, sexual orientation, and people with whom we associate. The content includes:

- Usernames, bios, and contact information
- Status updates, comments, photos, videos, and streams
- Event postings and group memberships
- Friend or follower lists and friend requests
- Private messages
- Account creation dates and location data

### Federal Immigration Surveillance

Law enforcement agencies including the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) and U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) monitor the social media posts of:

- Asylum seekers
- Tourists seeking visa waivers
- People applying for immigration benefits

This surveillance extends to anyone in these people's networks. Social media information is collected from travelers—including American residents—even when they are not suspected of any illegal activity. DHS has offered no transparency about its multi-million dollar program of spying on immigrants' and other foreign visitors' social media posts, which it uses as evidence in deportations and visa denials.

### Local Law Enforcement Surveillance

Federal, state, and local law enforcement agencies all monitor social media accounts. While this has typically been done to surveil political activists, it has sometimes been much broader.

**Example:** According to [an internal July 2020 memo](https://www.brennancenter.org/sites/default/files/2021-09/I.%20Beck%20FI%20Memo.pdf) obtained by the Brennan Center, LAPD officers were ordered to [note the social media information](https://www.brennancenter.org/sites/default/files/2021-09/A%20Series_LAPD%20Social%20Media%20Monitoring%20FOIA_2021.pdf) (including Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, and other accounts) of every civilian they questioned—including those who were not arrested or even accused of a crime.

Police may combine social media monitoring with other technologies, such as face recognition, to identify individuals. Since social media posts are highly contextual, police may misinterpret innocuous language, song lyrics, or inside jokes, leading to criminal consequences for individuals who may not have even been aware they were under surveillance.

## How Law Enforcement Monitors Social Media

### Manual Monitoring Methods

Police may use fake accounts to:

- Search through publicly available posts for names of groups or individuals
- Search for specific keywords and hashtags
- Scour social media pages and groups to determine names and affiliations
- Identify the time of planned events and other information

### Common Surveillance Purposes

- Monitoring asylum seekers and tourists
- Gathering information about planned protests or activities
- Identifying protesters and protest organizers
- Data mining to learn identities and affiliations of people engaged in legally protected conduct
- Searching for perceived threats to national security

### Legal Requests for Data

When police want additional information that's not publicly available or would take too long to scrape, they may use a subpoena or warrant to request social media data as part of a criminal investigation.

### Fake and Imposter Accounts

Police officers have also set up fake or imposter accounts:

- **DEA (2010):** An agent [created a phony Facebook account](https://www.buzzfeednews.com/article/chrishamby/government-says-federal-agents-can-impersonate-woman-online) in the name of a young woman without her knowledge or consent, posting photos from her seized materials—including pictures of her son and niece, who were young children—and sending friend requests from this imposter account.

- **DHS/ICE:** Created fake profiles and pages to trick immigrants into registering with a fake college called [The University of Farmington](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2020/dec/05/ice-fake-university-farmington-inside-story), leading to more than 170 arrests.

- **Memphis Police Department:** [Created fake profiles](https://theappeal.org/memphis-police-collected-black-lives-matter-activists-private-facebook-posts/) to monitor Black Lives Matter activists.

### Automated Monitoring Tools

Police use social media monitoring tools that collect and analyze large swaths of data. These tools often allow searches by hashtag or keyword and may generate profiles and predictions.

**Example:** The United States Postal Inspection Service (USPIS) [conducted blanket keyword searches](https://www.vice.com/en/article/n7nmaz/post-office-cops-used-social-media-surveillance-program-illegally) (such as "protest," "destroy," and "attack") across social media in its iCOP surveillance program. The Office of Inspector General later determined that USPIS did not have legal authority for these searches, since they were not connected to mail or the post office.

### Combined Data Mining

Some tools combine social media history with other information:

- **Palantir + New Orleans Police Department:** Combined social media history with additional data for "predictive policing"—attempting to predict the likelihood that certain individuals would commit acts of violence or become victims.

- **Minneapolis Police Department + Clearview AI:** Used Clearview AI for facial recognition; Clearview AI scraped billions of photos from social media.

- **Customs and Border Patrol:** [Inappropriately surveilled](https://www.nbcsandiego.com/news/investigations/report-cbp-inappropriately-flagged-u-s-citizens-during-migrant-caravan/2726925/) journalists, attorneys, and advocates who met migrants from Central America at the U.S.-Mexico border, creating dossiers combining social media data with other information.

## Threats Posed by Social Media Monitoring

### Chilling Effect on Free Speech

Social media monitoring can chill protected speech and association. People may fear:

- Being screened for their political opinion
- Being subjected to increased interrogation or denied entry to the U.S.
- Exposing others to surveillance
- Being included in a police database
- Facing criminal charges

### Documented Targeting of Protesters

Social media monitoring has been used to target nonviolent protesters engaging in protected speech:

**ICE:** Kept tabs on anti-Trump protesters in New York City.

**LAPD:** During protests following the murder of George Floyd:
- Monitored social media accounts of both protesters and journalists
- Sought communications containing keywords: "black lives matter," "BLM," "demonstration," "protest," "protester"
- Monitored terms including #BLMLA, #SayHerName, Sandra Bland, Tamir Rice, #fuckdonaldtrump, and names of other people killed by LA police

**DHS:** Collected data on Black Lives Matter activities posted on social media even for events expected to be peaceful.

**LookingGlass Cyber Solutions:** Gathered information on more than 600 protests against forced family separation and shared it with DHS and state fusion centers.

### Surveillance Beyond Protests

Some law enforcement agencies surveilled not just protests but also social gatherings and cultural events:

- **Texas Fusion Center:** Surveilled social gatherings and Black cultural events, including a Juneteenth celebration that took place online. Shared organizer names, social media information, notable guests, and attendance numbers with local, state, and federal law enforcement.

- **DHS:** Circulated information on a nationwide series of silent vigils and planned to monitor a community parade in Congress Heights (a predominantly Black neighborhood), a funk parade in U Street (a historically Black neighborhood), and a walk to end breast cancer.

### Minimal Oversight and Transparency

Social media monitoring allows law enforcement to monitor thousands of accounts with a single button push but is subject to extremely minimal oversight and transparency.

### Risk of Misinterpretation

Since social media posts are highly contextual, police may misinterpret innocuous language, leading to severe consequences for individuals who may not have been aware they were under surveillance. Police and prosecutors have used Facebook photos, "likes," and associations to make false allegations of criminal gang activity.

**Additional concerns:**
- It's unclear whether social media monitoring successfully identifies and stops threats to national security
- Interpretation is even more difficult for non-English languages and unknown cultural contexts
- It's not always easy to correctly link an individual with their account

### Data Quality and Retention Issues

Because many law enforcement agencies partner with data mining companies, they risk adding large amounts of unverified information into their databases. This data is often shared with other organizations, all with different retention policies. The longer data sits in their systems, the more opportunity there is for misuse.

## EFF's Work on Social Media Monitoring

### FOIA Lawsuit Against DHS

EFF sued DHS under the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) to obtain records on its Visa Lifecycle Vetting Initiative (VLVI), its social media program to spy on immigrants. DHS released no records in response to the request. EFF is seeking information on:

- The program's current status
- Whether the government is monitoring people's social media profiles and for what purpose
- How this impacts visa approvals and denials
- Details about a $4.8 million transaction
- VLVI contracts, notes on how the program works, performance work statements
- Recent datasets used for input, training materials, operating procedures
- Privacy impact statements, audits, and reports to legislative bodies

### Policy Advocacy

[EFF opposes the U.S. government's monitoring](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2017/11/eff-urges-dhs-abandon-social-media-surveillance-and-automated-extreme-vetting) of anyone's social media accounts and internet activity. EFF has urged DHS to abandon any such vetting program because:

- Social media surveillance invades privacy
- It violates the First Amendment by chilling speech
- It allows the government to target and punish people for expressing views it doesn't like
- [Vetting based on speech on social media](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2019/11/deep-dive-eff-dhs-stop-mass-collection-social-media-information) would be ineffective and discriminatory

### Lawsuit Against U.S. Postal Service

EFF [sued the U.S. Postal Service](https://www.eff.org/press/releases/eff-sues-us-postal-service-records-about-covert-social-media-spying-program) and its inspection agency seeking records about a covert program to secretly comb through online posts of social media users before street protests.

### Lawsuit Against Government Agencies (2009)

Working with the Samuelson Law Technology and Public Policy Clinic at UC Berkeley School of Law, EFF filed suit in December 2009 against a half-dozen government agencies for refusing to disclose their policies for using social networking sites for investigations, data-collection, and surveillance.

### Advocacy Against Fake Law Enforcement Accounts

EFF has called on Meta to:

- Publish the number of fake/impersonator law enforcement accounts identified in regular transparency reports
- Include information on which agencies the accounts belonged to and what action was taken
- Alert users and groups that interacted with these accounts
- Amend documentation for U.S. governments to make terms barring such accounts more explicit
- Notify departments with written policies of engaging in this behavior that it violates their rules

# Social Media Monitoring

## Overview

Social media contains some of the most intimate details of our lives: health information, political views, religious beliefs, sexual orientation, and our social connections. The data includes usernames, bios, contact information, status updates, comments, photos, videos, event postings, friend lists, groups, private messages, location data, and more.

Law enforcement agencies at all levels—federal, state, and local—monitor social media accounts, often without transparency or meaningful oversight.

## How It Works

### Government Surveillance Programs

**Immigration Enforcement:**
- The Department of Homeland Security (DHS) and U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) monitor social media posts of asylum seekers, tourists seeking visa waivers, and immigration benefit applicants
- Surveillance extends to anyone in these individuals' networks
- Social media information is collected from travelers—including American residents—even without suspicion of illegal activity
- DHS uses this data as evidence in deportations and visa denials

**Local Law Enforcement:**
- Police departments routinely monitor social media for various purposes
- Example: In July 2020, LAPD officers were ordered to record social media information (Facebook, Instagram, Twitter) of every civilian they questioned—including those not arrested or accused of any crime

### Monitoring Methods

**Manual Surveillance:**
- Police create fake accounts to search publicly available posts
- Officers search for names, groups, keywords, and hashtags
- They identify protest organizers, event times, and participant affiliations

**Legal Requests:**
- Subpoenas and warrants can compel social media companies to provide additional data not publicly available

**Fake and Imposter Accounts:**
- In 2010, a DEA agent created a phony Facebook account using a woman's identity without consent, posting her seized photos including pictures of her children
- DHS created fake university profiles ("University of Farmington") to entrap immigrants, leading to over 170 arrests
- Memphis Police Department created fake profiles to monitor Black Lives Matter activists

**Automated Monitoring Tools:**
Police use specialized software that collects and analyzes large volumes of social media data, allowing searches by hashtag or keyword and generating profiles and predictions.

Example: The U.S. Postal Inspection Service conducted blanket keyword searches (terms like "protest," "destroy," "attack") across social media through its iCOP surveillance program. The Office of Inspector General later determined these searches lacked legal authority.

## Commercial Vendors

Law enforcement agencies have contracted with numerous social media monitoring firms, including:

- Babel Street
- Cobwebs
- Dataminr
- Geofeedia
- Giant Oak (GOST)
- LookingGlass Cyber Solutions
- Media Sonar
- Palantir Technologies
- ShadowDragon (SocialNet, OIMonitor)
- Skopenow
- Voyager Analytics

> **Note:** Due to limited transparency, this list is incomplete and may be outdated. Some vendors have lost access to social media APIs after their surveillance activities were exposed.

### Integration with Other Technologies

Some tools combine social media data with other surveillance methods:
- **Palantir** provided software to New Orleans Police that combined social media history with other data for "predictive policing"
- **Clearview AI** scraped billions of photos from social media for facial recognition used by Minneapolis Police
- **Customs and Border Patrol** created dossiers on journalists, attorneys, and advocates, combining social media data with other information

## Threats and Concerns

### Chilling Effect on Free Speech

Social media monitoring can suppress protected speech and association. People may fear:
- Being screened for political opinions
- Increased interrogation or entry denial when traveling
- Exposing their contacts to surveillance
- Inclusion in police databases
- Criminal charges for protected speech

### Documented Targeting of Protesters

Law enforcement has used social media monitoring against nonviolent protesters:

- **ICE** tracked anti-Trump protesters in New York City
- **LAPD** monitored protesters and journalists during George Floyd protests, searching for keywords including "black lives matter," "BLM," "demonstration," "protest," #SayHerName, and names of people killed by police
- **DHS** collected data on Black Lives Matter activities even for expected peaceful events
- **LookingGlass Cyber Solutions** gathered information on over 600 protests against family separation, sharing it with DHS and fusion centers

### Surveillance Beyond Protests

A Texas fusion center surveilled not just protests but also:
- Social gatherings and Black cultural events
- A Juneteenth celebration held online
- Organizer names, social media information, and attendance numbers were shared with local, state, and federal agencies

DHS also monitored community parades, cultural events, and even a breast cancer awareness walk.

### Accuracy and Interpretation Problems

- **Context misinterpretation:** Police may misread innocuous language, song lyrics, or inside jokes as threats
- **Language barriers:** Non-English posts and unfamiliar cultural contexts increase misinterpretation risk
- **Identity errors:** Linking individuals to accounts is error-prone
- **Data quality:** Partnerships with data mining companies introduce unverified information into databases
- **Data sharing:** Information spreads across organizations with varying retention policies, increasing misuse potential

Police and prosecutors have used Facebook photos, "likes," and associations to make false allegations of criminal gang activity.

## EFF's Legal and Advocacy Work

### Active Legal Cases

**EFF v. DHS (VLVI):**
EFF sued DHS under FOIA to obtain records on its Visa Lifecycle Vetting Initiative, a social media program targeting immigrants. EFF seeks information on:
- Program status and purpose
- Impact on visa approvals and denials
- Details of a $4.8 million transaction
- Contracts, training materials, operating procedures, and privacy impact statements

**EFF v. USPS:**
EFF sued the U.S. Postal Service seeking records about its covert iCOP program that monitored social media posts before street protests.

### Policy Positions

EFF opposes government monitoring of social media accounts because:
- It invades privacy
- It violates the First Amendment by chilling speech
- It allows targeting and punishment based on viewpoints
- Vetting based on social media is ineffective and discriminatory

### Platform Accountability

EFF has called on Meta to:
- Publish the number of fake law enforcement accounts identified in transparency reports
- Alert users and groups that interacted with fake accounts
- Explicitly prohibit such accounts in government documentation
- Notify departments with policies permitting this behavior that it violates platform rules

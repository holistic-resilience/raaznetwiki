# Police Access to IoT Devices

Long gone are the days when our computers were the only electronic devices connected to the internet. Now, everything from refrigerators to robot vacuum cleaners can be accessed via apps from anywhere in the world. This is commonly called "the internet of things" (IoT).

## The Privacy Problem

Many IoT devices store personal data not locally on the device itself, but on remote company servers where it can be accessed by many individuals and entities—including law enforcement. 

The most concerning are networked devices that collect and store video and audio from in and around our homes, some in real-time. But other IoT data might also interest police:

- **Thermostat data** might show whether you were home at a relevant time
- **Refrigerator data** could indicate activity patterns
- **Fitness tracker data** has been used in criminal investigations
- **Doorbell cameras** capture video and audio of your surroundings

It's bad enough that police seek IoT company data about our lives. Even worse, some IoT companies have built police involvement directly into their infrastructure. Companies have created request forms allowing law enforcement to obtain customer data in "emergency" circumstances—without a warrant or customer permission.

**Key statistic:** As of January 2021, 18% of U.S. homes have a surveillance doorbell, making this one of the largest networked surveillance systems in the country.

## How IoT Devices Work

Consumer IoT devices connect to the internet in various ways:

- **Interconnected devices**: An electronic assistant like Echo or Alexa can dim lights or control your television
- **App-controlled devices**: Managed through smartphone applications
- **Voice-activated devices**: Always-listening microphones waiting for command words

### Security Devices with Audio/Video

Devices like Amazon Ring doorbells are triggered by motion, which activates both camera and microphone to record the incident. This data uploads to remote company servers. Even without motion-triggered events, users can use the app in real-time to look through, listen through, or talk through their device.

## How Law Enforcement Accesses IoT Data

As of 2019, 69% of U.S. homes had at least one IoT device. This means a tremendous amount of data in a majority of homes is potentially available to police. Law enforcement has sought data from:

- **Fitbit** fitness trackers
- **Google Nest** thermostats
- **Doorbell cameras** and security systems

**Where any data is collected on individuals, eventually police will come looking for it.**

### Methods of Police Access

1. **Direct requests**: Police can knock on your door and request footage from visible cameras
2. **Digital requests**: Police email you or have the manufacturer email you requesting footage (sometimes mass emails to many users at once)
3. **Warrants**: Presented to the device owner (if data is stored on-device) or to the company (if stored on company servers)
4. **Emergency requests**: Some companies have specialized forms for police to request data without a warrant or user consent

**Important:** If there is no warrant, you always have the right to say "no" to police requests for your data.

**Concerning practice:** Some companies do not inform device owners when they have turned over their data to police. Amazon has admitted to giving police Ring doorbell data without user consent in emergency situations.

## Corporate Ownership and Data Control

A challenge for privacy-conscious consumers is tracking which companies control their data, especially given frequent mergers and acquisitions. 

**Example:** The attempted sale of iRobot (makers of Roomba) to Amazon prompted fears that Amazon would inherit maps of people's homes—valuable data considering Amazon's own home robot devices and surveillance-based indoor drones.

## Threats to Privacy

Police access to IoT data stored on company servers represents a massive expansion of government surveillance. Without rigorous enforcement of warrant requirements, unencrypted data collected inside your home could be stored in one of the largest police evidence lockers in history—the servers of IoT companies.

The ubiquity of these devices could create a world where people don't feel comfortable having sensitive conversations in large portions of their home, yard, or neighborhood—fearing their conversation could be recorded, stored on company servers, and made ready for police access.

## Protecting Your Privacy

### What EFF Has Accomplished

- **End-to-end encryption**: Pushed Ring to introduce encryption for footage in early 2021, increasing user power to limit police access
- **Policy changes**: Pressured Ring to stop allowing police to send mass emails to customers requesting footage (changed June 2021)
- **Third-party trackers**: Discovered and exposed that the Amazon Ring app was full of undisclosed third-party trackers

### Ongoing Advocacy

EFF continues to push big tech companies to:
- Use their resources to fight overly broad warrants
- Protect user data from unreasonable government access
- Increase transparency about law enforcement requests

The fight to make consumer IoT devices more privacy-oriented is far from over.

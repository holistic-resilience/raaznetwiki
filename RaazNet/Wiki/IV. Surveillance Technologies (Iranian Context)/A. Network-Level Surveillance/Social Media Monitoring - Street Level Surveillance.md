# Social Media Monitoring

## How It Works

Social media contains some of the most intimate details of our lives, including:

- Health information
- Likes and dislikes
- Political views and religious beliefs
- Sexual orientation
- People with whom we associate

Social media content includes usernames, bios, contact information, status updates, comments, photos, videos and streams, event postings, friend or follower lists, friend requests, groups, private messages, account creation dates, location data, and more.

### Federal Immigration Surveillance

Law enforcement agencies including the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) and U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) monitor the social media posts of:

- Asylum seekers
- Tourists seeking visa waivers
- People applying for immigration benefits

Social media information is collected from travelers—including American residents—even when they are not suspected of any illegal activity. This surveillance extends to anyone in these people's networks. DHS has offered no transparency about its multi-million dollar program of spying on immigrants' and other foreign visitors' social media posts, which it uses as evidence in deportations and visa denials.

### Broad Law Enforcement Monitoring

Federal, state, and local law enforcement agencies all monitor social media accounts. While this has typically been done to surveil political activists, at times this has been much broader.

**Example:** LAPD officers were ordered to [note the social media information](https://www.brennancenter.org/sites/default/files/2021-09/A%20Series_LAPD%20Social%20Media%20Monitoring%20FOIA_2021.pdf) (including Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, and other accounts) of every civilian they questioned, according to [an internal July 2020 memo](https://www.brennancenter.org/sites/default/files/2021-09/I.%20Beck%20FI%20Memo.pdf) obtained by the Brennan Center. This included those who were not arrested or even accused of a crime.

Police may combine social media monitoring with other technologies, such as face recognition, to identify individuals. Since social media posts are highly contextual, police may misinterpret innocuous language, song lyrics, or inside jokes, leading to criminal consequences for individuals who may not have even been aware they were under surveillance.

## How Law Enforcement Monitors Social Media

### Manual Surveillance Methods

Police may use fake accounts to:

- Search through publicly available posts for names of groups or individuals
- Search for specific keywords and hashtags
- Scour social media pages and groups to determine names and affiliations
- Identify the time of planned events and other information

### Common Surveillance Purposes

- Monitoring asylum seekers and tourists
- Gathering information about planned protests or activities
- Identifying protesters and protest organizers
- Data mining to learn identities and affiliations of people engaged in legally protected conduct
- Searching for perceived threats to national security

### Legal Data Requests

Police may use a subpoena or warrant to request social media data as part of a criminal investigation when they want additional information that's not publicly available or would take too long to scrape.

### Fake and Imposter Accounts

Police officers have also set up fake or imposter accounts:

- **DEA (2010):** An agent [created a phony Facebook account](https://www.buzzfeednews.com/article/chrishamby/government-says-federal-agents-can-impersonate-woman-online) in the name of a young woman without her knowledge or consent, posting photos from her seized materials, including pictures of her son and niece.

- **DHS:** Created fake profiles and pages to trick immigrants into registering with a fake college called [The University of Farmington](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2020/dec/05/ice-fake-university-farmington-inside-story), leading to more than 170 arrests.

- **Memphis Police Department:** [Created fake profiles](https://theappeal.org/memphis-police-collected-black-lives-matter-activists-private-facebook-posts/) to monitor Black Lives Matter activists.

### Automated Monitoring Tools

Police use social media monitoring tools that collect and analyze large swaths of social media data. These tools often allow police to search by hashtag or keyword and may generate profiles and predictions.

**Example:** The United States Postal Inspection Service [conducted blanket keyword searches](https://www.vice.com/en/article/n7nmaz/post-office-cops-used-social-media-surveillance-program-illegally) (such as "protest," "destroy," and "attack") across social media in its iCOP surveillance program. The Office of Inspector General later determined that the USPIS did not have legal authority for these searches.

Some firms have lost access to social media data—for example, Geofeedia lost access after ACLU of Northern California's public records requests revealed the company was pitching its product as a resource to surveil people lawfully protesting against police violence.

### Data Integration and Predictive Policing

Some tools combine social media history with other information:

- **Palantir:** Provided software to the New Orleans Police Department that combined social media history with additional data for "predictive policing"—attempting to predict the likelihood that certain individuals would commit acts of violence or become victims.

- **Clearview AI:** The Minneapolis Police Department used Clearview AI for facial recognition; Clearview AI scraped billions of photos from social media.

- **Customs and Border Patrol:** [Inappropriately surveilled](https://www.nbcsandiego.com/news/investigations/report-cbp-inappropriately-flagged-u-s-citizens-during-migrant-caravan/2726925/) journalists, attorneys, and advocates who met migrants from Central America at the U.S.-Mexico border.

## Threats Posed by Social Media Monitoring

### Chilling Effect on Protected Speech

Social media monitoring can chill protected speech and association. People engaging in protected speech or traveling to the U.S. may fear:

- Being screened for their political opinion
- Increased interrogation or denied entry to the country
- Exposing others to surveillance
- Being included in a police database
- Facing criminal charges

### Documented Targeting of Protesters

Social media monitoring has been used to target nonviolent protesters engaging in protected speech:

**ICE:** Kept tabs on anti-Trump protesters in New York City.

**LAPD:** Monitored social media accounts belonging to protesters and journalists during protests following the murder of George Floyd, seeking communications containing keywords including:
- "black lives matter," "BLM"
- "demonstration," "protest," "protester"
- #BLMLA, #SayHerName
- Sandra Bland, Tamir Rice
- #fuckdonaldtrump
- Names of people killed by LA police

**DHS:** Collected data on Black Lives Matter activities posted on social media even for events expected to be peaceful.

**LookingGlass Cyber Solutions:** Gathered information on more than 600 protests against forced family separation, sharing it with DHS and state fusion centers.

### Surveillance Beyond Protests

Some agencies surveilled not just protests but social gatherings and cultural events:

- **Texas Fusion Center:** Surveilled a Juneteenth celebration that took place online, sharing organizer names, social media information, notable guests, and attendance numbers with local, state, and federal law enforcement.

- **DHS:** Circulated information on nationwide silent vigils and planned to monitor a community parade in Congress Heights, a funk parade in U Street, and a walk to end breast cancer.

### Systemic Concerns

**Minimal Oversight:** Social media monitoring allows law enforcement to monitor thousands of accounts with the push of a single button, but is subject to extremely minimal oversight and transparency.

**Misinterpretation:** Since social media posts are highly contextual, police may misinterpret innocuous language, leading to severe consequences for individuals unaware they were under surveillance. Police and prosecutors have used Facebook photos, "likes," and associations to make false allegations of criminal gang activity.

**Questionable Effectiveness:** It's not clear that social media monitoring successfully identifies and stops threats to national security. Interpretation may be even more difficult for non-English languages and unknown cultural contexts.

**Identity Verification Problems:** It is not always easy to correctly link an individual with their account. Law enforcement agencies partnering with data mining companies risk adding large amounts of unverified information into their databases. This data is often shared with other organizations with different retention policies, creating opportunities for misuse.

## EFF's Work Related to Social Media Monitoring

### Legal Actions

**EFF v. DHS (VLVI):** EFF sued DHS under the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) to obtain records on its Visa Lifecycle Vetting Initiative, its social media program to spy on immigrants. DHS released no records in response to the request. EFF is seeking information on:
- The program's current status
- Whether the government is monitoring people's social media profiles and for what purpose
- How this impacts visa approvals and denials
- Details about a $4.8 million transaction
- VLVI contracts, notes on how the program works, performance work statements, training materials, operating procedures, privacy impact statements, audits, and reports to legislative bodies

**EFF v. USPS:** EFF [sued the U.S. Postal Service](https://www.eff.org/press/releases/eff-sues-us-postal-service-records-about-covert-social-media-spying-program) seeking records about a covert program to secretly comb through online posts before street protests.

**2009 Lawsuit:** Working with the Samuelson Law Technology and Public Policy Clinic at UC Berkeley School of Law, EFF filed suit against a half-dozen government agencies for refusing to disclose their policies for using social networking sites for investigations, data-collection, and surveillance.

### Policy Advocacy

[EFF opposes the U.S. government's monitoring](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2017/11/eff-urges-dhs-abandon-social-media-surveillance-and-automated-extreme-vetting) of anyone's social media accounts and internet activity. EFF has urged DHS to abandon vetting programs because social media surveillance:
- Invades privacy
- Violates the First Amendment by chilling speech
- Allows the government to target and punish people for expressing views it doesn't like
- Would be [ineffective and discriminatory](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2019/11/deep-dive-eff-dhs-stop-mass-collection-social-media-information)

### Addressing Fake Law Enforcement Accounts

EFF has called on Meta to:
- Publish the number of fake/impersonator law enforcement accounts identified in transparency reports
- Include information on which agencies the accounts belonged to and what action was taken
- Alert users and groups that interacted with these accounts
- Amend documentation for U.S. governments to make terms barring such accounts more explicit
- Notify departments with written policies of engaging in this behavior that they are in violation

# Understanding and Cataloguing Our Information

To develop a complete picture of our security situation, we must recognize information as a valuable asset that we produce and use in our work. This section guides you through mapping your information and exploring both threats to its integrity and strategies for protection.

## Types of Information We Handle

Our work generates and relies upon several categories of information:

- **Work Products**: Reports, databases, images, voice and video recordings
- **Operational Information**: Text messages, files, progress reports, and other office communications
- **Personal Information**: Data identifying us as organization members, along with personal or professional affiliations
- **Metadata**: Data generated by our use of digital devices, which can reveal our movements or relationships

This information exists in various forms and locations. If accessed by adversaries, it can provide a comprehensive picture of our actions and relationships—making it an extremely valuable asset requiring careful protection.

## The Digital Security Challenge

As human rights defenders have gained increased access to digital technologies, their opponents have shown heightened interest in accessing their devices and intercepting communications. Several factors complicate protection efforts:

- Many popular digital tools offer limited protection against well-resourced adversaries
- Some opponents may have collaborative relationships with software providers or online service companies
- Consider adding these providers to your actor map when assessing risks

Developing digital literacy is therefore an empowering and essential step in activism.

## Categories of Information

Creating an information security strategy begins with understanding what information you have, where it resides, and how it moves. Cataloguing information transforms it from a vague mass of data into a tangible, manageable asset.

### Information States

Information exists in two primary states:

| State | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **At Rest** | Stationary, stored information | Files in a cabinet, data on a hard drive |
| **In Motion** | Information being transmitted | Messages via mobile phone, email exchanges |

**Important**: Digital information often changes states. Cloud storage data is typically at rest but enters motion during upload or access as it travels across the internet.

## Mapping Your Information Ecosystem

Create a comprehensive map of your personal and organizational information. This map can be a text document or spreadsheet and should be updated regularly.

### Key Questions for Your Information Map

**1. What information is it?**
- Group similar types together (e.g., all financial documents in one category)
- Find categories that work for your context
- Include software you use, as some applications may be sensitive
- Pay attention to metadata in documents and communications (photos, emails)
- Consider whether metadata could be sensitive and whether it can be removed or obscured

**2. Where does it reside?**
- Document physical and digital storage locations
- Note cloud services and third-party platforms

**3. Who can access it?**
- Identify all individuals with current access
- Consider both authorized and potential unauthorized access

**4. How sensitive is it?**

Use these classification levels:

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Secret** | Only specific persons should have access |
| **Confidential** | Not for public consumption, but staff may access |
| **Public** | No risk from public exposure |

## Building Your Security Strategy

By categorizing and annotating your information map with these questions, you gain an overview of your sensitive information's current status and who may access it.

This analysis reveals gaps in your current information security, enabling you to build a targeted strategy to address vulnerabilities.

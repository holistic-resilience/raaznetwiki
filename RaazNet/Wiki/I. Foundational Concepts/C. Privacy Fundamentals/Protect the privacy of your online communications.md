# Protect the Privacy of Your Online Communications

Each communication method—digital or otherwise—comes with advantages and disadvantages in terms of convenience, popularity, cost, and security. It's up to each of us to weigh the benefits and risks of the methods we use to communicate. When our risks are higher, it is wise to choose our communication tools more deliberately.

This guide illustrates the steps needed to create and implement a communication plan as well as specific best practices aimed at protecting your privacy and sensitive information when communicating with others online.

---

## Map Your Communications and Make a Plan

### Assess the Sensitivity Level of Your Communications

Start by mapping your current communication landscape:

- **Who** is communicating with whom?
- **What channels** are you using?
- **What information** are you sharing, and in what forms?
- Are you sharing all information over one channel, or using different channels for different types of communications?

Ask yourself what would happen if information you need to share was compromised, lost, or exposed. Sensitive information may:

- Disrupt work continuity (e.g., loss of irreplaceable files critical to operations)
- Endanger individuals (colleagues, allies, donors) through identification or contextual details
- Enable impersonation or reputational harm (e.g., login credentials or private communications accessed by unauthorized parties)
- Violate laws in specific jurisdictions (e.g., copyrighted material, explicit content)

Rethink your communications based on their sensitivity level and decide how you will protect the most sensitive ones.

### Reflect on Who Needs Access

Decide whether the information you are sharing should be restricted to specific recipients or can be shared more widely.

### Analyze Your Needs and Capacities

Ask yourself these questions when choosing a communication channel for sensitive information:

- Should participants' identities be kept secret from administrators and others with server access?
- Do you need real-time communication or can you wait for replies?
- What formats do you need to send (text, files, media)?
- Do you prefer writing, calls, or video conferences?
- Do you need access on both computers and phones? What operating systems are in use?
- What tools do your contacts already use? Can they learn new tools?
- What's your budget? Can you self-host your communication platforms?

### Basic Security Measures

When selecting communication tools:

- **Enable 2-factor authentication** whenever available
- **Ensure HTTPS/TLS is enabled** on all platforms
- **Verify end-to-end encryption** claims—check if the encryption technology is free and open-source

> **Understanding Encryption Levels:**
> 
> Most online services protect messages with **client-to-server encryption** (HTTPS/TLS) as they travel from your device through your ISP to the provider's servers. This prevents eavesdropping on your local network.
> 
> However, messages remain unencrypted on the platform's servers and the recipient's device. Anyone with server access can read your communications. For sensitive information, use **end-to-end encrypted** services.
> 
> If end-to-end encryption isn't available (common with large-group video conferencing), consider using a service hosted by a trusted entity, or self-hosting if you have the technical expertise.

### Define a Communication Plan

Based on your assessment, establish and rehearse a communication plan for yourself and your community:

1. **Define your Traffic Light Protocol (TLP)** to decide what sensitive information can be shared with whom, in what forms, and over which channels. See [CIRCL's Traffic Light Protocol](https://www.circl.lu/pub/traffic-light-protocol/) for guidance.

2. **Talk to your contacts.** Propose, discuss, and agree upon a specific plan for how you will (and will not) communicate.

3. **Consider informal and formal steps** you can take to protect communications.

4. **Rehearse your communication plan.** In a crisis, we don't think as clearly and may make decisions that endanger us. A rehearsed plan helps prevent emergencies and contributes to safety in critical situations.

## Create Multiple Identities

Consider creating alternative online identities to separate your communications based on levels of trust.

Using different identities for each of your activities can help mitigate threats like political or economic retribution for what you do online.

**Learn more:** [Managing and Protecting Multiple Online Identities](https://securityinabox.org/en/communication/multiple-identities/)

## Protect and Anonymize Your Connections

- **Browse more securely** to protect your online activities from surveillance
- **Anonymize your connections** to achieve goals without leaving traces, especially in high-risk situations

Using the Tor Browser or other anonymity tools allows you to visit websites without disclosing who you are or where you are from. Note that if you log in to a website with your usual account while using these tools, you will still share your account information with that platform.

**Learn more:**
- [Safer Browsing](https://securityinabox.org/en/internet-connection/safer-browsing)

## Use Email More Safely

Although many people prefer encrypted chat apps or video calls for everyday communications, email remains essential for creating online accounts and organizing work.

By default, email is not the most secure communication method:
- Not encrypted by default
- Carries significant metadata
- Persists on providers' servers
- Exposes you to phishing and malware attacks

However, email is a resilient technology that ensures communication continuity even when servers experience temporary outages. Learning to use it safely is worthwhile.

**Learn more:** [Secure Your Email Communications](https://securityinabox.org/en/communication/secure-email/)

## Use Messaging Apps More Safely

Chat services may access and collect information on your:
- Location
- Activity
- Message content
- Contact list

Picking the right messaging app is important to protecting your safety.

**Learn more:** [Use Chat Apps More Securely](https://securityinabox.org/en/communication/secure-chat/)

## Remove Identifying Information from Files

It may seem like using face blur or covering sensitive details will protect people or places in your images. However, some methods of saving edited photos don't prevent someone from seeing what you tried to hide.

All files contain invisible information called **metadata** that can reveal:
- When and where files were created
- Who created them
- What device was used

You can view some metadata by right-clicking a file and selecting "Properties" or "Get Info."

**Learn more:** [Remove Identifying Information from Photos, Videos, PDFs, and Other Files](https://securityinabox.org/en/files/destroy-identifying-information/)

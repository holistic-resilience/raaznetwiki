# A Journalist's Guide to Digital Security Risk Assessment

A risk assessment (or "threat model") is a systematic process for journalists and filmmakers to identify digital security challenges and develop strategies to mitigate them. It involves evaluating the digital assets you own, create, or engage with.

This tool is **extensible**—you can work through this process repeatedly as a first step for covering a new story, or during each phase of an investigation. Risk changes based on the work you are doing and where you are doing it.

---

## What Am I Protecting? (Assets)

From morning until night, using a device means generating assets. Start your risk assessment by tracking everything you do with your phone, computer, and other devices that store information.

**Common digital assets include:**

- **Devices:** Cellphones, computers, tablets
- **Communications:** Email, texts, social media posts, direct messages
- **Documents:** Tips from sources, field notes, drafts
- **Footage:** Interviews, b-roll
- **Browsing data:** Search history, visited sites

Once you've tracked your activities, identify patterns and prioritize assets most important to you—such as passwords to accounts containing personal information or contact details of sensitive sources. Identifying high-priority assets helps you address risks efficiently.

---

## Who Am I Protecting My Assets From? (Adversaries)

### Governments
Both foreign and domestic governments collect and use data in various ways. They may gain access to privileged information through networked devices, including [targeted exploits](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/04/02/us/politics/nso-contract-us-spy.html) aimed at specific individuals. Governments also employ [dragnet surveillance policies](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2024/apr/16/house-fisa-government-surveillance-senate) affecting nearly everyone online.

### Corporations
Companies increasingly buy and sell personal data for [targeted advertising](https://www.newamerica.org/oti/reports/special-delivery/the-role-of-data-in-the-targeted-advertising-industry/) and [financial gain](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/data-brokers-are-running-wild-and-only-congress-can-rein-them). Personal information can [become an asset](https://themarkup.org/privacy/2024/02/23/what-happens-to-your-sensitive-data-when-a-data-broker-goes-bankrupt) when companies are bought and sold.

### Hackers
Hackers exploit system vulnerabilities for [ransomware](https://techcrunch.com/2024/10/31/2024-looks-set-to-be-another-record-breaking-year-for-ransomware-and-its-likely-going-to-get-worse/) or data exfiltration. They often work in groups and may be [sponsored by nation-states](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2024/03/08/microsoft-hack-email-russia/).

### Scammers
Scammers engage in [social engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_engineering_(security))—psychological manipulation to extract confidential information. Attack vectors include phishing emails, phone calls, text messages, and [evolving tactics](https://hbr.org/2024/10/phishing-attacks-are-evolving-heres-how-to-resist-them).

### Trolls
Trolls engage in [provocative online behaviors](https://datasociety.net/library/source-hacking-media-manipulation-in-practice/), including doxxing (sharing personal information like addresses and phone numbers) to unleash [real-world threats](https://pressfreedomtracker.us/all-incidents/five-members-neo-nazi-group-arrested-alleged-targeted-harassment-journalists/).

## What Might Adversaries Be Capable Of? (Resources)

Adversary capabilities fall into two main categories:

### Legal Methods
Government agencies worldwide can [legally request](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/one-nation-under-risaa/) user information from service providers (Meta, Google, ISPs). This data is collected for national security purposes or criminal investigations. In the United States, journalists have [legal protections](https://www.justice.gov/archives/jm/criminal-resource-manual-661-privacy-protection-act-1980) for newsgathering information.

### Illegal Methods
Some adversaries engage in illegal activities:
- Hacking networks and systems for unlawful access
- Extorting organizations and individuals
- Tricking targets into disclosing private information
- Online harassment and physical intimidation

> **Important:** These actors do not distinguish between professional and personal accounts, and may resort to intimidation tactics including online harassment and physical threats.

---

## How Big Is This Risk to Me? (Likelihood)

Determining how likely you are to be targeted is crucial. Consider:

- **Your beat and coverage areas**
- **Your identity:** Female journalists are [far more likely](https://www.theguardian.com/media/article/2024/may/06/female-journalists-under-attack-as-press-freedom-falters) to experience online harassment than male peers
- **Your visibility and platform**
- **Current stories and investigations**

While we regularly hear about new cybersecurity threats, understanding your individual likelihood helps prioritize protective measures.

## What Can I Do to Protect Myself? (Resources)

### Protect Against Hacking and Account Takeovers
- [Write solid passwords](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/three-types-of-passphrases/)
- [Use a password manager](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/choosing-a-password-manager/)
- [Enable strong two-factor authentication](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/2fa-beginners/)
- [Recognize phishing tactics](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/email-security-tips/)

### Safeguard Communications
- Use encrypted messaging: [Signal primer](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/signal-beginners/) and [deep dive](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/locking-down-signal/)
- [Upgrade WhatsApp security](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/upgrading-whatsapp-security/)
- [Encrypt email messages](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/encrypting-email-mailvelope-guide/) with tools like [ProtonMail](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/protonmail-pro/)

### Protect Browsing Habits
- [Choose a privacy-focused browser](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/-depth-guide-choosing-web-browser/)
- Understand [VPN use cases](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/choosing-a-vpn/)

### Protect Source Communications
- [Set up a secure tipline](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/security-confidential-tip-pages/)
- Be vigilant about [malware threats](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/that-usb-drive-might-not-be-safe/)

### Additional Training
For comprehensive preparation, seek hostile environment and first aid training from organizations like the [International Women's Media Foundation](https://www.iwmf.org/programs/hefat-training/).

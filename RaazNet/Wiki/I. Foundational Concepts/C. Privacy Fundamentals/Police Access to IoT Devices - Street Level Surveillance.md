# Police Access to IoT Devices

Long gone are the days when our computers were the only electronic devices in the house connected to the rest of the world via the internet. Now, everything from refrigerators to robot vacuum cleaners can be accessed via apps from anywhere in the world. This is often called "the internet of things" (IoT).

## The Privacy Problem

Many IoT devices store personal data not locally on the device itself, but on remote company servers where it can be accessed by many individuals and entities, including law enforcement. Perhaps most concerning are networked devices that collect and store video and audio from in and around our homes, some in real-time.

Other IoT data might also interest police. For example, data from a networked thermostat or refrigerator might show whether someone was home at a relevant time.

**The situation is compounded by two factors:**

1. Police are going directly to IoT companies to get data about our lives
2. Some IoT companies have chosen to build police involvement into their infrastructure, creating request forms that allow law enforcement to get customer data in "emergency" circumstances without a warrant or customer permission

As of January 2021, **18% of U.S. homes** have a surveillance doorbell—making this one of the largest networked surveillance systems in the country.

## How IoT Devices Work

Consumer IoT devices connect to the internet in various ways:

- **Interconnected devices**: An electronic assistant like Echo or Alexa can dim lights or control televisions
- **App-controlled devices**: Managed remotely through smartphone applications
- **Voice-activated devices**: Microphones remain at least somewhat active, waiting for command words

### Security Cameras and Doorbells

Devices like Amazon Ring doorbells with video and audio capabilities are typically motion-triggered. When activated:

1. The camera and microphone begin recording
2. Data is uploaded to remote company servers
3. Users can access real-time feeds through apps to look, listen, or speak through the device

## How Law Enforcement Accesses IoT Data

As of 2019, **69% of U.S. homes** had at least one IoT device, meaning a tremendous amount of data is potentially available to police. Law enforcement has sought data from:

- Fitbit fitness trackers
- Google Nest thermostats
- Doorbell cameras and security systems

### Methods of Police Access

**1. Direct Request**
Police who see a doorbell or security camera pointed at a crime scene can knock on your door and request footage. This can also happen digitally, with police emailing users—sometimes en masse—to request footage.

> **Important**: If there is no warrant, you always have the right to say "no."

**2. Warrant-Based Access**
Police can present a warrant to:
- The device owner (if data is stored on-device)
- The company (if data is stored on company servers)

Note: Some companies do not inform device owners when they turn over data to police.

**3. Emergency Requests**
Some companies have specialized forms allowing police to request data directly without a warrant or user consent in claimed "emergency" circumstances—as Amazon has done with Ring cameras.

## Who Sells IoT Devices

A vast number of technology and electronics companies now sell networked devices that collect user data. A challenge for privacy-conscious consumers is tracking which companies control their data, especially given how frequently IoT companies are bought and sold.

**Example**: The attempted sale of iRobot (makers of Roomba) to Amazon raised concerns that Amazon would inherit detailed maps of people's homes—valuable data considering Amazon's own home robot devices and surveillance-based indoor drone products.

## Threats to Privacy

Police access to IoT data stored on company servers represents a significant expansion of government surveillance. Without proper safeguards—including rigorous enforcement of warrant requirements—unencrypted data collected inside homes could become one of the largest police evidence repositories in history.

The ubiquity of these devices could create a world where people feel uncomfortable having sensitive conversations in their own homes, yards, and neighborhoods, fearing their words could be recorded and stored on company servers ready for police access.

## Protecting Your Privacy

### What EFF Has Accomplished

- **End-to-end encryption**: Pushed Ring to introduce encryption for footage in early 2021, increasing user control over police access
- **Mass email restrictions**: Advocated for Ring to stop allowing police to send mass emails requesting footage (policy changed June 2021)
- **Third-party tracker exposure**: Discovered that the Amazon Ring app contained undisclosed third-party trackers and pressured the company to stop
- **Warrant challenges**: Continues pushing tech companies to fight overly broad warrants

### Your Rights

- You can **refuse** voluntary requests for footage when police don't have a warrant
- You can choose devices with **end-to-end encryption** to limit third-party access
- You can research company **privacy policies** before purchasing IoT devices

# Exercise: Security Indicators in Daily Life

## Purpose

This exercise helps you visualize your daily routines and identify moments where you can check for security indicators—signs that something may be wrong in your physical environment. By establishing a baseline of what's "normal," you can more easily detect anomalies that might indicate surveillance, tampering, or other threats.

## Materials Needed

- Drawing materials (pen, pencil, markers)
- Notebook, whiteboard, or electronic document for creating your checklist

## Instructions

### Step 1: Map Your Daily Routine

Use drawing to visualize your typical day. While this may feel unusual, externalizing your routine helps you see familiar activities from a fresh, security-focused perspective.

Consider these aspects of your day:

- **Morning**: Where do you have breakfast? What's your environment like?
- **Commute**: How do you travel to work? What vehicle, route, and companions?
- **Belongings**: What devices, keys, or items do you carry daily?
- **Workplace**: Where do you work? Who else is present? What devices do you use?
- **Meals**: Where and when do you eat? How long are your breaks?
- **Return home**: What time do you leave work? What route do you take?
- **Evening**: What do you do before sleep? What time do you typically go to bed?
- **Other locations**: Where else do you regularly spend time?

### Step 2: Identify Security Checkpoints

For each part of your routine, consider what you should check:

| Area | What to Check |
|------|---------------|
| **Vehicle** | Signs of tampering (wheels, brakes, steering) |
| **Route** | Dangerous areas; whether you're being followed |
| **Workspace** | Items in their proper place; doors and windows secured |
| **Home surroundings** | Unfamiliar people, vehicles, or police presence |

### Step 3: Create Your Checklist

Document the specific moments when you'll check for signs of danger. Consider sharing this checklist with trusted friends, neighbors, or colleagues.

If you're at higher risk, extend this analysis to include the daily routines of family members or close associates.

## Tips

- If your work involves diverse activities, repeat this exercise for each type of work you do
- Sharing your observations with a trusted person helps verify your baseline and identify blind spots
- Review and update your checklist periodically as routines change

> **For High-Risk Situations**: During protests, resistance actions, or documentation missions, situations can change rapidly. Create a separate, more detailed checklist for these specific activities, with additional checkpoints appropriate to the heightened risk level.

# Police Databases

Law enforcement agencies maintain extensive data repositories on individuals who have come into contact with the criminal justice system. These range from databases of RAP sheets to lists of alleged gang members. This data is frequently shared regionally, across states, and nationally through data switchboards operated by both government and non-government services. Many law enforcement agencies also purchase data from commercial data brokers catering to the public safety sector.

## Federal Law Enforcement Networks

The FBI's Criminal Justice Information Services (CJIS) operates at least [five distinct criminal justice information systems](https://www.justice.gov/tribal/national-crime-information-systems):

- **National Crime Information Center (NCIC)** - The most commonly accessed system by law enforcement officers
- **Next Generation Identification (NGI)** - Biometric identification system
- **National Data Exchange (N-DEx)** - Criminal justice information sharing
- **National Instant Criminal Background Check System (NICS)** - Firearm purchase background checks
- **Law Enforcement Enterprise Portal (LEEP)** - Connects to RISSnet, National Gang Intelligence Center, and eGuardian

### National Crime Information Center (NCIC)

NCIC has been the United States' central database of criminal justice information [since 1967](https://www.fbi.gov/news/stories/ncic-turns-50), when it was founded during the J. Edgar Hoover administration. It contains:

- Criminal record history information
- Fugitive records
- Stolen property listings
- Missing persons data

CJIS establishes security standards for all law enforcement agencies that access or integrate data from these systems, covering everything from password complexity to vetting requirements for maintenance workers.

## Nlets

The [National Law Enforcement Telecommunications System](https://nlets.org/) (Nlets) serves as a switchboard for data across the United States. It is not a government agency, but rather a non-profit organization with a board of directors composed of law enforcement officers.

State agencies, often including motor vehicle departments, connect their databases to Nlets, enabling data sharing across state lines. It also facilitates access to and from Canadian entities and Interpol.

Nlets provides law enforcement access to dozens of data systems and maintains a [public, interactive map](https://nlets.org/resources/maps/message-keys/key) showing which agencies can send or receive data from each source.

## State and Local Police Databases

Most state governments maintain statewide networks, such as:

- California Law Enforcement Telecommunications System (CLETS)
- Oklahoma Law Enforcement Telecommunications System (OLETS)
- Florida Crime Information Center (FCIC)

These systems typically provide access to state-level databases and connect data sources between local law enforcement agencies. They also share data with out-of-state agencies, usually through Nlets.

Local and regional agencies maintain their own systems as well. For example, in the San Diego area, SANDAG maintained the Automated Regional Justice Information System (ARJIS), while the Los Angeles Police Department operates the Network Communications System (NCS).

## Gang Databases

Gang databases identify people who authorities have connected to gangs, often based on [unsubstantiated evidence](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/02/03/opinion/los-angeles-gang-database.html) such as:

- Personal associations
- Clothing choices
- Tattoos

In some cases, individuals have been entered into gang databases based on [falsified information](https://abc7.com/lapd-falsified-records-scandal-moore/6257995/).

### Key Problems with Gang Databases

- Typically [unaudited](https://www.reuters.com/legal/government/dcs-gang-database-highlights-unconstitutional-systems-nationwide-2022-01-19/)
- No mechanism for individuals to challenge their inclusion
- No clear [petition process for removal](https://voiceofsandiego.org/2019/12/09/few-people-have-successfully-removed-themselves-from-state-gang-database/)—even without evidence of gang activity

### Recent Developments

**California (CalGang):** In 2020, CalGang contained information on over 90,000 people. Activists pushed local police departments to [dissociate from the database](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2020-06-24/california-police-urged-to-stop-using-gang-database-deemed-biased), and LAPD announced it was [discontinuing use](https://www.mercurynews.com/2020/06/22/lapd-ending-use-of-calgang-database-after-2-decades/). In 2023, a San Diego city subcommittee [began studying](https://voiceofsandiego.org/2023/07/26/sdpds-use-of-gang-database-is-under-the-microscope/) SDPD's use of CalGang.

**Chicago:** In 2023, Chicago planned to re-launch its gang database after its [previous iteration](https://www.chicagotribune.com/news/criminal-justice/ct-chicago-police-gang-database-update-20230106-cidgtynd4je4fc6x3rzioqgn3i-story.html) was criticized for racial bias. The 134,000-person list had little oversight, and those listed faced risks of severe sentencing, high bond, deportation, or job loss.

### Racial Disparities

Gang databases disproportionately affect people of color. In 2019, nearly 99% of the 18,000-person [New York City gang database were people of color](https://theappeal.org/spotlight-the-dangers-of-gang-databases-and-gang-policing/), with 88% being Black and Latine individuals. ICE has also used gang databases for deportation efforts, including cases where individuals were [erroneously entered](https://theappeal.org/spotlight-the-dangers-of-gang-databases-and-gang-policing/) into these unregulated systems.

## Commercial Databases

Law enforcement agencies frequently purchase subscriptions to privately-managed intelligence databases that provide access to:

- Law enforcement data
- Consumer records
- [Utility data](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2021/02/26/ice-private-utility-data/)
- Real-time location databases
- Face recognition databases
- Automated license plate reader databases

### Common Commercial Products

- Thomson Reuters's CLEAR
- Lexis-Nexis's Accurint
- Transunion's TLOxp
- CoplinkX

Some of these systems claim to use artificial intelligence to develop leads or identify connections between suspects.

## Threats and Harms

### Database Misuse by Personnel

Law enforcement personnel regularly access criminal justice information inappropriately and illegally, according to research by the [Associated Press](https://apnews.com/general-news-699236946e3140659fff8a2362e16f43) and [EFF](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2018/06/clets-misuse-2017). Common patterns include:

- Officers [using data to target women](https://www.wired.com/story/minnesota-police-dmv-database-abuse/) for personal reasons
- Using database access as part of [domestic abuse patterns](https://www.cleveland.com/akron/2015/09/akron_police_officer_pleads_gu.html)
- Officers [assisting criminals](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-2010-nov-14-la-me-lapd-gang-ties-20101115-story.html) with database access
- [Adding false information](https://www.justice.gov/usao-sdca/pr/former-supervisory-us-border-patrol-agent-sentenced-following-convictions-civil-rights) for retribution

### Interstate Data Sharing Risks

Interstate sharing of police data threatens vulnerable populations:

- Prosecutors in states with abortion bans may access data from states with protected abortion rights
- Federal agencies like ICE may attempt to access databases to apprehend undocumented immigrants in sanctuary states

### Data Breach Vulnerabilities

Greater data collection increases breach risks. Recent examples include:

- [Leak of personal information](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/us-news/california-doj-data-breach-exposes-personal-information-concealed-carr-rcna35849) of California concealed carry permit holders
- "[Blueleaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BlueLeaks)" - massive dump of records from hacked agencies, including fusion centers

### Gang Database Consequences

Inclusion in a gang database can result in:

- Increased stops, interrogations, and use of force by law enforcement
- Enhanced punishment in the criminal legal system
- Negative effects on employment prospects

Poor database maintenance compounds these issues. In Cook County, Illinois, [ProPublica reported](https://www.propublica.org/article/cook-county-sheriffs-office-database-new-ban-law) that hundreds of people in a 25,000-entry gang database were actually deceased.

## EFF Advocacy and Reform Efforts

Since 2015, EFF has been [documenting abuse](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2015/11/misuse-rampant-oversight-lacking-californias-law-enforcement-network) of CLETS by California agencies. Key achievements include:

- Successfully campaigning for improved misuse data collection by California DOJ
- Supporting the California Values Act to ban federal agencies from accessing certain criminal justice data for immigration enforcement
- Lobbying CADOJ to classify [immigration enforcement as formal misuse](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2019/05/california-now-classifies-immigration-enforcement-misuse-statewide-law-enforcement), leading to ICE's Enforcement and Removal Operations [being removed from CLETS](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2019/12/california-doj-cuts-ice-deportation-officers-state-law-enforcement-database) in 2019
- [Supporting AB 1242](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/governor-newsom-signs-assemblymember-bauer-kahan-and-attorney-general-bonta%E2%80%99s), signed by Gov. Gavin Newsom, to prevent law enforcement from releasing data to out-of-state agencies investigating abortions

EFF supports the abolition of CalGang and joined a coalition calling for an [immediate moratorium](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2020/06/california-coalition-calls-moratorium-state-gang-database) on its use, while also [supporting legislation](https://www.eff.org/es/deeplinks/2017/10/victory-california-just-reformed-its-gang-databases-and-made-them-more-accountable) to reform it.

EFF has also advocated for a [ban on predictive policing algorithms](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2020/09/technology-cant-predict-crime-it-can-only-weaponize-proximity-policing), which often use similar questionable information to label individuals as potential threats.

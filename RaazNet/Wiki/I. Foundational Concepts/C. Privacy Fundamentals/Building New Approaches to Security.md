# Building New Approaches to Security

Defining a comprehensive security strategy differs significantly from taking an ad-hoc or improvised approach. While many instinctive reactions to threats keep us safe, some may not—and can even be harmful.

A strategic approach involves updating our security practices through new tactics and capacities that maintain and expand our socio-political space for work as human rights defenders.

## Three Core Security Strategies

Security strategies generally fall into three complementary categories. Most effective security plans incorporate activities from two or all three approaches, depending on context and identified threats.

### Acceptance Strategies

Acceptance strategies involve engaging with other actors—allies, neutral parties, and opponents—to foster tolerance, acceptance, and support for human rights activities in society.

**Key activities include:**
- Advocacy and campaigning
- Diplomacy and relationship building
- Education and awareness raising

### Protection Strategies

Protection strategies emphasize learning and implementing new methods that defend our space for work.

**Key elements:**
- Developing new security practices and protocols
- Leveraging ally strength for protection
- Bridging gaps in existing security measures

### Deterrence Strategies

Deterrence strategies focus on raising the cost of attacks against us or our work for adversaries—whether monetary, reputational, or otherwise.

**Requirements:**
- Well-developed actor mapping
- Strong ally networks
- Advocacy and diplomatic capacity

## Building Security Capacities

Effective capacity building requires attention to five interconnected elements:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Well-being** | Physical and mental conditions for active learning, plus adequate time and safe space |
| **Attitudes** | Subjective valuation of learning processes (can be affected by stress, fear, and trauma) |
| **Knowledge** | Strong understanding of political, economic, social, technological, legal, and environmental context |
| **Skills** | Abilities to engage with and navigate your environment—physical fitness, advocacy, technical tools |
| **Resources** | Financial, material, and human resources that enable improvement in other elements |

## Working with External Trainers and Consultants

External support can be valuable for developing security plans and skills. When engaging consultants:

**What to look for:**
- Empowerment-focused approach rather than "quick-fix" solutions
- Training on tools and tactics relevant to your specific activities
- Collaborative analysis involving group members
- Respect for your decision-making authority

**What to avoid:**
- Consultants who conduct analysis without involving your team
- Those who make changes or decisions on your behalf
- Promises of immediate security improvements

### Selecting Trainers

**Recommendations:**
- Seek advice from trusted colleagues, fellow defenders, and international organizations
- Define clear learning expectations while respecting trainer expertise on achievable outcomes
- Evaluate trainer experience and knowledge relevant to your needs
- Consider practical factors: language, timing, location, follow-up opportunities
- Ensure group buy-in—disinterested members reduce training effectiveness

## Resource Considerations

Building security capacities often has financial implications. Costs may include:
- Hardware replacement (e.g., compromised laptops)
- Training and consultation fees
- Security tools and infrastructure

Organizations supporting human rights defenders with security concerns may offer financial assistance—research available support in your region.

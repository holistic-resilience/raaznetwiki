# Automated License Plate Readers (ALPRs)

Automated license plate readers (ALPRs) are high-speed, computer-controlled camera systems typically mounted on street poles, streetlights, highway overpasses, mobile trailers, or police squad cars. ALPRs automatically capture all license plate numbers that come into view, along with the location, date, and time. The data, which includes photographs of the vehicle and sometimes its driver and passengers, is then uploaded to a central server.

[Vendors claim](https://www.vigilantsolutions.com/) the information collected can be used by police to find out where a plate has been in the past, determine whether a vehicle was at the scene of a crime, identify travel patterns, and discover vehicles that may be associated with each other. Law enforcement agencies can choose to [share their information with thousands of other agencies](https://www.desertsun.com/story/news/nation/california/2023/11/14/california-police-face-new-limits-in-sharing-automated-license-plate-reader-alpr-location-data/71574307007/).

**Key Privacy Concern:** Taken in aggregate, ALPR data can paint an intimate portrait of a driver's life and chill First Amendment protected activity. ALPR technology can target drivers who visit sensitive places such as health centers, immigration clinics, gun shops, union halls, protests, or centers of religious worship.

Drivers have no control over whether their vehicle displays a license plate because the government requires it. This makes it particularly disturbing that automatic license plate readers track and record the movements of millions of ordinary people, even though the overwhelming majority are not connected to any crime.

## How ALPR Systems Work

Automated license plate readers can be broadly divided into three categories:

### Stationary or Fixed ALPR Cameras

These are installed in fixed locations such as traffic lights, telephone poles, facility entrances, or freeway exit ramps. They generally capture only vehicles in motion that pass within view.

**Capabilities:**
- Multiple cameras along a thoroughfare can reveal direction and speed of travel
- With enough cameras, police can track a vehicle in real-time
- Stored data over time can reveal that a driver likely lives or works near certain locations
- Smaller municipalities often install cameras at town entrances/exits, creating a "virtual gated community"

Police sometimes disguise ALPRs as mundane objects, such as traffic cones or [cacti](https://www.azcentral.com/story/news/local/scottsdale/2015/05/11/paradise-valley-license-plate-camera-reader/27144369/). ALPR cameras are often used in conjunction with automated red-light and speed enforcement systems, as well as toll assessment on roads and bridges.

### Mobile ALPR Cameras

Mobile ALPRs are often attached to police patrol cars, allowing officers to capture data as they drive around the city throughout their shifts. These cameras are typically turned on at the beginning of a shift and not turned off until the end.

**Capabilities:**
- Capture images of passing vehicles
- Effectively capture license plates of parked cars (driving through a parking lot can capture hundreds of plates in minutes)
- Enable "[gridding](https://www.azmirror.com/2019/07/08/arizona-police-agencies-gather-share-license-plate-data-but-few-ensure-rules-are-being-followed/)" — systematically driving up and down every block to capture intelligence on residents

Private vendors like Vigilant Solutions also capture plate data with mobile ALPRs and sell that data to police agencies and others.

### ALPR Trailers

ALPRs are also available as [trailers](https://www.cehrp.org/tags/lpr-speed-trailer/) that police can tow to particular areas and leave for extended periods. These collect data similarly to fixed ALPRs without permanent installation.

The [Drug Enforcement Administration](https://blog.tenthamendmentcenter.com/2018/10/dea-plans-to-expand-license-plate-surveillance-program/) has purchased these systems disguised as speed enforcement trailers to track vehicles along the U.S.-Mexico border. Trailers or ALPR-equipped vehicles have also been parked near [gun shows](https://www.wsj.com/articles/gun-show-customers-license-plates-come-under-scrutiny-1475451302) and [political rallies](http://www.thenewspaper.com/news/42/4232.asp).

## ALPR Databases and Hotlists

### Database Storage

Most ALPR data is stored in databases for extended periods—often as long as five years. Databases may be maintained by police departments or by private companies such as Vigilant Solutions or Flock Safety.

- Law enforcement agencies without their own ALPR systems can access data through regional sharing systems and private company networks
- Several companies operate independent, non-law enforcement ALPR databases, contracting with drivers to put cameras on private vehicles
- This commercial data is sold to insurers and others, with law enforcement able to purchase access on a subscription basis

### Hotlists

Law enforcement agencies pre-load lists of license plates the ALPR system actively searches for—such as stolen vehicles and vehicles associated with outstanding warrants. Officers can also create their own hotlists.

When an ALPR camera scans a plate on the list, the system sends an alert to the officer (for mobile readers) or the agency (for fixed readers). Some hotlists include low-level misdemeanors and traffic offenses, and some agencies use hotlists to generate revenue by stopping citation scofflaws.

## What Data ALPRs Collect

ALPRs collect:
- License plate numbers
- Location data
- Exact date and time
- Photographs of the vehicle
- Images of drivers and passengers (sometimes)
- Vehicle's immediate surroundings

**Scale of collection:** [They can collect thousands of plates per minute](http://www.policemag.com/channel/technology/articles/2016/04/lpr-technology.aspx). One vendor claims its dataset includes more than [6.5 billion scans](http://drndata.com/) and grows at a rate of [120 million data points](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2016/04/how-license-plate-readers-have-helped-police-and-lenders-target-the-poor/479436/) each month.

**Analytical capabilities:**
- Combined data can reveal direction and speed of travel through triangulation
- Aggregate historical data can reveal travel patterns
- Algorithms can predict where a driver may be in the future
- Data reveals all visitors to a particular location

Some products create "vehicle fingerprints" including vehicle color, make, model, physical damage, and bumper stickers. While data generally doesn't include driver names, law enforcement can connect names with plate numbers through other databases.

## How Law Enforcement Uses ALPRs

ALPR data is gathered indiscriminately, collecting information on millions of ordinary people. By plotting vehicle times and locations, police can paint a specific portrait of drivers' lives, determining past patterns and [possibly predicting future ones](https://www.eff.org/files/2017/02/22/neal_v._fairfax_pd_-_eff_amicus_brief_file_endorsed.pdf)—despite the vast majority of people whose data is collected never being accused of a crime.

**Key finding:** [A 2021 EFF report](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2021/04/data-driven-2-california-dragnet-new-dataset-shows-scale-vehicle-surveillance) found that according to data from 63 California law enforcement agencies, only **0.05%** of ALPR data collected was relevant to a public safety interest at the time of capture.

### Real-Time Investigations

By adding a license plate to a "hot list," officers can automatically identify or track particular vehicles in real time. Plates are added because the vehicle is:
- Stolen or associated with an outstanding warrant
- Seen at a crime scene
- Owned by a crime suspect
- Believed to be associated with a gang

### Historical Investigations

Since ALPRs collect information on everyone, officers can search and analyze historical data using a plate, partial plate, or physical address. For example, an officer may enter a convenience store location to identify vehicles near a robbery, then look up those plates to find other locations where they've been captured.

**Note:** Training materials and policies in some jurisdictions instruct officers that a hot-list alert alone may not warrant a stop—visual confirmation is required. Failure to manually confirm, combined with machine error, has caused wrongful stops.

## Threats Posed by ALPR Technology

ALPR is a powerful surveillance technology that can invade individual privacy and violate the rights of entire communities.

### Documented Abuses

- **New York:** Police drove down a street and [electronically recorded license plates of everyone parked near a mosque](http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2012/02/24/nypd-defends-tactics-over_n_1298997.html)
- **Birmingham, UK:** Police [targeted a Muslim community](https://www.theguardian.com/uk/2010/oct/25/birmingham-cctv-muslim-areas-surveillance) while misleading the public
- **Oakland:** [EFF data](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2015/01/what-we-learned-oakland-raw-alpr-data) showed police disproportionately deploy ALPR-mounted vehicles in low-income communities and communities of color
- **Washington, D.C. (1998):** An officer [pleaded guilty to extortion](https://www.wsj.com/news/articles/SB10000872396390443995604578004723603576296) after looking up plates near a gay bar and blackmailing vehicle owners
- **Kechi, Kansas:** [An officer was arrested](https://www.kwch.com/2022/11/04/kechi-police-lieutenants-arrest-puts-flock-technology-under-scrutiny/) on suspicion of accessing a Flock Safety ALPR database to stalk his estranged wife

### Misidentification Incidents

- **San Francisco (2009):** Denise Green, an African-American city worker, was pulled over, handcuffed at gunpoint, forced to her knees, and searched because her car was misidentified as stolen due to a license plate reader error. This led the U.S. Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals to rule that technology alone can't be the basis of such a stop—but this doesn't apply everywhere.
- **Aurora, Colorado:** [A group of Black youths were traumatized by police](https://www.denver7.com/news/local-news/aurora-police-detain-black-family-after-mistaking-their-vehicle-as-stolen) after an ALPR system incorrectly identified their vehicle as stolen.

### Data Security Concerns

Aggregate data stored for lengthy periods becomes more invasive and susceptible to misuse and breach. Even Customs & Border Protection saw its ALPR vendor, Perceptics, [hacked with data published online](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2019/07/02/border-surveillance-subcontractor-suspended-after-cyberattack-misuse-traveler-images/).

**Recommended protections:**
- Sensible retention limits
- Specific policies about who can access data
- Audit and control processes
- Ideally, police should retain no information when a passing vehicle doesn't match a hot list

### Vulnerable Populations

ALPRs can be used to target:
- [Immigrant communities](https://www.aclu.org/news/immigrants-rights/documents-reveal-ice-using-driver-location-data)
- People seeking or providing [reproductive health services](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2022/09/automated-license-plate-readers-threaten-abortion-access-heres-how-policymakers)

## EFF's Work on ALPR

EFF has been investigating and combating ALPR privacy threats through public records requests, litigation, and legislative advocacy since 2012.

### Key Litigation

- **[ACLU of Southern California and EFF v. LAPD and LASD](https://www.eff.org/cases/automated-license-plate-readers-aclu-eff-v-lapd-lasd):** Sued after agencies refused to hand over ALPR data, claiming records were exempt as investigative records. In 2017, the California Supreme Court ruled in EFF and ACLU's favor.

- **[Lagleva v. Marin County Sheriff](https://www.eff.org/cases/lagleva-v-marin):** Sued in 2021 on behalf of local activists after the sheriff shared ALPR data with out-of-state agencies, including ICE, in violation of California law. Settlement required the sheriff to cease sharing data outside California.

- Additional briefs filed in cases in [Virginia](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2017/02/virginia-supreme-court-should-protect-drivers-license-plate-surveillance), [Massachusetts, and Nevada](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2020/05/courts-issue-rulings-two-cases-challenging-law-enforcement-searches-license-plate)

### Legislative Advocacy

In 2015, California passed S.B. 34, requiring ALPR users to:
- Protect data
- Maintain access logs
- Hold public meetings before starting an ALPR program
- Implement usage and privacy policies
- Prohibit selling, sharing, or transferring ALPR data except to other public agencies

EFF has collected [ALPR policies across California](https://www.eff.org/pages/california-automated-license-plate-reader-policies) and [exposed agencies failing to comply](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2016/04/here-are-79-policies-california-surveillance-tech-where-are-other-90).

In 2019, EFF [successfully advocated](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2019/06/victory-california-orders-state-audit-automated-license-plate-readers) for a California Legislature audit of law enforcement agencies' compliance with S.B. 34. The California State Auditor [issued a damning report](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2020/02/california-auditor-releases-damning-report-about-law-enforcements-use-automated) substantiating concerns that agencies weren't following the law, failed to enact adequate policies, and shared data too broadly.

# Protect Your Information and Devices While Traveling

Traveling exposes you, your devices, and your data to significant risks. Laptops and other devices can fail, get stolen, lost, damaged, or impounded. Data transmitted from public access points—such as airport and hotel WiFi networks—can be intercepted. Using untrusted systems may expose you to keyloggers and other attacks designed to capture sensitive information.

When crossing any national border, you have few if any rights to protest or refuse when a border control or customs officer wants to inspect your luggage, including data stored on your laptop, mobile device, digital camera, or recorder. In many countries, including the US, a customs officer needs no "probable cause" or warrant to search your device or confiscate it for further inspection.

This guide provides recommendations to prepare for your travels and minimize risks for your data and devices.

---

## Basic Security Practices

Implement these general security practices with special care when planning a journey:

- **Set strong, unique passwords** for all your accounts before traveling
- **Enable multi-factor authentication** on email, social media, and other important accounts
- **Update your operating system and software**—outdated systems are more vulnerable to attacks
- **Run updated antivirus software** on your devices
- **Avoid phishing attacks** by not opening unrequested attachments or links in emails or messages
- **Research your destination country**: Check if the internet is censored, if VPNs and circumvention tools are legal, if encryption is banned, and if laws require you to surrender passwords or encryption keys to border police
- **Set automatic screen lock** when your device is unattended, and require a password at login and when resuming from standby
- **Watch for shoulder surfers**: Be aware of who can see you type passwords, passphrases, and PINs—look for cameras and protect your keystrokes from observers

## Prepare for Crossing Borders

### Minimize the Data You Carry

- Determine what data you actually need while traveling—leave everything else behind
- Copy unneeded data to a fully encrypted computer or hard drive at home, then delete it from travel devices
- Consider storing encrypted data or a system image on a secure cloud service, traveling with a minimal laptop, and downloading what you need at your destination

**Why this matters**: Data you don't carry cannot be lost or stolen. Border searches may flag potentially illegal content (including authorized copies of copyrighted material or certain images), which could result in device confiscation.

### Use an Alternative Email Address

- Log out of your regular email accounts
- Create a new web-based email account specifically for your travels
- Use this address for non-sensitive communications and dispose of it afterward

**Why this matters**: Border agents may inspect your mailbox. Using a temporary travel email limits exposure if the account is compromised through untrusted connections.

### Remove Accounts from Your Devices

- Log out of all accounts before crossing the border
- Remove or hide social media and other sensitive apps
- Consider creating secondary "clean" profiles on major platforms, populated with harmless content in advance

**Why this matters**: Border agents may inspect social media accounts on your devices. Logging out and removing apps prevents access to potentially compromising content.

### Clean Up Your Web Browsers

- Log out of all accounts (email, social networks, etc.)
- Clear temporary internet files and browsing history

### Clean Up Your Chat Apps

- Evaluate which chat apps you have installed—in some countries, certain apps (like Signal) may raise suspicion at checkpoints
- Search for and remove sensitive conversations from your chat history
- Rename groups you administer to use non-descriptive names
- Enable disappearing messages in sensitive chats
- Consider uninstalling messenger apps if needed, though having no messaging apps may also appear suspicious

### Hide Sensitive Files or Folders (Advanced)

- **Android**: Use the Safe folder in the Files app
- **iOS**: Hide photos in a Hidden album
- **Tella app** (Android/iOS): Hide sensitive files and camouflage the app as a calculator (Android)
- **VeraCrypt** (computer): Create a hidden encrypted folder

**Why this matters**: If you must carry sensitive files, hiding them in encrypted containers provides additional protection during border inspections.

## Use Encryption to Protect Your Data and Communications

### Check Encryption Legality

Before traveling, research whether the import, export, or use of cryptography is legal in your destination countries.

If encryption is illegal, the safest approach is to:
1. Encrypt all needed information
2. Upload it to secure cloud storage
3. Delete it from your travel devices
4. Download it after arrival

### Encrypt Your Devices

- Enable full-device encryption on all devices
- Encrypt external storage devices (USB drives, external hard drives)

**Why this matters**: Encryption ensures that if someone accesses your device without your passphrase, they see only random data. However, encryption makes data recovery harder if your drive is damaged—maintain good backups.

### Protect Your Cryptographic Keys (Advanced)

If you use PGP encryption, your private keys are among your most valuable data. Protect them carefully:
- Never allow devices containing keys to be in others' hands unsupervised
- Consider storing private keys on an encrypted USB drive that you keep on your person at all times, rather than on your main devices

## Public Access Security

### Shared Computers

Always consider shared computers insecure. To reduce risks:

- **Physical keyloggers**: Bring a small USB keyboard to plug into untrusted computers
- **Software keyloggers/spyware**: Boot from a custom live USB with your own trusted operating system, bypassing the computer's standard setup

**Why this matters**: Keyloggers and spyware can capture everything you do. Hardware keyloggers are dongles between the keyboard and computer—check for these before using any unfamiliar keyboard.

### Protect Connections on Untrusted Networks

Hotel WiFi, conference networks, and public hotspots are all potentially compromised. Protect yourself:

- Configure your browser to always use HTTPS
- Install one or more VPNs before your trip
- Use protective browser extensions, especially ad blockers like uBlock Origin
- Pay attention to security warnings when connecting—they may indicate a machine-in-the-middle (MITM) attack

**Why this matters**: Rogue network nodes can intercept site addresses, email contents, and divert your traffic to malicious sites. HTTPS and VPNs encrypt your data in transit.

## Internet Censorship and Blockages

- Learn how the internet works and how it can be censored
- Research circumvention tools and techniques before traveling
- If you're a human rights defender, journalist, or activist traveling to countries with sophisticated censorship and surveillance, contact **Front Line Defenders** or **Access Now Digital Security Helpline** for country-specific guidance

## Mobile Phone Security

### Travel with a Different Phone

Mobile phones should generally be considered insecure. Best practices:

- Leave your primary phone at home
- Travel with a different device that doesn't contain your regular data
- If you must bring your work phone, back up all data to a separate device and remove it from the phone

### Use Secure Messaging Apps

- Use end-to-end encrypted messaging apps for sensitive communications
- Verify that your contacts also use secure apps before relying on them

### Hide Sensitive Apps (Advanced)

- **Android**: Use Private Space to hide sensitive apps
- **iOS**: Use built-in app hiding features

# Body-Worn Cameras

Body-worn cameras are small recording devices worn by police officers to capture video and audio of their encounters with the public. While often promoted as tools for police accountability, these devices also pose significant privacy threats and can expand government surveillance without meaningful oversight.

## How Body-Worn Cameras Work

Body-worn cameras can be clipped onto a police officer's uniform or worn as a headset. When activated, they record video and audio of law enforcement encounters. The footage is typically saved with:

- Time and date stamps
- GPS coordinates
- Officer identification information

### Activation Methods

Modern body cameras offer various triggering options:

- **Manual activation** by the officer
- **Real-time video streaming** to command centers
- **Bluetooth triggers** for automatic recording
- **Automatic activation** when:
  - Cruiser sirens are turned on
  - Vehicle exceeds certain speeds
  - Nearby dashboard or body cameras are switched on
  - A firearm is drawn from its holster (via wireless holster sensors)

### Data Storage

Footage is uploaded to external databases maintained by:
- Police agencies directly
- Third-party vendors (such as Axon's Evidence.com or Amazon Web Services)

At least one company has developed body-worn cameras with live facial recognition capabilities, raising additional civil liberties concerns.

## Data Collection

When activated, body-worn cameras collect:

- **Video footage** of people in the officer's vicinity
- **Audio recordings** of conversations and ambient sound
- **Metadata** including date, time, and GPS location
- **Facial data** that can be analyzed using face recognition technology

This data can capture people in public spaces, private homes, and sensitive situations—often without the knowledge or consent of those being recorded.

## Current Deployment

According to EFF's Atlas of Surveillance, more than **4,517 police departments** across the United States use body-worn cameras. Many states mandate their use by law enforcement officers interacting with the public.

### Common Recording Situations

Police may record during:
- Stop and frisk encounters
- Property searches
- Vehicle stops and pursuits
- Witness interviews
- Issuing summonses
- Making arrests

### Policy Variations

The Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights maintains a [Body-worn Camera Scorecard](https://www.bwcscorecard.org/) documenting significant variations in policies across jurisdictions. Key findings include:

- **52 of 68 major city police departments** have body-worn camera programs with policies
- Only **4 departments** (Cincinnati, Las Vegas, Parker, and Washington D.C.) provide recorded individuals access to all footage
- Only **12 departments** prohibit officers from reviewing footage before filing initial reports for critical incidents
- Only **7 departments** (Baltimore, Baltimore County, Boston, Cincinnati, Montgomery County, Parker, and Seattle) limit the use of biometric technologies with camera footage

## Privacy and Civil Liberties Threats

### Surveillance Expansion

Body-worn cameras can massively expand government surveillance capabilities:

- Footage may be kept indefinitely
- Recordings can be subjected to facial recognition analysis
- Data can be combined with other surveillance technologies
- Thousands of hours of footage can be algorithmically analyzed and stored in searchable databases

### Disproportionate Impact

Given ongoing inequities in the criminal justice system, body-worn cameras disproportionately affect:

- People of color
- Immigrants
- Other vulnerable communities
- Protesters and activists exercising First Amendment rights

### Privacy in Private Spaces

When police enter homes with body-worn cameras, they may capture:

- Children and family members
- People in various states of undress
- Victims of domestic violence or sexual assault
- Other innocent private domestic activity

Many department policies fail to adequately address these privacy concerns.

### Data Security Risks

Long-term storage of footage creates risks of:

- Data breaches
- Employee misuse
- Unauthorized access
- Long-term surveillance of individuals

### Access Asymmetry

A significant problem exists in who can access footage:

- Police can typically access video immediately
- Recorded civilians and their attorneys often cannot
- Some vendors require defense attorneys to agree to restrictive terms of service to access discovery materials

### Selective Release

Police departments often have discretion to release portions of video selectively, which can be used as a public relations strategy to deflect misconduct claims rather than promote transparency.

### Officer Discretion Problems

When officers control camera activation:

- They often neglect to record when required
- Compliance with recording policies can be low
- Failures to record during use-of-force incidents frequently go undisciplined
- Officers may review footage before making statements, allowing them to tailor their accounts

## EFF's Recommended Safeguards

EFF opposes body-worn cameras unless strict rules are in place to protect privacy and advance police accountability:

### 1. Mandated Activation
Officers must turn cameras on at the start of all investigative encounters with civilians and leave them on until the encounter ends.

### 2. No Political Spying
Officers must not use cameras to gather information about people exercising their First Amendment rights.

### 3. Limited Retention
Footage must be stored for a few months to allow injured civilians time to request it, then promptly destroyed to prevent long-term surveillance.

### 4. No Face Surveillance
Facial recognition technology is too dangerous for police use, especially in connection with body-worn cameras.

### 5. Restricted Officer Review
For footage depicting use of force or subject to civilian complaint, officers must make an initial statement before reviewing the footage.

### 6. Public Access to Footage
- Recorded individuals must have access to footage depicting them
- Everyone must have access to footage depicting use of force
- Courts must weigh privacy costs against accountability benefits for other misconduct claims

### 7. Rigorous Enforcement
- Footage collected in violation of rules must be excluded from court
- Missing footage should create an evidentiary presumption favoring the civilian

### 8. Community Control
Police must not deploy body-worn cameras unless the city council votes to approve them after community input. Many communities may choose to reject them.

## Major Vendors

The body-worn camera market includes numerous manufacturers:

**Primary vendors:** Axon (formerly Taser), Motorola, Panasonic, WatchGuard

**Other vendors:** Aventura, Black Mamba, BrickHouse Security, COBAN, Digital Ally, GoPro, L-3 Mobile-Vision, Patrol Eyes, Reveal Media, Safety Vision, VIEVU, Wolfcom, Zepcam, and others.

Some vendors also provide cloud storage services for footage, including partnerships with Amazon Web Services.

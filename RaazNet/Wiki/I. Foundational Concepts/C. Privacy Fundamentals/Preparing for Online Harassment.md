# Preparing for Online Harassment

Journalists are increasingly attacked online for their work, both by persistent individuals and collective mobs. Harassment takes many forms, with journalists, their families, and colleagues receiving barrages of hate-filled messages. Sometimes these threats escalate to the physical world when harassers attempt to identify a reporter's address and location.

**It's far easier to prepare defenses in advance than while experiencing the stress of harassment.** Preparing for these situations puts you in control and can be deeply empowering.

The following resources help journalists prepare for harassment by taking steps to cultivate digital security and psychological safety.

## Protecting Your Personal Information

### [Yael Grauer's Big Ass Data Broker Opt-Out List](https://github.com/yaelwrites/Big-Ass-Data-Broker-Opt-Out-List)

One of the key ways harassers identify someone's location and contact information is through data broker sites (e.g., Acxiom, Radaris). These services aggregate information from:
- State and public court records
- Publicly accessible social media
- Personal data sold by companies trading in customer data

**As a preventative measure**, you can send opt-out requests to data brokers to remove your personal information. This comprehensive list documents opt-out procedures for dozens of data broker websites.

### [NYT Open's How to Dox Yourself on the Internet](https://open.nytimes.com/how-to-dox-yourself-on-the-internet-d2892b4c5954)

From The New York Times' Information Security team, this guide provides structured guidance to:
- Determine your digital footprint
- Lock down social media accounts
- Find your personal information on search engines and data brokers
- Configure ideal privacy and security settings

## Key Takeaways

1. **Prepare proactively** — setting up defenses before harassment occurs is significantly easier than responding during a crisis
2. **Reduce your digital footprint** — opt out of data brokers and audit your online presence
3. **Document everything** — if harassment occurs, thorough documentation supports all response options
4. **Seek support** — multiple organizations offer direct assistance and resources
5. **Prioritize psychological safety** — harassment response includes mental health considerations

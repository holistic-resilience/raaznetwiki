# Building New Approaches to Security

Defining a comprehensive security strategy differs significantly from taking an improvised approach. While many instinctive reactions to threats keep us safe, some may be ineffective or even harmful.

Building an effective security strategy involves developing new tactics and capacities that maintain and expand our space for human rights work.

## Three Core Security Strategies

### Acceptance Strategies

Acceptance strategies involve engaging with allies, neutral parties, and opponents to foster tolerance and support for human rights activities. Key activities include:

- Advocacy and campaigning
- Diplomacy
- Public education

### Protection Strategies

Protection strategies emphasize implementing new methods and practices to defend our working space. This includes:

- Leveraging ally support for protection
- Bridging gaps in existing security practices
- Learning defensive techniques and tools

### Deterrence Strategies

Deterrence strategies focus on raising the cost of attacks against defenders or their work. These costs may be:

- Monetary
- Reputational
- Political

Effective deterrence requires a well-developed understanding of which allies can help raise the cost of attacks.

> **Note:** Most security plans incorporate activities from two or all three categories, depending on context and identified threats.

## Building Capacity: Key Elements

Effective capacity building requires attention to five interconnected elements:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Well-being** | Physical and mental conditions for active learning, including adequate time and safe space |
| **Attitudes** | Subjective valuation of the learning process; can be affected by stress, fear, and trauma |
| **Knowledge** | Understanding of political, economic, social, technological, legal, and environmental context |
| **Skills** | Abilities to engage with your environment—physical fitness, advocacy, technical tools, self-care |
| **Resources** | Financial and material support that enables improvement in other areas |

## Working with External Trainers and Consultants

External support can be valuable for developing security plans and skills. When engaging consultants:

**What to look for:**
- Trainers who empower you regarding your own security situation
- Focus on tools and tactics relevant to your specific activities
- Collaborative approach involving group members in analysis

**Red flags to avoid:**
- Consultants who conduct analysis without involving your team
- Anyone who makes decisions for you
- Promises of immediate security fixes

### Selecting Trainers

Consider these factors when choosing trainings or trainers:

1. **Referrals** — Seek recommendations from trusted colleagues and organizations
2. **Clear expectations** — Define what you want to learn while respecting trainer expertise
3. **Relevant experience** — Ensure they have appropriate background knowledge
4. **Logistics** — Consider language, timing, location, and follow-up options
5. **Group buy-in** — Discuss within your team to ensure engagement from all members

## Resource Considerations

Improving security capacities often has financial implications:

- Hardware replacement costs
- Training expenses
- Tool subscriptions
- Consultation fees

Various organizations offer financial support to human rights defenders with security concerns. Budget for security as a core operational need rather than an afterthought.

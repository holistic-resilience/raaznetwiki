# Data Brokers and Privacy Threats

Data brokers are companies that collect and sell consumers' personal information, often obtained by purchasing data from other businesses and scraping public records. That unsettled feeling you get when you are served an all-too-accurate advertisement? Data brokers at work.

## The Problem

While many Americans feel creeped out by this relentless tracking, America's relationship with data brokers has been one step forward, two steps back. Killing off much-needed regulation is just the latest setback; the federal government has long been in on the game of purchasing location data.

### Government Involvement

Is this a violation of our Fourth Amendment rights? Probably. This data broker loophole directly affects journalists and whistleblowers, as the government can purchase data that would otherwise require a warrant to obtain.

## Signs of Progress

Some positive developments are emerging:

- **State-level action**: Some states are making progress toward closing the loophole that links data brokers and law enforcement
- **Congressional pressure**: Senators are holding 30-plus brokers accountable after news reports found them hiding their opt-out pages from Google search results

## How to Protect Yourself

### 1. Understand the Landscape

Learn more about how your data is tracked, bought, and sold. Knowing what's going on behind the scenes will help you right-size your defenses.

### 2. Use an Opt-Out Service

Consider signing up for an opt-out service like Abine's DeleteMe. While it comes with a yearly subscription, Consumer Reports has published a helpful comparison guide to help you find an opt-out service that fits your needs and budget.

### 3. DIY Opt-Out Approach

For those who prefer a hands-on approach, check out Yael Grauer's **Big Ass Data Broker Opt-Out List** on GitHub. If you're already using an opt-out service, reviewing Yael's list against your service's reports can help identify gaps in coverage.

## Key Takeaways

- Data brokers collect and sell personal information from multiple sources
- Government agencies purchase this data, potentially circumventing Fourth Amendment protections
- Journalists and whistleblowers face particular risks from this surveillance infrastructure
- Both paid services and DIY approaches exist for opting out of data broker databases

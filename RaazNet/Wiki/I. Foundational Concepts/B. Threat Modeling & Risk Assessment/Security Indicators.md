# Security Indicators

Security indicators are anything out of the ordinary that may impact your security situation. They range from concrete incidents—such as threats, attacks on partner organizations, or suspicious behavior—to subtle changes in device behavior or personal health and well-being.

What these indicators share is their potential to signal a change in your security situation. They should be shared and analyzed among trusted contacts to establish their meaning.

We naturally monitor situations and notice when we feel unsafe. Developing the practice of analyzing security indicators means taking a more deliberate, organized approach. Regular noting, recording, sharing, and analyzing of indicators with colleagues and allies helps maintain objectivity about your security situation.

## Types of Security Indicators

### Socio-Political Environment

Monitoring the political, economic, social, technological, legal, and environmental context of your work is vital for security and operational success.

**Key monitoring practices:**
- Talk to trusted friends, colleagues, and partner organizations
- Follow and document relevant news
- Meet with experts when beginning work in new areas

### Digital Security

Digital security indicators often require deliberate attention. With increasing electronic surveillance against human rights defenders, learning to identify digital anomalies is essential.

**Establish a baseline** of normal behavior for your digital hardware (computers, phones, routers, hard drives, servers) and monitor for changes.

**Regular monitoring activities:**
- Scan devices with anti-malware programs
- Check firewall settings and logs
- Review running processes and installed programs for unauthorized items
- Enable two-step authentication for online services where possible
- Mark devices physically (UV marker or tamper-tape) and photograph them to detect tampering

If you suspect something is wrong, consult a trusted expert.

### Daily Activities

Security indicators in daily life vary based on individual habits, routines, and work patterns. Consider:

- How you travel between locations
- Regular activities throughout your day
- Devices you carry
- People you interact with

Understanding your normal patterns helps you identify what factors could signal a security change.

### Health and Well-Being

Changes in health can indicate reactions to external problems like increased stress, which directly impacts your security situation. Activists often push through difficulties, risking lasting health damage and impaired security analysis.

Adopt a methodical approach to monitoring your physical, emotional, and psychological state. Changes may occur gradually, making regular self-assessment valuable.

## Sharing and Analyzing Security Indicators

Sharing indicators with trusted contacts helps identify patterns and verify your perception of threats. Since security incidents are sensitive—both as information and emotionally—discuss them in safe spaces.

**Recommended practices:**
- Include security indicators as a regular meeting agenda item
- Create structured processes for analyzing concrete incidents
- Handle health and well-being indicators in informal, supportive settings

## Analysis Framework for Security Incidents

For significant indicators, particularly concrete incidents, use these questions as an analysis foundation:

1. **What happened?**
2. **When did it happen?**
3. **Where did it happen?**
4. **Who was affected?**
5. **Was gender-based violence involved?** Consider both physical and psychological factors.
6. **Who was responsible?** (for aggressions)
7. **Why did this happen?** Focus on establishing facts rather than accusations.
8. **What was its origin?** Was this related to common crime, environmental factors, or your work and activism?

## Recording Security Indicators

Create a secure space to record indicators in detail for later sharing and analysis. This might be a document or spreadsheet that you review periodically (weekly or monthly) to identify trends.

Use the analysis questions above to structure your records. **Treat this information as highly sensitive.**

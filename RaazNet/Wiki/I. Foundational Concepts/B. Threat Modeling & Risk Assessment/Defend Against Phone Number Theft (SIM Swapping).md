# Defend Against Phone Number Theft (SIM Swapping)

## What is SIM Swapping?

Your phone number is extremely valuable to attackers because it's commonly used to log into financial services and receive two-factor authentication codes. In a SIM swap attack, criminals impersonate you to your phone carrier, convincing customer service that they have a new phone and need your number transferred to their device.

Once successful, attackers can:
- Intercept SMS-based two-factor authentication codes
- Break into your online accounts (banking, email, social media)
- Commit financial fraud or steal cryptocurrency

## AT&T's New Protection

AT&T has implemented [Wireless Account Lock](https://www.att.com/support/article/wireless/000102016/), a feature that disables several account changes to reduce the risk of phone number hijacking.

## What You Can Do

### Enable Carrier Protection Against SIM Swapping

Check if your phone carrier supports SIM swap protection:

| Carrier | Protection Feature |
|---------|-------------------|
| **AT&T** | [Wireless Account Lock](https://www.att.com/support/article/wireless/000102016/) |
| **T-Mobile** | [Account Fraud Protection](https://www.t-mobile.com/support/plans-features/help-with-t-mobile-account-fraud) |
| **Verizon** | [SIM Swapping Protection](https://www.verizon.com/about/account-security/sim-swapping) |

If your carrier doesn't offer this feature, contact customer service to manually add a PIN to your account. Note that this isn't bulletproof—representatives can still override your PIN in some circumstances.

### Use Stronger Two-Factor Authentication

Since SMS codes can be intercepted through SIM swapping, consider upgrading to more secure 2FA methods:

- **Authentication apps** (like Google Authenticator or Authy)
- **Hardware security keys** (like YubiKey)
- **Passkeys** (where supported)

For detailed setup instructions, see Freedom of the Press Foundation's [2FA beginner's guide](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/2fa-beginners/).

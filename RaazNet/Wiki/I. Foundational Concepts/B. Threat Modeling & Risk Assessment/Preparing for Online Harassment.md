# Preparing for Online Harassment

Journalists are increasingly attacked online for their work, both by persistent individuals and collective mobs. Harassment takes many forms, with journalists, their families, and colleagues receiving barrages of hate-filled messages. Sometimes these threats escalate to the physical world when harassers attempt to identify a reporter's address and location.

**It's far easier to prepare defenses in advance than while experiencing the stress of harassment.** Preparing for these situations puts you in control and can be deeply empowering.

The following resources help journalists prepare for harassment by taking steps to cultivate digital security and psychological safety.

---

## Comprehensive Harassment Guides

### [PEN America's Online Harassment Field Manual](https://onlineharassmentfieldmanual.pen.org/)

PEN America maintains an exhaustive set of resources covering:
- **Preventive measures** when anticipating harassment
- **Response strategies** for active harassment situations
- **Documentation guidance** for recording abuse
- **Psychological safety** recommendations
- **Law enforcement considerations**
- **Guidance for employers and supporters**

### [OnlineSOS Action Center](https://onlinesos.org/action-center)

OnlineSOS provides succinct, actionable lists organized by:
- **Type of harassment** you're experiencing
- **Type of information** you want to protect

This resource prioritizes getting accurate guidance without filler.

## Protecting Your Personal Information

### [Yael Grauer's Big Ass Data Broker Opt-Out List](https://github.com/yaelwrites/Big-Ass-Data-Broker-Opt-Out-List)

Harassers often locate targets through data broker sites (e.g., Acxiom, Radaris) that aggregate personal information from:
- State and public court records
- Publicly accessible social media
- Personal data sold by other companies

This comprehensive list provides opt-out procedures for dozens of data broker websites, allowing you to request removal of your personal information.

### [NYT Open's How to Dox Yourself on the Internet](https://open.nytimes.com/how-to-dox-yourself-on-the-internet-d2892b4c5954)

From The New York Times' Information Security team, this guide provides structured guidance for:
- Determining your digital footprint
- Finding your personal information on search engines and data brokers
- Locking down social media accounts
- Configuring ideal privacy and security settings

## Key Takeaways

1. **Prepare proactively** — Building defenses before harassment occurs is more effective than reacting under stress
2. **Reduce your digital footprint** — Opt out of data brokers and audit your online presence
3. **Lock down social media** — Review privacy settings across all platforms
4. **Know your resources** — Bookmark support organizations before you need them
5. **Document everything** — If harassment occurs, thorough documentation supports response options

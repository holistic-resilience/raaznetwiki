# Protect Against Physical Threats

We do a lot of digital work to protect sensitive information, but that is only one aspect of information security. The work you do to protect your valuable devices and documents can be undone in an instant if your devices are lost, stolen, tampered with, confiscated, or damaged. Planning for physical security is as important as protecting your devices digitally.

Careful risk assessment, maintaining a safe work environment, and writing up a security policy can help you avoid physical disasters. Even if you are not working with a formal organization, it is a good idea to write out guidelines and response plans for yourself, your household, and the people you work with.

Both criminals and politically motivated attackers may have reasons to target your data. They might be seeking financial information, sensitive data related to your work, or personal details they can use to intimidate, blackmail, or impersonate you. You could also be targeted due to information received from others, such as contact lists, interviews, testimonies, or identifying information on victims or witnesses.

> **Important:** When planning your protection, always consider the well-being of all the people involved. See [Front Line Defenders' resources for well-being and stress management](https://www.frontlinedefenders.org/en/resources-wellbeing-stress-management).

While working on your physical protection, you will exchange and store sensitive information. Make sure to protect both [your sensitive information](https://securityinabox.org/en/files/secure-file-storage) and [the privacy of your online communications](https://securityinabox.org/en/communication/private-communication/).

## Think About Inexpensive, Low-Tech Security Measures

- Money is not the only way to achieve protection. Your actions, procedures, teamwork, preparation, planning, practice, time, and learning are all free of cost.
- Consider creative, low-tech protection solutions: A dog can be as effective as the latest surveillance camera. Ancient Japanese castles used intentionally creaky floors to detect intruders.
- Plan for many different security measures. Like threads in a rope, the more layers you have, the stronger your security.

**Why this matters:** Using many different security measures is called "defense in depth." Each measure may be defeated or fail, but the combination makes you more protected.

## Create a Physical Security Policy

### Working with Colleagues and Family

- If you live with others or share an office, talk to them about security and determine what behaviors you can expect from one another and from visitors.
- Get to know your neighbors—depending on your security climate, they may become allies or potential concerns.
- Set aside dedicated time to work on a policy document.
- Schedule regular reviews of the policy.
- Plan regular security capacity-building exercises.
- Establish security briefing procedures and expectations about sharing information on incidents.
- Store and back up your policy documents in ways that are quickly accessible.
- Plan how you will present your policy to people joining your organization.

**Resources:**
- [Front Line Defenders' Workbook on Security](https://www.frontlinedefenders.org/en/resource-publication/workbook-security-practical-steps-human-rights-defenders-risk)
- [Access Now Digital Security Helpline's security policy templates](https://gitlab.com/AccessNowHelpline/helpline_documentation_resources/-/tree/master/templates/Organizational_Security_Policies-Templates)

**Why this matters:** Emergencies often impact our ability to think clearly. Writing up plans in advance ensures everyone knows how to act on important details when stress is high.

## Emergency Communication Plan

Your plan should address:

- **Support network:** Persons who can come to your assistance
- **Emergency contacts:** Staff emergency contacts and medical conditions
- **Disaster response:** Whom to contact for fire, flood, or natural disasters
- **Security incidents:** How to respond to burglary or office raids
- **Device loss:** Steps to take if a device is lost or stolen
- **Data disclosure:** Who to notify if sensitive information is disclosed or misplaced
- **Data recovery:** How to recover information from off-site backups
- **Emergency repairs:** How to perform important emergency repairs
- **Service providers:** Contact information for electric power, water, and internet companies

---

## What Goes in Your Security Plan?

Consider documenting:

- How might your information be lost or compromised?
- What would happen if it was?
- What devices do you use and where are they physically located?
- What is your policy on personal devices for work (BYOD)?
- What communication channels do you use and how?
- How do you store important or sensitive information?
  - Include whether data is encrypted, where backups exist, and who has access to decryption keys
- How do you destroy sensitive data when no longer needed?
- What is your travel security plan?
- What will you do in different emergency scenarios?

**Why this matters:** Any piece of information might be vulnerable in multiple ways. For example, files on a USB stick could be vulnerable to malware, loss, or theft. Include each possibility in your plan.

## Office Access Policy

### Physical Security Layers

Think of your physical security as having these layers, and plan protection for each:

1. **Perimeter** (building exterior, grounds)
2. **Building access** (doors, windows, entry points)
3. **Internal zones** (restricted areas, secure rooms)
4. **Device-level** (individual computers, servers)

### Policy Elements

- Rules about key distribution
- Monitoring systems (cameras, alarms)
- Procedures for deliveries, maintenance, and cleaning staff
- Decide which parts of your space should be restricted to visitors
- Arrange rooms for greater privacy and security

### Device Security

- Purchase devices from known, trusted vendors (Apple, Google, Samsung, Sony, etc.)
- Avoid no-name brands that may come preloaded with malware or lack security updates
- Keep USB, ethernet, and other ports on printers, monitors, and projectors out of reach of public areas

**Why this matters:** Hackers consider "social engineering"—convincing you to let them access physical spaces near your devices—as part of their toolkit. A clear access policy limits this possibility.

## Working Outside Your Home or Office

- Treat public wifi and internet cafes as insecure
- Carry your laptop in something that doesn't look like a laptop bag
- Keep devices near you at all times—don't leave them unattended while charging
- Consider traveling with a security cable and practice finding workspaces near objects to attach it
- Thieves often exploit meal times and restroom visits to steal unattended equipment
- Remember that hotel safe deposit boxes are accessible to staff with master keys

## Surveillance Cameras and Motion Sensors

### Considerations

- Low-tech solutions like bells, alarms, or dogs can be as effective as cameras
- CCTV cameras require someone to monitor them or review footage
- Consider whether cameras could put those nearby at risk if your adversary gained access
- Balance this risk against your need to know if your space has been searched or burglarized

### Recommendations

- **Avoid** "Internet of Things" devices like Amazon's Ring—they are vulnerable to spying and may share footage with law enforcement without permission
- **Consider** [Haven](https://securityinabox.org/en/assess-plan/tools/#haven)—created specifically for human rights defenders to monitor spaces with control over their own data
- If using surveillance cameras, transmit video encrypted to a separate location
- Plan how to protect physical access to videos, how long to keep them, and how to securely delete them

## Protect Your Local Network

### Wired vs. Wireless

- Ethernet cables may be safer than wireless—wifi can be breached without physical access
- Avoid running ethernet cables outside your building or physically protected areas

### Wireless Security

- Use a [strong passphrase](https://securityinabox.org/en/passwords/passwords) for your wireless network
- [Set your network to use WPA2 or WPA3 security](https://securityinabox.org/en/phones-and-computers/malware/#secure-your-router)

**Why this matters:** Anyone in range of your wifi signal can potentially spy on your network. A weak or missing password makes anyone within range a potential intruder.

### Additional Network Protection

| Action | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| Install a firewall router | Separates your network from ISP's router; prevents ISP from seeing connected devices |
| Disconnect unnecessary devices | TVs, cameras, IoT devices carry security risks |
| Change your wifi network name | Avoid names that identify you, your organization, or location |
| Create a separate guest wifi | Easier to change passwords; protects main network |
| Lock up networking equipment | Prevents tampering with servers, routers, switches, modems |
| Encrypt your servers | Protects files if servers are seized, confiscated, or need repair |

## Prevent Accidents and Outages

Computers, networking equipment, and data storage devices are delicate. Electric fluctuations, extreme temperatures, dust, and moisture can all cause damage.

### Power Protection

- **Use grounded electrical sockets** to prolong equipment life and reduce failure risk
- **Use surge protectors** (check for maximum voltage and joule rating)
- **Consider power filters or line conditioners** if electricity is unstable
- **Install Uninterruptible Power Supplies (UPSs)** for servers and desktop computers
- **Position power strips** where they won't be accidentally unplugged

### Before Moving to a New Building

- Test the power supply before plugging in important equipment
- If power behaves poorly with lamps and fans, it will likely damage digital devices
- Have a trusted electrician review the cabling

### Environmental Considerations

- Use high-quality cables, surge protectors, and power strips
- Ensure adequate ventilation for computers—don't house them near radiators or heating vents
- Consider backup lighting (flashlights, battery-powered lights, solar-charged lights)

## Protect Against Theft, Tampering, and Confiscation

### Physical Locks

- Install high-quality locks on doors and windows
- Consider a laptop safe or locking cabinet for sensitive documents and equipment
- Lock devices themselves when possible

**Why this matters:** Locks act as outer layers of security, helping delay and detect intrusions—not only stop them.

### Device Placement

- Avoid placing important devices in easily accessible locations (hallways, reception areas, near windows or doors)
- Protect your building's main electric switches with locks

**Why this matters:** Equipment in easy-to-access areas can be stolen or have malware installed. Attackers may cut electricity before entering, adding to confusion.

### Leaving Devices Behind vs. Taking Them

Consider your specific situation:

- Is it likely someone will raid your space while you're out?
- Is it more likely you'll be detained and searched with devices on you?
- Will you travel, cross borders, or attend protests where arrest is possible?

**Detection techniques:**
- Use security tape to cover USB ports and disk drives
- Scatter small objects over your device and photograph their position
- Leave a thread or hair on the device (if no breeze) to detect movement
- Use a monitoring app like [Haven](https://securityinabox.org/en/assess-plan/tools/#haven)
- Consider a locked box to secure devices during searches

### Device Registration

If your local law enforcement is trustworthy, registering device model and serial numbers can help recover stolen devices.

## Consider What Can Be Seen

### Clean Desk Policy

Ensure you and colleagues don't leave sensitive information visible:
- Passwords
- Paper calendars and planners
- Journals and address books
- Sticky notes with sensitive information

### Screen Privacy

- Position screens so they cannot be seen from outside (account for windows, doors, waiting areas)
- Consider ways to avoid using devices where someone could look over your shoulder
- Purchase privacy screens for laptops, monitors, tablets, and smartphones

### Location Privacy

If you need to hide your location, remember these clues can reveal where you are:
- Background details in video calls
- Ambient sounds during calls
- Visible landmarks or identifying features

### Camera Security

Cover cameras on your devices when not in use to prevent remote spying. [Read more on camera covers](https://securityinabox.org/en/phones-and-computers/windows/#use-a-camera-cover).

**Why this matters:** Adversaries may not need physical or electronic access if they can see your information by observing your space or device.

---

## Dispose of Sensitive Information Securely

- Set a regular schedule to securely erase files
- Learn how to securely delete files, wipe blank space, and dispose of old devices in the [guide on destroying sensitive information](https://securityinabox.org/en/files/destroy-sensitive-information)
- Incorporate secure deletion instructions for each device type (Android, iOS, Windows, macOS, Linux) into your security plan

### Hard Drive Destruction

When disposing of computer hard drives:
- Use a power drill to destroy the platters
- Drive nails through the drive
- Use a hammer to deliver strong blows

**Do not:** burn drives, pour acid on them, or microwave them.

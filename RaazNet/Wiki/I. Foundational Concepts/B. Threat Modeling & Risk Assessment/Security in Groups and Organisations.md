# Security in Groups and Organisations

Groups and organisations have complex internal dynamics and cultures that influence how we interact and plan for our security. This becomes more challenging when we consider, as a group, the genuine possibility of unpleasant things happening during our work.

Security planning in a group can be stressful and emotional. Fears about appearing overly concerned or paranoid are common. Practically speaking, it can also be difficult to carry out analyses in a group and reach agreements. For a security plan to be successful, everyone needs to be on board—but in groups, this can be a tall order.

This guide explores key issues around building and improving security strategies and plans within groups and organisations.

---

## Creating and Maintaining Security Plans

### Achieving Buy-In

Introduce your plan logically and walk colleagues through each step. Security is an emotional subject, and we all have different perceptions of it. This means discussions require time and a safe space.

- Present facts calmly without creating unnecessary fear
- Create space for questions and concerns
- Allow time for people to process information

### Participatory Design

Some people may not welcome a security plan decided without their consultation. High-risk activities and emergencies can be distressing, so it's important that everyone is comfortable with their assigned role.

- Gather input from as many people as possible during development
- Provide space for individuals to express concerns in advance
- Ensure everyone understands and accepts their responsibilities

### Role-Playing

In some cases, designing role-plays can help members practice responding to certain situations or emergencies.

> **Caution**: Exercise care and ensure everyone consents to participate. Role-playing can trigger emotional responses from people who have experienced trauma.

### Regular Review and Re-Planning

All security plans should be considered **live documents** requiring regular re-evaluation, especially when new members join the group.

- Schedule fixed dates to review security practices and plans
- Integrate security as central to your strategic planning—not an afterthought
- Update plans as your context and threats evolve

## Emergency Planning

### Defining an Emergency

Decide what constitutes an "emergency"—the point at which you should implement planned actions and contingency measures.

This is important because situations are not always clear-cut. Consider: if you lose contact with a colleague on a field mission, how long would you wait before declaring an emergency?

### Roles and Responsibilities

Assign each person a clear role that they are aware of and have agreed to in advance. This reduces disorganisation and panic during an incident.

For each threat scenario, consider:
- The different roles people may need to assume
- The practicalities involved in responding
- Who makes decisions and when

### Activating Support Networks

A key emergency strategy is activating your **support network**—a broad network of allies that may include:

- Friends and family
- Community members
- Local allies
- Sympathetic elements within government
- National or international allies (NGOs, journalists, embassies)

**Preparation steps:**
- Contact allies in advance to verify their willingness to help
- Clarify what you expect of them during an emergency
- Establish how they can best support you

### Communication Channels

Establish clear protocols for emergency communication:

- **What** to communicate (and what not to)
- **Which channels** to use (considering security and reliability)
- **To whom** different types of information should go

## Early Alert and Response System

An Early Alert and Response System is a centralised document (electronic or physical) to be opened during an emergency. It serves as both a coordination tool and documentation for future analysis.

### Components

| Element | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| **Timeline of incidents** | Record security indicators and incidents as they occur |
| **Response actions** | Clear description of planned actions and their sequence |
| **De-escalation indicators** | Signs that the risk has decreased |
| **After-care actions** | Steps to protect those involved and support physical and emotional recovery |

### Benefits

- Provides documentation for subsequent analysis
- Helps improve prevention tactics
- Informs better responses to future threats

## Improving Organisational Security Management

Organisations must consider security management as a process of consistent re-evaluation. This applies to managers, staff, and volunteers alike.

Regularly evaluate:
- Whether your security strategy remains consistent with your operating context
- Whether plans are accepted and implemented by members
- What gaps exist between policy and practice

Security management is not a one-time task but an ongoing organisational commitment.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Create safe spaces** for discussing security—it's an emotional topic
2. **Involve everyone** in security planning through participatory design
3. **Define emergencies clearly** so responses are timely and appropriate
4. **Assign roles in advance** to reduce confusion during incidents
5. **Build and maintain support networks** before you need them
6. **Document everything** using an Early Alert and Response System
7. **Review regularly**—security plans are living documents

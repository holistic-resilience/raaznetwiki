# Identifying and Analysing Threats

Threat analysis is the most detailed tool for diagnosing your security situation. In this context, **threats refer to any potential event or occurrence that could cause harm to you or your work.**

This section will help you:
- Name threats explicitly
- Prioritize them by **likelihood** and **impact**
- Analyze potential causes and consequences
- Identify tactics to **prevent** threats or **respond** if they occur

Security plans combine prevention and response tactics to reduce both the probability of threats materializing and their impact if they do.

> **Note:** This process can be emotional, but identifying and countering threats should be empowering. Threats can even indicate your work is effective enough that adversaries want to hinder it.

## Creating a Threat Brainstorm

Start by examining what you already know about existing threats, then expand to potential ones. Consider threats from multiple sources—not all are politically motivated or work-related.

**Common threat categories include:**
- Politically motivated opposition
- Petty crime
- Gender-based violence
- Environmental hazards
- Digital surveillance

> **Remember:** Sometimes non-political threats represent the greatest risk to human rights defenders.

## Perceiving Threats Accurately

Our perception of threats is never perfect, but it can improve. Group brainstorming often reveals threats individuals hadn't considered. Critical reflection helps develop more accurate threat perception.

**Questions to improve threat perception:**
- What threats have others in similar situations faced?
- What blind spots might exist in my current assessment?
- How can I verify whether perceived threats are real?

## Analysing Risk and Prioritising Threats

Evaluate each threat using two key factors:

1. **Likelihood** - How probable is it that this threat will occur?
2. **Impact** - How severe would the consequences be if it happens?

### The Threat Matrix

Use a simple matrix to categorize threats:

| | Low Impact | High Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **High Likelihood** | Monitor | Priority Action |
| **Low Likelihood** | Accept/Ignore | Contingency Plan |

This framework helps prioritize which threats require immediate attention versus ongoing monitoring.

## Understanding Declared Threats

Declared threats are explicit messages from opponents expressing intent to cause harm. These are designed to compel you to stop your work, punish you, or discourage others.

**Characteristics of declared threats:**
- **Intentional** - Made with clear purpose
- **Strategic** - Part of a plan to hinder your work
- **Personal** - Specific to you and your activities
- **Fear-based** - Meant to intimidate you into stopping

Declared threats may appear through:
- Direct messages
- Public statements
- Judicial harassment
- Proposed legislation

### Analyzing Declared Threats

Not all declared threats indicate planned action—some aim only to create unfounded fear. When analyzing declared threats:

1. Review your actor mapping
2. Consider the adversary's resources
3. Assess their actual interests and capabilities
4. Determine whether a genuine threat exists

## Creating a Threat Inventory

Document your threats systematically, including:
- Threat description
- Likelihood rating
- Impact rating
- Potential causes
- Possible consequences
- Prevention strategies
- Response plans

**Important:** Revisit this inventory regularly. Set a schedule for periodic review as your situation evolves.

## Next Steps

After completing threat analysis, examine your existing practices, resources, and **capacities** that may protect you, as well as gaps and **vulnerabilities** that increase susceptibility to identified threats.

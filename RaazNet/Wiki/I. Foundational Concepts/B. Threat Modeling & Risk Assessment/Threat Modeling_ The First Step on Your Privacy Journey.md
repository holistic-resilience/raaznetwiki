# Threat Modeling: The First Step on Your Privacy Journey

If you wanted to use the **most** secure tools available, you'd have to sacrifice *a lot* of usability. And even then, nothing is ever fully secure. There's **high** security, but never **full** security. That's why threat models are important.

## What Is a Threat Model?

A threat model is a list of the most probable threats to your security and privacy endeavors. Since it's impossible to protect yourself against **every** attack(er), you should focus on the **most probable** threats. In computer security, a threat is an event that could undermine your efforts to stay private and secure.

Focusing on the threats that matter to you narrows down your thinking about the protection you need, so you can choose the tools that are right for the job.

## Creating Your Threat Model

Building an effective threat model requires answering five fundamental questions:

1. What do I want to protect?
2. Who do I want to protect it from?
3. How likely is it that I will need to protect it?
4. How bad are the consequences if I fail?
5. How much trouble am I willing to go through to prevent potential consequences?

### 1. What Do I Want to Protect?

An "asset" is something you value and want to protect. In the context of digital security, an asset is usually some kind of information. For example:

- Emails and contact lists
- Instant messages
- Location data
- Files and documents
- Your devices themselves

### 2. Who Do I Want to Protect It From?

Identify who might want to target you or your information. A person or entity that poses a threat to your assets is an "adversary." Examples of potential adversaries include:

- Your employer
- A former partner
- Business competitors
- Your government
- Hackers on public networks

> **Action Step:** Make a list of your adversaries or those who might want to get hold of your assets. Your list may include individuals, government agencies, or corporations. Depending on who your adversaries are, this list might be something you want to destroy after you've finished developing your threat model.

### 3. How Likely Is It That I Will Need to Protect It?

**Risk** is the likelihood that a particular threat against a particular asset will actually occur. It goes hand-in-hand with **capability**. While your mobile phone provider has the capability to access all of your data, the risk of them posting your private data online to harm your reputation is low.

It is important to distinguish between what *might* happen and the *probability* it may happen. For instance, there is a threat that your building might collapse, but the risk of this happening is far greater in San Francisco (where earthquakes are common) than in Stockholm (where they are not).

Assessing risks is both a personal and subjective process:

- Some people find certain threats unacceptable regardless of likelihood
- Others disregard high risks because they don't view the threat as a problem

> **Action Step:** Write down which threats you are going to take seriously, and which may be too rare or too harmless (or too difficult to combat) to worry about.

### 4. How Bad Are the Consequences If I Fail?

There are many ways an adversary could gain access to your data:

- Reading your private communications as they pass through the network
- Deleting or corrupting your data
- Publishing secret content without your knowledge

The motives of adversaries differ widely, as do their tactics. A government trying to prevent the spread of a video showing police violence may be content to simply delete or reduce the availability of that video. In contrast, a political opponent may wish to gain access to secret content and publish it.

Security planning involves understanding how bad the consequences could be if an adversary successfully gains access to one of your assets. Consider the capability of your adversary:

- Your mobile phone provider has access to all of your phone records
- A hacker on an open Wi-Fi network can access your unencrypted communications
- Your government might have stronger capabilities

> **Action Step:** Write down what your adversary might want to do with your private data.

### 5. How Much Trouble Am I Willing to Go Through?

There is no perfect option for security. Not everyone has the same priorities, concerns, or access to resources. Your risk assessment will allow you to plan the right strategy for you, balancing convenience, cost, and privacy.

For example, an attorney representing a client in a national security case may be willing to go to greater lengths to protect communications about that case, such as using encrypted email, than a mother who regularly emails her daughter funny cat videos.

> **Action Step:** Write down what options you have available to help mitigate your unique threats. Note any financial constraints, technical constraints, or social constraints.

## Practical Example: Protecting Your Home

These questions can apply to a wide variety of situations, online and offline. As a demonstration, let's build a plan to keep your house and possessions safe.

| Question | Application |
|----------|-------------|
| **What do you want to protect?** | Jewelry, electronics, important documents, photos |
| **Who do you want to protect it from?** | Burglars, roommates, guests |
| **How likely do you need to protect it?** | Consider neighborhood history, trustworthiness of others, adversary capabilities |
| **How bad are the consequences?** | Irreplaceable items? Insurance coverage? Replacement costs? |
| **How much trouble are you willing to go through?** | Buy a safe? High-quality lock? Bank security box? |

Only once you have asked yourself these questions will you be in a position to assess what measures to take:

- If your possessions are valuable but the probability of a break-in is low, you may not want to invest too much money in a lock
- If the probability of a break-in is high, you'll want to get the best lock on the market and consider adding a security system

Making a security plan will help you understand the threats that are unique to you and evaluate your assets, your adversaries, and your adversaries' capabilities, along with the likelihood of risks you face.

# Body-Worn Cameras

## Overview

Body-worn cameras are small recording devices worn by police officers that capture video and audio of law enforcement encounters with the public. While often promoted as tools for police accountability, they also pose significant threats to privacy, protest rights, and racial justice without proper safeguards in place.

## How Body-Worn Cameras Work

Body-worn cameras are compact devices that can be clipped onto a police officer's uniform or worn as a headset. When activated, they record video and audio of law enforcement encounters with the public. The recorded footage is typically saved with:

- Time and date stamps
- GPS coordinates
- Officer identification information

### Recording Activation Methods

Body cameras can be activated through various methods:

- **Manual activation** by the officer
- **Real-time video streaming** capabilities
- **Bluetooth trigger options** for automatic recording
- **Automatic triggers** such as:
  - Turning on a cruiser's sirens
  - Driving over a certain speed
  - Nearby dashboard or body cameras being switched on
  - Drawing a weapon from a wireless holster sensor

Footage is uploaded to external databases maintained by police agencies or third-party vendors. At least one company has been developing body-worn cameras with live facial recognition capabilities.

## Data Collection

When police activate body-worn cameras, they collect:

- **Video footage** of people in the vicinity of the officer
- **Audio recordings** of conversations and ambient sound
- **Date and time stamps**
- **GPS coordinates**
- **Facial images** that can be analyzed by face recognition technology

## Law Enforcement Usage

### Deployment Scale

- Many states mandate statewide use of body-worn cameras by law enforcement officers
- EFF's Atlas of Surveillance has catalogued **more than 4,517 police departments** using body-worn cameras nationwide

### Common Recording Scenarios

Police can collect video footage during:

- Stop and frisk encounters
- Property searches
- Vehicle stops or chases
- Witness interviews
- Summons issuance
- Arrests

### Policy Variations

According to a 2017 scorecard by The Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights:

- **52 of 68 police departments** in major cities have body-worn camera programs with policies in place
- **49 departments** make policies available on their websites
- **4 departments** (Cincinnati, Las Vegas, Parker CO, and Washington D.C.) provide recorded individuals access to all footage
- **12 departments** prohibit officers from reviewing footage before filing initial written incident reports for critical incidents
- **7 departments** (Baltimore, Baltimore County, Boston, Cincinnati, Montgomery County MD, Parker, and Seattle) limit use of biometric technologies with camera footage

Some vendors, such as Axon, require police departments to sign contracts permitting footage to be stored in proprietary systems. Defense attorneys may need to agree to restrictive terms of service to access evidence in judicially mandated discovery.

## Privacy and Civil Liberties Threats

### Fundamental Concerns

Body-worn cameras pose threats to:

- **Privacy** - massive expansion of government video and audio recording in public and private places
- **Protest rights** - potential chilling effect on First Amendment activities
- **Racial justice** - disproportionate impact on people of color, immigrants, and vulnerable groups

### Specific Risks

**Data Retention and Security**
- Footage may be kept indefinitely
- Risk of data breaches and employee misuse
- Potential for long-term surveillance through searchable databases

**Home Recording**
- Police entering homes may capture innocent private domestic activity
- Recording of children, people in various states of undress
- Recording of domestic violence or sexual assault victims without informed consent

**Algorithmic Analysis**
- Thousands of hours of footage can be converted into searchable metadata
- Face surveillance integration potential
- Combination with other surveillance technologies like stationary pole cameras

**Asymmetric Access**
- Police can often immediately access video while recorded civilians cannot
- In some instances, footage is made public without blurring bystander faces

### Officer Discretion Problems

**Recording Failures**
- When given discretion, officers often neglect to turn on cameras
- Compliance with recording requirements can be low
- Failures to record during use-of-force incidents don't always result in discipline

**Pre-Report Viewing**
- Many departments allow officers to review footage before making initial statements
- This enables officers to tailor stories to video evidence
- Allows potential "fishing expeditions" searching for additional crimes

**Selective Release**
- Police can release portions of video selectively as a public relations strategy
- Example: Ferguson Police released surveillance video of Michael Brown after his shooting, though the shooting officer was unaware of the earlier incident
- Chicago Police delayed release of Laquan McDonald shooting footage for 13 months

## EFF's Recommended Safeguards

EFF opposes body-worn cameras without strict rules ensuring privacy protection and police accountability:

### Activation Requirements
- Officers must turn cameras on at the start of **all investigative encounters** with civilians
- Cameras must remain on until the encounter ends

### Prohibited Uses
- Officers must **not use cameras to gather information** about people exercising First Amendment rights
- **No face surveillance** - the technology is too dangerous for police use

### Data Retention
- Footage must be stored for **a few months** so injured civilians have time to request it
- Footage must then be **promptly destroyed** to prevent long-term surveillance

### Officer Review Restrictions
- For footage depicting **use of force** or episodes subject to **civilian complaint**:
  - Officers must make an **initial statement before reviewing** the footage

### Public Access Rights
- If footage depicts a person, **they must have access**
- If footage depicts force, **everyone must have access**
- For other police misconduct claims, courts must weigh privacy costs against accountability benefits

### Enforcement Mechanisms
- Footage collected **in violation of rules must be excluded from court**
- Missing footage should create an **evidentiary presumption** favoring the civilian

### Community Control
- Police must **not deploy body-worn cameras** unless the city council votes to approve them
- Approval must come **after community input**
- Communities should have the option to say "no"

## EFF's Advocacy History

- **2015**: Opposed LAPD's body-worn camera policies due to inadequate safeguards
- **2016**: Opposed California A.B. 1940, which would have allowed officers to view footage before writing statements
- **2017**: Sent recommendations to Santa Clara County Board on Sheriff's body-worn camera policy
- **2019**: Supported California A.B. 1215, placing a three-year moratorium on face surveillance with body camera footage
- **2019**: Successfully enforced the moratorium in San Diego
- **2020**: Recommended safeguards to the federal Justice in Policing Act's body-worn camera provisions

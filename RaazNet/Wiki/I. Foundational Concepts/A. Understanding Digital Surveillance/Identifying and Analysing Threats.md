# Identifying and Analysing Threats

The final and most detailed tool for making a 'diagnosis' of our security situation is threat analysis. Threats refer to **any potential event or occurrence which would cause harm to ourselves or our work.**

This section covers how to name threats explicitly, prioritise them according to their **likelihood** and potential **impact**, and analyse their potential causes and consequences. This process helps identify the right tactics to **prevent** threats or **respond** to them should they occur.

Security plans combine **prevention** and **response** tactics to reduce both the likelihood of threats becoming reality and their impact if they do.

> **Note:** This can be an emotional process, but taking steps to identify and counter threats should be empowering. Threats can also indicate that our work is effective—effective enough that adversaries want to hinder it.

## Threat Brainstorming

Start with what you 'know' to be existing threats and scrutinise them. Create a brainstorm of existing and potential threats using structured exercises.

> [Exercise: Threat Brainstorm](https://holistic-security.tacticaltech.org/exercises/explore/threat-brainstorm.html)

**Important:** Not all threats to well-being and security are politically motivated or work-related. Threats also arise from:
- Petty crime
- Gender-based violence
- Environmental hazards

Sometimes these non-political threats represent the greatest risk to human rights defenders.

## Perceiving Threats

Our perception of threats is never perfect, but we can improve it. Group brainstorming often reveals threats that individuals haven't previously considered.

## Analysing Risk and Prioritising Threats

Risk analysis considers two key factors:

1. **Likelihood** — the probability that the threat will occur
2. **Impact** — the severity of consequences if it does occur

![Threat Matrix Diagram](https://holistic-security.tacticaltech.org/ckeditor_assets/pictures/28/content_threatmatrix.png)

By evaluating threats against these two dimensions, you can prioritize which require immediate attention and move toward deeper analysis of:
- Conditions required for threats to materialize
- Potential consequences
- Appropriate response planning

## Declared Threats

Human rights defenders often receive **declared threats**—messages from individuals, groups, or organizations that openly express intent to cause harm. These threats aim to:
- Compel you to stop work (temporarily or permanently)
- Punish or hurt you
- Discourage others from similar work

### Characteristics of Declared Threats

Declared threats are typically:

| Characteristic | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Intentional** | Made with clear intent |
| **Strategic** | Part of a plan to hinder work |
| **Personal** | Specific to you and your work |
| **Fear-based** | Designed to scare you into stopping |

Declared threats may also appear in:
- Opponents' public statements
- Judicial harassment
- Proposed legislation

They are effective and "economical" because they may achieve their goal without carrying out the actual threat.

### Analysing Declared Threats

When analysing declared threats:

1. **Distinguish genuine threats from intimidation tactics** — Some declared threats have concrete implications; others create unfounded fears with no planned action
2. **Reference your actor map** — Consider the resources and interests of the adversary to assess whether a genuine threat is posed
3. **Document systematically** — Use structured analysis frameworks (see Front Line Defenders Handbook on Security, Annex B)

## Threat Inventory

Create and maintain a threat inventory that you revisit regularly. Determine how often you should revisit these lists and schedule dedicated time for review.

## Next Steps

After completing threat analysis, examine how your existing practices, resources, and **capacities** may protect you, and how gaps in practices and resources—along with other **vulnerabilities**—may make you susceptible to threats.

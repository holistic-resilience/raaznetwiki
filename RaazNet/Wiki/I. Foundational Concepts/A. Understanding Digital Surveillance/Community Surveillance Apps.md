# Community Surveillance Apps

Technology rarely invents new societal problems. Instead, it digitizes them, supersizes them, and allows them to balloon and duplicate at the speed of light. That's exactly the problem we've seen with location-based, crowd-sourced "public safety" apps.

A slew of popular apps have flooded the market which purport to show a person the potential hazards around them and in their neighborhood. The problem is that rather than making people feel safer and better informed, they can have the opposite effect of [making people feel terrified](https://theappeal.org/spotlight-neighborhood-crime-apps-stoke-fears-reinforce-racist-stereotypes-and-dont-prevent-crime/) and as if their community is less safe than it actually is by amplifying and notifying users of unfounded reports on a near constant basis.

There is also a very real fear that these applications, if not seriously moderated or shifted to make their objective more about community building and less about public paranoia, can inadvertently unleash [racially biased](https://www.fastcompany.com/90657035/false-arrest-racial-profiling-why-the-citizen-app-is-a-threat-to-vulnerable-communities) or even [vigilante violence](https://www.vice.com/en/article/93kb87/watch-new-documentary-tells-inside-story-of-vigilante-app-citizen) or harassment against already marginalized people.

## How It Works

These apps come in a wide spectrum:

- **Community-focused apps**: Let users connect with those around them by posting pictures, items for sale, or local tips
- **Suspicion-focused apps**: Focus exclusively on things and people that users see as "suspicious" or potentially hazardous
- **911-based apps**: Generate and map alerts based on 911 calls

Alerts run the gamut from active crimes, police activity, or just generally anything someone might call 911 about—including unconfirmed reports of what the caller interprets as suspicious behavior.

### The Problem with Raw Data

Having access to 911 call data through police scanners or other tools of citizen journalism comes with a grain of salt—people who use these tools often know they are listening to raw information that could turn out to be false or taken out of context. However, when these calls are presented as big red dots on a map near your home, they're much more likely to be taken as a real imminent threat rather than an initial report needing follow-up.

As the [New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2019/03/17/style/citizen-neighborhood-crime-app.html) writes, Citizen is "converting raw scanner traffic—which is by nature unvetted and mostly operational—into filtered, curated digital content, legible to regular people, rendered on a map in a far more digestible form." In other words, they're turning static into content with the same formula the long-running show *Cops* used to [normalize](https://www.theguardian.com/tv-and-radio/2020/jun/11/cops-american-police-tv-show) both paranoia and police violence.

### Integration with Other Surveillance Technologies

Apps like Citizen have also been known to be [triggered by ShotSpotter](https://citizen.com/-NUosk1nzF1q8sFvhErg), acoustic gunshot detection technology. Studies have shown ShotSpotter is [wildly inaccurate and can lead to heavily armed police confronting unarmed pedestrians](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2021/07/its-time-police-stop-using-shotspotter) based on false positive alerts.

## Threats Posed

These apps are often designed with a goal of crowd-sourced surveillance—like a digital neighborhood watch turning the aggregate eyes and phones of the neighborhood into an early warning system. Instead, they often exacerbate the same dangers, biases, and problems that exist within policing.

### Key Concerns

**Summoning Authorities**: The likely outcome of posting a suspicious sight isn't just to warn neighbors—it's to summon authorities to address the issue.

**Experimental Features**: Apps like Citizen have rolled out concerning features:
- A [private security force](https://www.vice.com/en/article/v7evbx/citizen-app-private-security-leaked-emails) available at the touch of a button
- A 24/7 concierge service that will [call the police for you](https://techcrunch.com/2021/08/03/controversial-crime-app-citizen-launches-20-month-protect-service/) for $20/month

These paid services profit from fear while normalizing constant surveillance.

### The Vigilante Problem

Citizen began its life as "Vigilante," and much of its DNA continues to match its former name. The app remains unsure if it wants to be a community forum or a bounty-hunting platform.

**Case Study**: When a brush fire broke out in Los Angeles in May 2021, almost a million people saw a Citizen notification offering a [$30,000 reward](https://www.sfgate.com/california-wildfires/article/citizen-app-california-palisades-wildfire-reward-16185996.php) for information leading to the arrest of a man they thought was responsible. The suspect was actually an unhoused man completely unrelated to the fire.

This kind of incentivized targeting of innocent people demonstrates the real danger these apps pose and reveals a narrow view of who the "public" is and what "safety" entails.

## The Broader Problem

Paranoia about crime and racial gatekeeping in neighborhoods is not new. Apps like Citizen digitize that old problem, making knee-jerk sightings of so-called suspicious behavior capable of being broadcast to thousands of people.

Consider the difference in usage patterns:
- No one plans their block party on Citizen
- Neighbors aren't coordinating trick-or-treating on a platform filled with notifications like "unconfirmed report of a man armed with pipe"
- When you download an app that makes you feel [a comfortable neighborhood is under siege](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2019-03-11/do-crime-tracking-apps-offer-transparency-or-paranoia), you're likely to report your own suspicions

These apps are part of what law professor Elizabeth Joh calls "networked surveillance ecosystems." The lack of laws governing private surveillance networks—in conjunction with social networking and vigilante apps—is only exacerbating age-old problems.

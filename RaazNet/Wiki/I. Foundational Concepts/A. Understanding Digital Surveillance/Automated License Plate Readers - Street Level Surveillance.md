# Automated License Plate Readers (ALPRs)

Automated license plate readers (ALPRs) are high-speed, computer-controlled camera systems typically mounted on street poles, streetlights, highway overpasses, mobile trailers, or police squad cars. ALPRs automatically capture all license plate numbers that come into view, along with the location, date, and time. The data, which includes photographs of the vehicle and sometimes its driver and passengers, is then uploaded to a central server.

The information collected can be used by police to determine where a plate has been in the past, identify whether a vehicle was at the scene of a crime, discover travel patterns, and find vehicles that may be associated with each other. Law enforcement agencies can choose to share their information with thousands of other agencies.

**Key Privacy Concern:** Taken in aggregate, ALPR data can paint an intimate portrait of a driver's life and chill First Amendment protected activity. ALPR technology can target drivers who visit sensitive places such as health centers, immigration clinics, gun shops, union halls, protests, or centers of religious worship.

Drivers have no control over whether their vehicle displays a license plate because the government requires all car, truck, and motorcycle drivers to display license plates in public view. It's particularly disturbing that automatic license plate readers track and record the movements of millions of ordinary people, even though the overwhelming majority are not connected to any crime.

## How ALPR Systems Work

Automated license plate readers can be broadly divided into three categories.

### Stationary or Fixed ALPR Cameras

Fixed ALPRs are installed in permanent locations such as traffic lights, telephone poles, facility entrances, or freeway exit ramps. These cameras generally capture only vehicles in motion that pass within view.

**Capabilities:**
- Multiple cameras along a single thoroughfare can reveal direction and speed of travel
- With enough cameras, police can track a vehicle in real-time
- Stored data over time can reveal every time a particular plate has passed a given location
- Police can infer that drivers likely live or work close to frequently captured locations

Smaller municipalities often install ALPR cameras at town entrances and exits, creating a virtual gated community where no one can visit without being documented. Police sometimes disguise ALPRs as mundane objects, such as traffic cones or cacti.

ALPR cameras are often used in conjunction with automated red-light and speed enforcement systems, as well as toll assessment on roads and bridges.

### Mobile ALPR Cameras

Mobile ALPRs are typically attached to police patrol cars, allowing officers to capture data from license plates as they drive throughout their shifts. These cameras are usually turned on at the beginning of a shift and remain active until the end.

**Key Features:**
- Capture images of passing vehicles
- Highly effective at capturing license plates of parked cars
- Can scan hundreds of plates in a public parking lot within minutes
- Enable "gridding"—systematically driving up and down every block of a neighborhood to capture intelligence on residents

Private vendors like Vigilant Solutions also capture plate data with mobile ALPRs and sell that data to police agencies and others.

### ALPR Trailers

ALPRs are available as portable trailers that police can tow to particular areas and leave for extended periods. These collect data similar to fixed ALPRs without permanent installation.

**Notable Uses:**
- The Drug Enforcement Administration has purchased these systems disguised as speed enforcement trailers to track vehicles along the U.S.-Mexico border
- Police have parked trailers or ALPR-equipped vehicles near gun shows and political rallies

## ALPR Databases and Hotlists

### Database Storage

Most ALPR data is stored in databases for extended periods—often as long as five years. Databases may be maintained by police departments or private companies such as Vigilant Solutions or Flock Safety.

**Data Access:**
- Law enforcement agencies without their own ALPR systems can access data through regional sharing systems
- Private company networks enable cross-agency data sharing
- Several companies operate independent, non-law enforcement ALPR databases
- Commercial data is collected through partnerships with drivers who put cameras on private vehicles
- Law enforcement can purchase access to commercial data on a subscription basis

### Hotlists

Law enforcement agencies pre-load lists of license plates the ALPR system actively seeks—such as stolen vehicles and vehicles associated with outstanding warrants. Officers can also create their own hotlists.

**How Hotlists Work:**
- If an ALPR camera scans a plate on the list, the system sends an alert to the officer (mobile reader) or agency (fixed reader)
- Some hotlists include low-level misdemeanors and traffic offenses
- Some agencies use hotlists to generate revenue by stopping citation scofflaws

## What Data ALPRs Collect

ALPRs collect:
- License plate numbers
- Location data
- Exact date and time of capture
- Vehicle make and model (some systems)
- Photographs of vehicles, drivers, passengers, and surroundings
- "Vehicle fingerprints" including color, physical damage, and bumper stickers

**Scale of Collection:**
- Systems can collect thousands of plates per minute
- One vendor's dataset includes more than 6.5 billion scans
- Data grows at a rate of 120 million data points each month

**Analytical Capabilities:**
- Combined data reveals direction and speed of travel through triangulation
- Historical data reveals vehicle travel patterns over time
- Algorithms can predict where a driver may be in the future
- Data reveals all visitors to a particular location
- While data doesn't include driver names, law enforcement can connect names to plate numbers through other databases

## How Law Enforcement Uses ALPR Technology

ALPR data is gathered indiscriminately, collecting information on millions of ordinary people. By plotting vehicle times and locations, police can paint specific portraits of drivers' lives, determining past patterns of behavior and potentially predicting future ones—despite the vast majority of people whose data is collected never being accused of a crime.

**A 2021 EFF report found that according to data from 63 California law enforcement agencies, only 0.05% of ALPR data collected was relevant to a public safety interest at the time of capture.**

### Real-Time Investigations

By adding a license plate to a "hot list," officers can automatically identify or track particular vehicles in real time. Plates are added to hot lists because:
- The vehicle is stolen
- There's an outstanding warrant associated with the vehicle
- The vehicle was seen at a crime scene
- The owner is a suspect in a crime
- The vehicle is believed to be associated with a gang
- Low-level offenses are involved

### Historical Investigations

Since ALPRs typically collect information on everyone, officers can search and analyze historical data using:
- A plate number
- A partial plate
- A physical address

For example, an officer may enter a convenience store's location to identify vehicles seen nearby during a robbery, then look up those plates to find other locations where they've been captured.

**Important Note:** Training materials and policies in some jurisdictions instruct officers that a hot-list alert alone may not warrant a stop. Officers should visually confirm plate numbers. Failure to manually confirm, combined with machine error, has caused wrongful stops.

### Data Retention

The length of time ALPR data is retained varies from agency to agency—from mere days to several years. Some entities, including private companies, may retain data indefinitely.

## Privacy Threats

ALPR is a powerful surveillance technology that can invade individual privacy and violate the rights of entire communities.

### Documented Abuses

**Targeting Communities:**
- New York police drove down a street and electronically recorded license plate numbers of everyone parked near a mosque
- Birmingham police targeted a Muslim community while misleading the public about the project
- ALPR data EFF obtained from Oakland Police Department showed disproportionate deployment of ALPR-mounted vehicles in low-income communities and communities of color

**Individual Officer Abuse:**
- In 1998, a Washington, D.C. police officer pleaded guilty to extortion after looking up plates of vehicles near a gay bar and blackmailing vehicle owners
- In Kechi, Kansas, an officer was arrested on suspicion of accessing a Flock Safety ALPR database to stalk his estranged wife

### Technology Errors

ALPRs sometimes misread plates, leading to dire consequences:

- **Denise Green Case (2009):** San Francisco police pulled over this African-American city worker, handcuffed her at gunpoint, forced her to her knees, and searched her and her vehicle—all because her car was misidentified as stolen due to a license plate reader error. This led the U.S. Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals to rule that technology alone can't be the basis of such a stop, though this judgment doesn't apply everywhere.

- **Aurora, Colorado:** A group of Black youths were traumatized by police after an ALPR system incorrectly identified their vehicle as stolen.

### Data Security Risks

Aggregate data stored for lengthy periods becomes more invasive and susceptible to misuse and data breach:

- Even Customs & Border Protection, arguably the largest and best-funded law enforcement agency in the U.S., saw its ALPR vendor, Perceptics, hacked and data published online

### Vulnerable Populations

Automated license plate readers can also target:
- Immigrant communities
- People seeking or providing reproductive health services

### Recommended Protections

- Sensible retention limits
- Specific policies about who inside an agency can access data
- Audit and control processes
- Ideally, police should retain no information when a passing vehicle doesn't match a hot list

## EFF's Work on ALPR

EFF has been investigating and combating ALPR privacy threats through public records requests, litigation, and legislative advocacy since 2012.

### ALPR Litigation

**ACLU of Southern California and EFF v. LAPD and LASD:**
EFF and the ACLU sued after agencies refused to hand over ALPR data, claiming records were exempt from the California Public Records Act as "investigative records." This argument amounts to claiming all Los Angelenos are under investigation—a point both a lawyer for the LAPD and a California Supreme Court Justice agreed sounded "Orwellian." In 2017, the California Supreme Court ruled in EFF and ACLU's favor.

**Lagleva v. Marin County Sheriff (2021):**
EFF and the ACLU sued on behalf of local activists after the sheriff shared ALPR data with out-of-state agencies, including Immigration & Customs Enforcement, violating laws regulating ALPR use. As part of a settlement, the sheriff agreed to cease sharing data outside of California.

**Additional Cases:**
EFF has filed briefs in lawsuits over excessive ALPR data storage in Virginia, Massachusetts, and Nevada.

### ALPR Accountability and Transparency

**California S.B. 34 (2015):**
This bill requires ALPR users to:
- Protect data
- Maintain access logs
- Hold public meetings before starting an ALPR program
- Implement a usage and privacy policy
- Prohibits public agencies from selling, sharing, or transferring ALPR data except to other public agencies

**EFF Initiatives:**
- Coordinated volunteers to collect ALPR policies across California
- Exposed agencies failing to comply with the law
- Filed public records requests with dozens of agencies through Data Driven and Data Driven 2 projects
- Successfully advocated for the California Legislature to order an audit of law enforcement agencies' compliance with S.B. 34 (2019)
- The resulting California State Auditor report substantiated many of EFF's concerns about agencies not following the law, failing to enact adequate policies, and sharing data too broadly

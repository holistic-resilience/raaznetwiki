# Social Media Monitoring

## How It Works

Social media contains some of the most intimate details of our lives, including health information, likes and dislikes, political views, religious beliefs, sexual orientation, and the people with whom we associate. Monitored content includes:

- Usernames, bios, and contact information
- Status updates, comments, photos, videos, and streams
- Event postings and group memberships
- Friend/follower lists and friend requests
- Private messages
- Account creation dates and location data

### Federal Monitoring Programs

Law enforcement agencies—including the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) and U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE)—monitor social media posts of:

- Asylum seekers
- Tourists seeking visa waivers
- People applying for immigration benefits
- American residents (even without suspicion of illegal activity)
- Anyone in these people's networks

DHS has offered no transparency about its multi-million dollar program of spying on immigrants' and foreign visitors' social media posts, which it uses as evidence in deportations and visa denials.

### Local Law Enforcement Practices

Federal, state, and local law enforcement agencies all monitor social media accounts. While this has typically targeted political activists, monitoring is sometimes much broader.

**Example:** According to [an internal July 2020 memo](https://www.brennancenter.org/sites/default/files/2021-09/I.%20Beck%20FI%20Memo.pdf) obtained by the Brennan Center, LAPD officers were ordered to [note the social media information](https://www.brennancenter.org/sites/default/files/2021-09/A%20Series_LAPD%20Social%20Media%20Monitoring%20FOIA_2021.pdf) of every civilian they questioned—including those not arrested or accused of any crime.

Police may combine social media monitoring with other technologies, such as face recognition, to identify individuals. Since social media posts are highly contextual, police may misinterpret innocuous language, song lyrics, or inside jokes, leading to criminal consequences for individuals who may not have even been aware they were under surveillance.

## How Law Enforcement Monitors Social Media

### Manual Surveillance Methods

Police may use fake accounts to:

- Search publicly available posts for names, groups, or individuals
- Track specific keywords and hashtags
- Scour social media pages and groups for names, affiliations, event times, and other information

### Legal Requests for Data

When police want additional information that's not publicly available or would take too long to scrape, they may use a **subpoena** or **warrant** to request social media data as part of a criminal investigation.

### Fake and Imposter Accounts

Law enforcement has created fraudulent accounts to gather intelligence:

- **2010 DEA case:** An agent [created a phony Facebook account](https://www.buzzfeednews.com/article/chrishamby/government-says-federal-agents-can-impersonate-woman-online) in a young woman's name without her knowledge, posting photos from her seized belongings—including pictures of her son and niece.

- **University of Farmington:** DHS created fake profiles and pages to trick immigrants into registering with [a fake college](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2020/dec/05/ice-fake-university-farmington-inside-story), leading to more than 170 arrests.

- **Memphis Police Department:** Created [fake profiles to monitor Black Lives Matter activists](https://theappeal.org/memphis-police-collected-black-lives-matter-activists-private-facebook-posts/).

### Automated Monitoring Tools

Police use social media monitoring tools that collect and analyze large amounts of data, often allowing searches by hashtag or keyword and generating profiles and predictions.

**Example:** The United States Postal Inspection Service (USPIS) [conducted blanket keyword searches](https://www.vice.com/en/article/n7nmaz/post-office-cops-used-social-media-surveillance-program-illegally) (such as "protest," "destroy," and "attack") across social media in its iCOP surveillance program. The Office of Inspector General later determined USPIS lacked legal authority for these searches since they were unconnected to mail or the post office.

Some vendors, like Geofeedia, lost access to social media APIs after the ACLU of Northern California revealed the company was pitching its product for surveilling people lawfully protesting police violence.

### Combined Data Systems

Some tools merge social media history with other data sources:

- **Palantir** provided software to the New Orleans Police Department combining social media history with additional data for "predictive policing"
- **Clearview AI** scraped billions of photos from social media for facial recognition used by the Minneapolis Police Department
- **Customs and Border Patrol** created dossiers [inappropriately surveilling](https://www.nbcsandiego.com/news/investigations/report-cbp-inappropriately-flagged-u-s-citizens-during-migrant-caravan/2726925/) journalists, attorneys, and advocates meeting migrants at the U.S.-Mexico border

## Threats Posed by Social Media Monitoring

### Chilling Effect on Protected Speech

Social media monitoring can chill protected speech and association. People engaging in protected speech or traveling to the U.S. may legitimately fear:

- Being screened for political opinions
- Increased interrogation or entry denial
- Exposing others to surveillance
- Inclusion in police databases
- Criminal charges

### Documented Targeting of Protesters

Social media monitoring has been used to target nonviolent protesters engaging in protected speech:

**ICE and LAPD:**
- ICE monitored anti-Trump protesters in New York City
- LAPD monitored social media accounts of protesters and journalists during George Floyd protests, searching for keywords including:
  - "black lives matter," "BLM," "demonstration," "protest," "protester"
  - Hashtags: #BLMLA, #SayHerName, #fuckdonaldtrump
  - Names: Sandra Bland, Tamir Rice, and others killed by LA police

**DHS and Fusion Centers:**
- DHS collected data on Black Lives Matter activities even for events expected to be peaceful
- LookingGlass Cyber Solutions gathered information on 600+ protests against forced family separation, sharing with DHS and state fusion centers

**Surveillance Beyond Protests:**
- A Texas fusion center surveilled social gatherings and Black cultural events, including an online Juneteenth celebration
- DHS circulated information on nationwide silent vigils
- DHS planned to monitor a community parade in Congress Heights, a funk parade on U Street, and a breast cancer awareness walk

### Oversight and Accuracy Problems

- **Minimal oversight and transparency** despite ability to monitor thousands of accounts with a button push
- **Misinterpretation risks:** Police may misinterpret innocuous language, song lyrics, or inside jokes
- **False allegations:** Police and prosecutors have used Facebook photos, "likes," and associations to make false gang activity allegations
- **Language barriers:** Interpretation may be difficult for non-English languages and unfamiliar cultural contexts
- **Identity errors:** Correctly linking individuals to accounts is challenging
- **Data proliferation:** Many agencies partner with data mining companies, adding unverified information to databases shared across organizations with different retention policies

## EFF's Work on Social Media Monitoring

### Legal Actions

**EFF v. DHS (VLVI):** We sued DHS under FOIA for records on its Visa Lifecycle Vetting Initiative, a social media program to spy on immigrants. We're seeking information on:
- Current program status and purposes
- Impact on visa approvals and denials
- Details about a $4.8 million transaction
- Contracts, training materials, operating procedures, privacy impact statements, and audits

**EFF v. USPS:** We [sued the U.S. Postal Service](https://www.eff.org/press/releases/eff-sues-us-postal-service-records-about-covert-social-media-spying-program) seeking records about its covert program to secretly monitor online posts before street protests.

**2009 Social Networking Suit:** Working with the Samuelson Law Technology and Public Policy Clinic at UC Berkeley, we filed suit against six government agencies for refusing to disclose policies for using social networking sites for investigations and surveillance.

### Policy Advocacy

[EFF opposes the U.S. government's monitoring](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2017/11/eff-urges-dhs-abandon-social-media-surveillance-and-automated-extreme-vetting) of anyone's social media accounts and internet activity. We've urged DHS to abandon such programs because social media surveillance:

- Invades privacy
- Violates the First Amendment by chilling speech
- Allows government targeting based on political views
- Is [ineffective and discriminatory](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2019/11/deep-dive-eff-dhs-stop-mass-collection-social-media-information)

### Addressing Fake Law Enforcement Accounts

EFF has called on Meta to:
- Publish the number of fake law enforcement accounts identified in transparency reports
- Include information on which agencies accounts belonged to
- Alert users and groups that interacted with fake accounts
- Amend documentation to explicitly bar such accounts
- Notify departments with policies allowing this behavior that they're violating platform rules

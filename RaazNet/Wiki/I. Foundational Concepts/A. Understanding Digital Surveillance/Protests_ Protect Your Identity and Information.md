# Protests: Protect Your Identity and Information

Many human rights defenders and activists depend heavily on computers and mobile phones for organizing and coordinating peaceful protests. This makes confiscation, theft, and surveillance of devices and communications a priority for adversaries attempting to limit free speech and assembly.

While transparency and open engagement can be useful security tools in many contexts, protecting the identity and sensitive information of those involved in a protest is often essential.

---

## Understanding Surveillance Threats

When planning or coordinating a protest over the internet or using mobile phones, consider ways to protect your identity and your network.

**Common surveillance tactics include:**
- Direct observation and infiltration of groups
- Automated search engines scanning social networks for specific words or images
- Monitoring of social media platforms to predict, prevent, or disrupt protests

> **Warning:** Relying on social media to organize a protest can be dangerous due to heavy surveillance.

---

## Protecting Your Online Identity

### Social Media Precautions

- **Avoid using your real name** on social networks when organizing or participating in actions
- **Don't associate yourself** with groups likely to be under surveillance on social media
- **Be cautious with connection requests** — avoid accepting friend requests from protesters you don't already know

### Hiding Your Digital Footprint

- Use a **VPN** to hide your IP address from site administrators
- Use the **[Tor Browser](https://torproject.org/)** to conceal browsing activities from your internet service provider

---

## Securing Your Device

To address threats of confiscation, loss, or theft, take these protective measures:

### Android Devices (Version 4.0+)

1. Navigate to **Settings → Security**
2. Select the option to **encrypt your phone**
3. Encryption ensures data is inaccessible without your passphrase when the device is powered off

**Important considerations:**
- Encrypt your device **at least one day before** the protest
- **Back up your data first** — the encryption process takes approximately one hour
- The **Micro-SD card may not be encrypted** — only built-in storage is protected

### iPhone and iPad

1. Go to **Settings → Privacy**
2. Enable a **passphrase** to lock the screen
3. Confirm that **"Data protection is enabled"** appears after setting the passphrase
4. *Optional:* Enable the setting to **erase data after 10 incorrect passphrase attempts** — this can also serve as a quick way to delete sensitive data in emergencies

# Surveillance Camera Networks

Surveillance camera networks have become ubiquitous in public spaces, creating significant implications for privacy and civil liberties. This guide covers the types of cameras you may encounter, how law enforcement accesses and shares footage, and emerging threats to privacy.

## Recognizing Camera Types

Understanding the different types of surveillance cameras helps you identify what you may encounter in daily life. Note that some cameras incorporate multiple features described below.

### Bullet Cameras

Bullet cameras are compact, tube-shaped devices with the camera, lens, and housing packaged within a cylindrical body. Key characteristics:

- Face a single, fixed direction
- Often include infrared LEDs for low-light operation
- Used both indoors and outdoors

### Dome Cameras

Dome cameras are typically mounted on ceilings for indoor surveillance. Their distinctive features include:

- Dome shape makes it difficult to determine which direction the camera faces
- Optional infrared capability for night vision
- Some models include tamper-resistant features
- Varifocal lens options allow operators to adjust focal distance

### PTZ (Pan-Tilt-Zoom) Cameras

Unlike fixed cameras that face one direction, PTZ cameras offer remote control capabilities:

- **Pan**: Move left or right
- **Tilt**: Move up and down
- **Zoom**: Adjust focal distance closer or farther

These features allow operators to follow individuals as they move, zoom in on specific areas, run automated patterns, or turn to preset positions.

### Mobile Surveillance Towers and Poles

Law enforcement agencies deploy mobile surveillance units for temporary monitoring:

- **Manned towers**: Can be raised 25 feet in the air
- **Unmanned trailers**: Extend poles with PTZ cameras covering all directions

These systems are commonly deployed at public events and high-traffic commercial areas to establish situational awareness.

### Thermal Cameras

Thermal cameras capture video based on body temperature rather than visible or infrared light:

- May not be visually distinguishable from conventional security cameras
- The brand name "FLIR" often indicates thermal imaging capability
- Can track movement based on heat signatures

### Automated License Plate Readers (ALPR)

ALPR systems are computer-controlled cameras that capture:

- License plate numbers
- Photographs of vehicles
- Sometimes images of drivers and passengers

ALPR cameras come in two main forms:
- **Fixed cameras**: Mounted on traffic lights, telephone poles, or other stationary locations
- **Mobile cameras**: Attached to police patrol cars or placed on mobile trailers

*For more information, see [EFF's ALPR resource page](https://www.eff.org/pages/automated-license-plate-readers-alpr).*

## Data Sharing and Access

Surveillance cameras increasingly broadcast footage over the internet, enabling remote monitoring. Law enforcement gains access to this footage through multiple channels.

### Police-Operated Systems

Some cities directly pay for and operate surveillance systems. For example, in 2019, EFF obtained a dataset revealing the extensive network of surveillance cameras maintained by the San Francisco District Attorney's Office.

### Private Camera Registration Programs

Many law enforcement agencies operate private security camera registration programs (sometimes called "SafeCam"):

1. Residents and business owners register their cameras and locations
2. When crimes are reported, police search the database
3. Officers contact camera owners directly to obtain footage

Examples include:
- Philadelphia Police Department's SafeCam program
- Fultondale Police Department (Alabama)
- Phoenix Police Department's "Virtual Block Watch"

### Business Improvement District Networks

Private organizations sometimes build extensive camera networks that police can access:

**Case Study: San Francisco Union Square**
- Started in 2012 with 6 cameras
- Expanded to over 450 surveillance cameras
- Police regularly seek footage for investigations
- During the 2020 George Floyd protests, police used the entire network for real-time monitoring over 8 days
- Approximately 90% funded through grants; participating businesses pay the remainder

## Real-Time Crime Centers (RTCC)

Real-time crime centers (also called "domain awareness centers") are monitoring headquarters where multiple surveillance feeds converge.

### Scope and Distribution

EFF's [Atlas of Surveillance project](https://atlasofsurveillance.org/real-time-crime-centers) has identified more than 80 RTCCs across the United States, with concentrations in the South and Northeast.

### Notable Examples

**New Orleans**
- More than 300 street-facing surveillance cameras feed into a $5 million monitoring center (created 2017)
- 24/7 monitoring
- Integrated with private cameras and automated license plate readers
- Continued expansion of camera installations

**Birmingham, Alabama**
- Nearly 100 surveillance cameras on 64 power poles:
  - 24 PTZ cameras
  - 17 dome cameras
  - 54 automated license plate readers
- Monitored by Jefferson County Metro Area Crime Center
- ALPR data uploaded to FBI's Criminal Justice Information Services (CJIS) database

### Funding Sources

RTCC costs range from a few hundred thousand dollars to $11 million (New York City). Funding comes from:

- City budgets
- Voter-approved bonds
- State and federal grants
- Private institutions
- Individual donors

**Example**: Atlanta's Loudermilk Operation Shield Video Integration Center started with a $1 million donation from real-estate developers and $350,000 from the city.

Some RTCCs have proven unsustainable. The Fresno Police Department let go of part-time RTCC staff in 2019 and ceased real-time operations in 2020, though officers can still access stored footage.

## Emerging Privacy Threats

### Security Vulnerabilities

Internet-connected camera networks are vulnerable to attacks:

- **Washington D.C. (2017)**: Hackers attacked two-thirds of police surveillance cameras days before the presidential inauguration in a ransomware attack, causing over 100 cameras to go dark
- **ALPR Exposures (2015)**: EFF discovered more than 100 ALPR cameras exposed online with open web pages, allowing anyone to view live video streams and plate captures in real time

### Face Recognition Integration

When combined with [face recognition technology](https://www.eff.org/pages/face-recognition), surveillance camera networks raise serious additional concerns:

- Face recognition data is prone to error
- Creates significant civil rights implications
- Enables tracking individuals across camera networks

### Advanced Video Analytics

Beyond face recognition, sophisticated analytical software expands surveillance capabilities:

**BriefCam** and similar platforms can:
- Track individuals based on clothing, glasses, or facial hair
- Analyze video footage in real time
- Integrate with real-time crime centers

### Private Camera Integration

One of the most significant emerging trends is law enforcement's ability to connect to private security cameras on homes and businesses.

**Fusus Platform**

Georgia-based company Fusus sells:
- Cloud-based platform for creating real-time crime centers
- Streamlined interface for multiple surveillance streams (predictive policing, gunshot detection, license plate readers, drones)
- Hardware that converts privately-owned cameras into parts of the police surveillance network

Cities including Atlanta, Memphis, and Orlando have encouraged residents and businesses to integrate their cameras into Fusus-fueled surveillance systems.

## Key Takeaways

The proliferation of surveillance cameras has rapidly reduced anonymity in public spaces. Key concerns include:

1. **Pervasive coverage**: Multiple camera types create overlapping surveillance zones
2. **Data aggregation**: Real-time crime centers combine footage from public and private sources
3. **Security risks**: Internet-connected cameras are vulnerable to breaches
4. **Expanding capabilities**: Face recognition and video analytics enable tracking and identification
5. **Private integration**: Police increasingly access private camera networks, blurring the line between public and private surveillance

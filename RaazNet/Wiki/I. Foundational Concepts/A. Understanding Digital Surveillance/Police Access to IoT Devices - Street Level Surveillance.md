# Police Access to IoT Devices

Long gone are the days when our computers were the only electronic devices in the house connected to the rest of the world via the internet. Now, everything from refrigerators to robot vacuum cleaners can be accessed via apps from anywhere in the world. This is often called "the internet of things" (IoT).

## The Privacy Problem

Many IoT devices store personal data not locally on the device itself, but on remote company servers where it can be accessed by various entities, including law enforcement. Most concerning are networked devices that collect and store video and audio from in and around our homes, some in real-time.

Other IoT data might also interest police:
- **Networked thermostats** - May show whether you were home at a relevant time
- **Smart refrigerators** - Can indicate activity patterns
- **Fitness trackers** - Record movement and location data
- **Robot vacuums** - Create maps of your home

It's troubling enough that police seek IoT data from companies. Even worse, some companies have built police involvement directly into their infrastructure, creating specialized request forms that allow law enforcement to obtain customer data in "emergency" circumstances—without a warrant or customer permission.

As of January 2021, **18% of U.S. homes** have a surveillance doorbell, making this one of the largest networked surveillance systems in the country.

## How IoT Devices Work

Consumer IoT devices connect to the internet in various ways:
- **Interconnected systems** - Devices like Echo or Alexa can control lights, televisions, and other smart home devices
- **App-controlled** - Many devices are managed through smartphone applications
- **Voice-activated** - Some respond to voice commands, meaning the microphone is always at least somewhat active, waiting for the command word

### Video Doorbell Example

IoT security devices like Amazon Ring doorbells:
1. Are triggered by motion
2. Activate both camera and microphone to record the incident
3. Upload data to remote company servers
4. Allow real-time access through the app to view, listen, or speak through the device

## How Law Enforcement Accesses IoT Data

As of 2019, **69% of U.S. homes** had at least one IoT device—meaning a tremendous amount of data is potentially available to police. Law enforcement has sought data from Fitbit fitness trackers and Google Nest thermostats, demonstrating that wherever data is collected on individuals, police will eventually come looking for it.

### Methods Police Use to Obtain IoT Data

| Method | Description | Your Rights |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Direct Request** | Police knock on your door or email you requesting footage | You can always say "no" without a warrant |
| **Mass Email Requests** | Police have companies send bulk requests to many users at once | You can decline these requests |
| **Warrant to Owner** | Police present a warrant for data stored on your device | Must comply with valid warrant |
| **Warrant to Company** | Police present a warrant to the company for server-stored data | Company may not notify you |
| **Emergency Requests** | Some companies allow police to bypass warrants in "emergencies" | Varies by company policy |

**Important**: If there is no warrant, you always have the right to say "no" to police requests for your IoT data.

## Industry Concerns

A challenge in managing your IoT privacy is the frequency with which these companies are bought and sold. When one company acquires another, your data may transfer to an entity with different privacy practices.

**Example**: The attempted sale of iRobot (makers of Roomba) to Amazon raised concerns that Amazon would inherit detailed maps of people's homes—potentially useful for their own home robot devices and surveillance-based indoor drones.

## Threats to Privacy

Police access to IoT data stored on company servers represents a significant expansion of government surveillance. Without rigorous enforcement of warrant requirements, unencrypted data collected inside your home could be stored in what amounts to one of the largest police evidence lockers in history—the servers of IoT companies.

This ubiquity could create a world where people feel uncomfortable having sensitive conversations in large portions of their house, yard, and neighborhood, fearing their words could be recorded and stored on company servers, ready for police access.

## Protecting Yourself

### What EFF Has Accomplished

- **End-to-end encryption**: Pushed Ring to introduce encryption for footage in early 2021, increasing user control over police access
- **Mass email policy change**: Pressured Ring to stop allowing police to send mass emails requesting footage (changed June 2021)
- **Third-party tracker exposure**: Discovered and publicized that the Amazon Ring app contained undisclosed third-party trackers

### Steps You Can Take

1. **Know your rights** - You can refuse warrantless requests for your data
2. **Enable encryption** - Use end-to-end encryption when available
3. **Review privacy settings** - Regularly check your device and app settings
4. **Research before buying** - Consider a company's law enforcement policies before purchasing IoT devices
5. **Limit data collection** - Disable features you don't need

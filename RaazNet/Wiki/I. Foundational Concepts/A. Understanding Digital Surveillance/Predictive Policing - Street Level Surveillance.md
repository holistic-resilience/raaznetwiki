# Predictive Policing

For well over a century, police have attempted to find ways to predict where, when, and by whom crime is going to be committed. In the 21st century, this ambition often takes the form of predictive algorithms that use historical data in an attempt to anticipate future events. Police often plug in events as disparate as traffic information, crime statistics and incident reports, or even the grades of school children. The algorithmic outputs often guide police procedure, patrols, and even who to put under increased surveillance.

But police cannot actually predict crime, with or without a computer. Nobody can see the future. What police can do, however, is make assumptions about who and where crime is most likely to occur and put those places and people under intense scrutiny. Such heightened surveillance can lead to increased traffic and sidewalk stops, documentation of a person's activities over time, and even use of force.

## The Self-Fulfilling Prophecy Problem

Predictive policing is often a self-fulfilling prophecy. If police focus their efforts in one neighborhood and arrest dozens of people there during the span of a week, the data may reflect that area as a hotbed of criminal activity, leading the algorithm to deploy more officers there the following week.

The system also considers only reported crime, so neighborhoods where the police are called more often might see predictive policing technology concentrate resources there. This system can aggravate existing patterns of policing—especially in neighborhoods with concentrations of people of color, unhoused individuals, and immigrants—by using the cloak of scientific legitimacy and the supposed unbiased nature of data.

## How It Works

Predictive policing programs generally work by feeding crime data, and sometimes massive amounts of other data, into an algorithm which tries to look for unseen patterns revealing where and when crimes are most likely to take place. That information can include:

- Locations of all crimes recorded by police
- Time at which crimes were committed
- Weather data
- Traffic information
- Other demographic and behavioral data

The predictive model can shape deployment of patrols.

### Individual-Focused Programs

In some cases, programs like these can focus on individuals. In Pasco County, Florida, police assigned a point system to individuals based on factors like a family's income bracket or grades in order to assign a level of likelihood a child is to commit crimes—and then harass them and their family.

## How Law Enforcement Uses It

Law enforcement often claims that predictive policing is a "force multiplier," something that allows departments to allocate officers and resources more strategically. This means that police feed data into software which identifies patterns which police hope will dictate where they should deploy officers in order to prevent criminal activity.

This can take the form of:
- **Heat maps** that display city blocks supposedly having higher-than-normal crime levels
- **"Cops on the dots"** deployment of officers to flagged blocks

## Major Vendors

### Geolitica (formerly PredPol)

PredPol, now named Geolitica, was an early predictive policing vendor. It began a pilot program in the Santa Cruz Police Department in 2011. By 2020, some police departments began canceling their contracts amid public concerns over civil liberties:

- **Los Angeles** ended its subscription
- **Santa Cruz** banned the use of predictive policing algorithms altogether
- **New Orleans** banned predictive policing (though has since rolled back these protections)

### Palantir

In 2018, it was reported that Palantir, a company that often tries to help agencies organize and visualize large sets of data, was testing a predictive policing system in New Orleans without the knowledge of elected officials.

### Banjo

Banjo briefly held a multi-million dollar contract with the state of Utah but was eventually found by a government audit to be incapable of providing the services it had offered. The company was given access to state databases containing sensitive information.

### Other Vendors

- **NYPD/Microsoft Domain Awareness Systems** - Custom-built predictive policing tool
- **ShotSpotter** - Acoustic gunshot detection company that acquired HunchLab in 2018 to predict where gunshots might happen in the future

## Civil Liberties Threats

For at least a century, police departments have been using statistics and mapping in an attempt to understand where and when crime might occur, but digital technology poses new civil liberties threats.

### Constitutional Concerns

No one should have their constitutional rights abated because they live in or walk through areas where an algorithm has predicted there will be crime.

### Data Quality Problems

There are major concerns about the type of data being fed into these programs:

- More and more types of sensitive personal data about individuals are being fed into these programs
- Skewed data generated by police departments creates bias
- If police put majority Black neighborhoods under intense police scrutiny and surveillance, they will be more likely to make stops and arrests for minor quality of life infractions
- Derivative maps purporting to show where future crimes might be committed will disproportionately weigh neighborhoods already living under intense police presence

This creates a self-fulfilling prophecy that uses the supposed objectivity of math to legitimize racially biased police procedures.

### Facial Analysis Claims

Police agencies and vendors also claim they can train machine learning algorithms to predict whether a person is likely to commit crimes. A particularly dubious claim is that this can be done by analyzing physical attributes of a person's face.

## EFF's Position

Because of the risk predictive policing poses to civil liberties and vulnerable populations, EFF advocates for bans on predictive policing. EFF also supports lawmakers who want to take a more active role in probing predictive policing vendors and police departments, and will continue to support lawmakers at local, state, and national levels in their attempts to rein in this dangerous technology.

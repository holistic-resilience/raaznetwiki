# Protect Your Information and Devices While Traveling

Traveling exposes you, your devices, and your data to significant risks. Laptops and other devices can fail, get stolen, lost, damaged, or impounded. Data transmitted from public access points—such as airport and hotel WiFi networks—can be intercepted. Using untrusted systems may expose you to keyloggers and other attacks designed to capture sensitive information.

When crossing any national border, you have few if any rights to protest or refuse when a border control or customs officer wants to inspect your luggage, including data stored on your laptop, mobile device, digital camera, or recorder. In many countries, including the US, a customs officer needs no "probable cause" or warrant to search your device or confiscate it for further inspection.

This guide provides recommendations to prepare for your travels and minimize risks for your data and devices when traveling and crossing borders.

## Basic Security Practices

These general security practices should be implemented with special care when planning a journey.

### Account Security
- Set **strong, unique passwords** for all your accounts before traveling
- Enable **multi-factor authentication** on email, social media, and other critical accounts
- Update your operating system and all software to patch vulnerabilities
- Ensure an updated antivirus is running on your device

### Awareness and Research
- Avoid opening unrequested attachments or links in emails or messages (phishing prevention)
- **Research your destination country:**
  - Is the internet censored?
  - Are VPNs and circumvention tools legal?
  - Is encryption banned?
  - Are there laws requiring you to hand over passwords or encryption keys to border police?

### Physical Security
- Set your device to **lock automatically** when unattended
- Require a password at login and when resuming from standby
- **Watch for shoulder surfers**—be aware of who can see your screen or keyboard when entering passwords, passphrases, or PINs

## Prepare for Crossing Borders

### Minimize the Data You Carry

**Why this matters:** Data you don't carry cannot be lost or stolen. When crossing borders, ensure you're not carrying data that could be illegal in any country you're visiting (copyrighted material, certain types of imagery, etc.).

**Actions to take:**
- Determine what data you actually need while traveling
- Copy everything else to an encrypted computer or hard drive that remains at home
- Delete unnecessary data from travel devices
- Consider storing encrypted data or a system image on a secure cloud service and traveling with a minimal laptop

### Use an Alternative Email Address

**Why this matters:** Border agents may inspect your mailbox. Using untrusted connections exposes your email to attacks. A temporary travel email limits the damage if compromised.

**Actions to take:**
- Log out of all email accounts (apps, clients, and browsers)
- Create a new web-based email account specifically for your travels
- Use this address for non-sensitive communications only
- Dispose of the account after your trip

### Remove Accounts from Your Devices

**Why this matters:** Border agents may inspect social media accounts found on your devices.

**Actions to take:**
- Log out of all accounts before crossing the border
- Remove or hide social media and sensitive apps
- Consider creating secondary social media profiles populated with harmless content (food photos, casual messages) weeks or months in advance

### Clean Up Your Web Browsers

- Log out of all accounts (email, social networking, etc.)
- Clear temporary internet files and browsing history

### Clean Up Your Chat Apps

- Assess which chat apps you have installed (in some countries, having Signal may raise suspicion)
- Search for and remove sensitive content from chat history
- Rename groups to non-descriptive names if you're the admin
- Enable disappearing messages for sensitive chats
- Note: Not having any messenger app may also appear suspicious

### [Advanced] Hide Sensitive Files or Folders

**Why this matters:** If you must carry sensitive files, hiding them in encrypted folders provides protection.

**Options:**
- **Android:** Use the Safe folder in the Files app
- **iOS:** Use the Hidden album for photos
- **Tella app (Android/iOS):** Hide sensitive files; on Android, camouflage Tella as a calculator
- **Computer:** Create a hidden folder with VeraCrypt

## Use Encryption to Protect Your Data and Communications

### Check Encryption Legality

Before traveling, verify whether encryption is legal in your destination countries. If encryption is illegal:
- Encrypt your sensitive information
- Upload it to secure cloud storage
- Delete it from travel devices
- Download it after arrival

### Encrypt Your Devices

**Why this matters:** Encryption ensures that if someone accesses your device without your passphrase, they see only random data.

**Actions to take:**
- Encrypt your entire device
- Encrypt external storage devices
- Maintain good backups (encryption makes data recovery more difficult if hardware fails)

### [Advanced] Protect Your Cryptographic Keys

If you use PGP encryption, your private keys are among the most valuable data on your device.

**Recommendations:**
- Never leave devices with private keys unattended or in third-party hands
- Consider storing private keys on an encrypted USB drive that you keep on your person at all times

## Public Access

When traveling, you may frequently use public computers or networks. Each situation carries specific risks.

### Shared Computers

**Why this matters:** Shared computers should always be considered insecure. Keyloggers and spyware can capture everything you do.

**Mitigation strategies:**
- Check for hardware keyloggers (dongles between keyboard and computer)
- Bring a small USB keyboard to use instead of the shared one
- Travel with a custom **live USB** containing your trusted operating system and software—boot from it to bypass the untrusted computer entirely

### Protect Your Connections on Untrusted Networks

**Why this matters:** Untrusted networks (hotel WiFi, hotspots) may enable machine-in-the-middle (MITM) attacks that capture site addresses, email content, or redirect you to fake sites.

**Actions to take:**
- Configure your browser to connect only through HTTPS
- Install one or more VPNs before your trip
- Use protective browser extensions, especially an ad blocker like uBlock Origin
- Pay attention to security warnings when connecting—they may indicate an attack (though absence of warnings doesn't guarantee safety)

## Internet Censorship and Blockages

In some countries, web access may be limited by censorship or country-wide blockages.

**Recommendations:**
- Learn how the internet works and how it can be censored
- Learn to use circumvention tools
- If you're a human rights defender, journalist, or activist traveling to countries with sophisticated surveillance, contact:
  - [Front Line Defenders](https://www.frontlinedefenders.org/emergency-contact)
  - [Access Now Digital Security Helpline](https://www.accessnow.org/help)

## Mobile Phone Security

### Travel with a Different Phone

Mobile phones should generally be considered insecure. 

**Recommendations:**
- Leave your regular phone at home
- Travel with a different device that doesn't contain all your data
- If you must bring your work phone, back up all data elsewhere and remove it from the phone

### Use Secure Messaging Apps

While traveling, secure messaging apps offer fast ways to protect communications. If your contacts use secure messaging apps, you can encrypt messages, files, and voice calls.

### [Advanced] Hide Sensitive Apps

If you need sensitive apps while traveling, hide them using native device features:
- **Android:** Use Private Space to hide sensitive apps
- **iOS:** Lock or hide apps using built-in settings

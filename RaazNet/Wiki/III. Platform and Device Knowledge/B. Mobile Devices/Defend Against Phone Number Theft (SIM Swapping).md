# Defend Against Phone Number Theft (SIM Swapping)

## What is SIM Swapping?

Your phone number is extremely valuable to attackers—it's often linked to financial services, email accounts, and other sensitive platforms. In a SIM swap attack, criminals impersonate you to your phone carrier, convincing customer service representatives that they have a new phone and need your number transferred to their device.

Once successful, attackers can:
- Intercept two-factor authentication codes sent via SMS
- Break into your online accounts (banking, email, social media)
- Commit financial fraud or steal cryptocurrency

## AT&T Wireless Account Lock

AT&T has implemented a feature called **Wireless Account Lock** that allows users to disable several account changes, significantly reducing the risk of phone number hijacking.

## What You Can Do

### 1. Enable Carrier-Level Protection

Search to learn if your phone carrier supports SIM swap protection features:

| Carrier | Protection Feature | How to Enable |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| **AT&T** | Wireless Account Lock | [AT&T Support Instructions](https://www.att.com/support/article/wireless/000102016/) |
| **T-Mobile** | SIM Swap Protection | [T-Mobile Account Fraud Help](https://www.t-mobile.com/support/plans-features/help-with-t-mobile-account-fraud) |
| **Verizon** | SIM Swap Protection | [Verizon Account Security](https://www.verizon.com/about/account-security/sim-swapping) |

> **Note:** If your carrier doesn't offer this feature, contact customer service to manually add a PIN to your account. Be aware this isn't bulletproof—representatives can still override your PIN in some circumstances.

### 2. Use Stronger Two-Factor Authentication

If your phone number can be hijacked, so can SMS-based 2FA codes. Consider upgrading to more secure authentication methods:

- **Authenticator apps** (Google Authenticator, Authy)
- **Hardware security keys** (YubiKey, Titan)
- **Passkeys** (device-based authentication)

For detailed guidance on setting up stronger 2FA, see Freedom of the Press Foundation's [2FA Beginner's Guide](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/2fa-beginners/).

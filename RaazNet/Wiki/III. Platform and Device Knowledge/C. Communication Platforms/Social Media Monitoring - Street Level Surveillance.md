# Social Media Monitoring

## How It Works

Social media contains some of the most intimate details of our lives, including health information, likes and dislikes, political views, religious beliefs, sexual orientation, and our social connections. The data collected includes:

- Usernames, bios, and contact information
- Status updates, comments, photos, videos, and live streams
- Event postings and group memberships
- Friend/follower lists and friend requests
- Private messages
- Account creation dates and location data

### Government Surveillance Programs

Law enforcement agencies at all levels—federal, state, and local—monitor social media accounts. Key examples include:

**Immigration-Related Monitoring**
- The Department of Homeland Security (DHS) and U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) monitor social media posts of asylum seekers, tourists seeking visa waivers, and immigration benefit applicants
- This surveillance extends to anyone in these people's networks
- Social media information is used as evidence in deportations and visa denials
- DHS has offered no transparency about its multi-million dollar monitoring program

**Domestic Law Enforcement**
- LAPD officers were ordered to record social media information (Facebook, Instagram, Twitter) of every civilian they questioned, including those not arrested or accused of any crime ([internal July 2020 memo](https://www.brennancenter.org/sites/default/files/2021-09/I.%20Beck%20FI%20Memo.pdf))
- Police may combine social media monitoring with face recognition to identify individuals

**Misinterpretation Risks**
Since social media posts are highly contextual, police may misinterpret innocuous language, song lyrics, or inside jokes, leading to criminal consequences for individuals who may not have been aware they were under surveillance.

## How Law Enforcement Monitors Social Media

### Manual Surveillance Methods

Police may use fake accounts to:
- Search publicly available posts for names, groups, or individuals
- Monitor specific keywords and hashtags
- Identify names and affiliations of people posting in groups
- Gather information about planned events and protests

### Legal Data Requests

Police may use subpoenas or warrants to request social media data as part of criminal investigations when they need information that's not publicly available.

### Fake and Imposter Accounts

Law enforcement has created deceptive accounts in several documented cases:

- **DEA Agent Impersonation (2010)**: A DEA agent [created a phony Facebook account](https://www.buzzfeednews.com/article/chrishamby/government-says-federal-agents-can-impersonate-woman-online) using a young woman's identity without consent, posting her seized photos including pictures of her children
- **University of Farmington**: DHS created fake profiles to trick immigrants into registering with a [fake college](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2020/dec/05/ice-fake-university-farmington-inside-story), leading to more than 170 arrests
- **Memphis Police Department**: Created [fake profiles to monitor Black Lives Matter activists](https://theappeal.org/memphis-police-collected-black-lives-matter-activists-private-facebook-posts/)

### Automated Monitoring Tools

Police use social media monitoring tools that collect and analyze large amounts of data, allowing searches by hashtag or keyword and generating profiles and predictions.

**Example - USPIS iCOP Program**: The United States Postal Inspection Service [conducted blanket keyword searches](https://www.vice.com/en/article/n7nmaz/post-office-cops-used-social-media-surveillance-program-illegally) (including "protest," "destroy," and "attack") across social media. The Office of Inspector General later determined USPIS lacked legal authority for these searches.

- Some firms are no longer used by law enforcement, often due to loss of data access
- Geofeedia lost access to Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter APIs after ACLU of Northern California revealed it was marketed for surveilling protesters

### Combined Data Mining

Some tools integrate social media with other data sources:

- **Palantir + New Orleans PD**: Combined social media history with other data for "predictive policing"
- **Clearview AI + Minneapolis PD**: Scraped billions of photos from social media for facial recognition
- **CBP Dossiers**: Customs and Border Patrol [inappropriately surveilled](https://www.nbcsandiego.com/news/investigations/report-cbp-inappropriately-flagged-u-s-citizens-during-migrant-caravan/2726925/) journalists, attorneys, and advocates at the U.S.-Mexico border

## Threats Posed by Social Media Monitoring

### Chilling Effects on Free Speech

Social media monitoring can suppress protected speech and association due to valid fears of:
- Being screened for political opinions
- Increased interrogation or denied entry to the U.S.
- Exposing others to surveillance
- Inclusion in police databases
- Facing criminal charges

### Documented Targeting of Protesters

Social media monitoring has been used against nonviolent protesters engaging in protected speech:

**Federal Agencies**
- ICE monitored anti-Trump protesters in New York City
- DHS collected data on Black Lives Matter activities, including events expected to be peaceful
- LookingGlass Cyber Solutions gathered information on 600+ protests against forced family separation, sharing with DHS and fusion centers

**Local Agencies**
- LAPD monitored protesters and journalists during George Floyd protests, searching for keywords including "black lives matter," "BLM," "demonstration," "protest," #BLMLA, #SayHerName, and names of people killed by LA police
- A Texas fusion center surveilled Black cultural events including an online Juneteenth celebration, sharing organizer names and social media information with federal, state, and local agencies
- DHS planned to monitor a community parade in Congress Heights, a funk parade in U Street, and a breast cancer walk

### Systemic Problems

**Minimal Oversight**: Despite allowing law enforcement to monitor thousands of accounts with a single button, social media monitoring has extremely limited oversight and transparency.

**Misinterpretation**: Highly contextual posts can be misunderstood, especially for non-English languages and unfamiliar cultural contexts. Police and prosecutors have used Facebook photos, "likes," and associations to make false allegations of gang activity.

**Identity Verification**: It's not always possible to correctly link individuals with their accounts.

**Data Quality and Retention**: Partnerships with data mining companies risk adding large amounts of unverified information to databases. This data is shared across organizations with different retention policies, increasing opportunities for misuse.

**Questionable Effectiveness**: It's unclear whether social media monitoring successfully identifies and stops actual threats to national security.

## EFF's Work on Social Media Monitoring

### Legal Actions

**EFF v. DHS (VLVI)**: Sued DHS under FOIA to obtain records on its Visa Lifecycle Vetting Initiative social media surveillance program. EFF is seeking:
- Current program status and purposes
- Impact on visa approvals and denials
- Details about a $4.8 million transaction
- Contracts, operational notes, training materials, and audit reports

**EFF v. USPS**: [Sued the U.S. Postal Service](https://www.eff.org/press/releases/eff-sues-us-postal-service-records-about-covert-social-media-spying-program) seeking records about covert social media monitoring before street protests.

**2009 Social Networking Lawsuit**: With the Samuelson Law Technology and Public Policy Clinic at UC Berkeley, filed suit against government agencies for refusing to disclose their social media surveillance policies.

### Policy Advocacy

EFF opposes government monitoring of social media accounts because:
- It invades privacy
- It violates the First Amendment by chilling speech
- It allows targeting and punishment of people for expressing disfavored views
- Vetting based on social media is ineffective and discriminatory

### Platform Accountability

EFF has called on Meta to:
- Publish numbers of fake law enforcement accounts in transparency reports
- Alert users and groups that interacted with fake accounts
- Make terms barring such accounts more explicit
- Notify departments with policies violating platform rules
